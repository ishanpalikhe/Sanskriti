The Name & The Nation

Origin of Nepal

Nestled between the vast plains of India and the Tibetan plateau, Nepal stands as one of the oldest living civilisations on earth — a land whose name, people, and sacred geography defy simple explanation.

Kathmandu Valley — Cradle of Civilisation

Etymology

What Does
"Nepal" Mean?

The origin of the word Nepal remains one of history's quietly contested puzzles. The most widely accepted tradition traces it to the sage Ne Muni, a Hindu ascetic said to have governed and spiritually protected the Kathmandu Valley in antiquity. The land under his care became known as Nepal — "the place governed by Ne."

A competing etymology links the name to the Newari language — the tongue of the valley's indigenous inhabitants — where Nepa is believed to mean "a place in the middle," aptly describing a nation poised between two great civilisations.

Tibeto-Burman linguistic scholars propose a third root: niyampal, meaning a mountain settlement, pointing to the very geography that has defined Nepali identity for millennia.

Chobar Gorge — Where Legend Drained a Lake

Sacred Legend

The Myth of
Nagdaha

Ancient Hindu and Buddhist texts speak with one voice on the origin of the Kathmandu Valley: it was once a vast, shimmering lake called Nagdaha — home to the divine serpents, the Nagas, and presided over by the lotus-dwelling deity Vipashvi Buddha.

The bodhisattva Manjushri, in his quest to make the sacred land accessible to humanity, raised his flaming sword of wisdom and struck the southern hills at Chobar. The mountains parted, the waters rushed out, and the fertile valley floor was revealed — ready for civilisation to take root.

Even today, the gorge at Chobar is revered as the site of this cosmic act. The Bagmati River flows through it still, carrying the memory of a lake that once held gods.

From the Terai to the Summit of the World

Land & People

A Nation of
Extraordinary Diversity

Nepal spans barely 147,181 square kilometres — yet within that compact territory lies one of the most astonishing ecological ranges on earth. From the tropical lowlands of the Terai, where tigers still roam, to the glacial heights of the Himalayas — home to eight of the world's ten tallest peaks — Nepal compresses entire climatic worlds into a single journey northward.

This geography shaped an equally diverse people. Over 125 distinct ethnic groups — Newars, Sherpas, Gurungs, Rais, Limbus, Tharus, Magars, Tamangs, and many more — each carry their own languages, traditions, and spiritual lives, forming a tapestry unlike anywhere else on earth.

Sagarmatha — Mount Everest, known in Tibetan as Chomolungma, Mother Goddess of the World — rises at 8,849 metres as both literal and symbolic peak of the nation.

Through the Ages

A Living
History

The Durbar Squares — Monuments of Dynasty

c. 7000 BCE — Neolithic Era

Earliest Settlements

Archaeological evidence from the Kathmandu Valley points to Neolithic habitation — early communities living by the lake's fertile shores, long before the myths took written form.

c. 563 BCE

Birth of the Buddha

Siddhartha Gautama — who would become the Buddha, the Awakened One — was born at Lumbini in the Terai plains of southern Nepal. The garden of his birth is today a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a place of pilgrimage for millions.

c. 400 BCE – 750 CE

The Licchavi Dynasty

The Licchavis established one of Nepal's first great recorded kingdoms, governing from the Kathmandu Valley. Under their reign, Hinduism and Buddhism intertwined — a coexistence that would become the hallmark of Nepali spiritual life. Trade flourished along the routes between India and Tibet.

750 – 1200 CE

The Thakuri Period

The Thakuri rulers maintained the kingdom through a period of transition. Cultural and artistic traditions deepened, and the valley's position as a crossroads of Asian civilisation grew ever more significant.

1200 – 1769 CE

The Malla Kingdoms

The Malla era is Nepal's golden age of art and architecture. The valley split into three rival city-kingdoms — Kathmandu, Patan, and Bhaktapur — each competing to build ever more magnificent Durbar Squares, pagoda temples, and royal palaces. Their rivalry produced some of Asia's finest medieval architecture.

1768 – 1769 CE

Unification by Prithvi Narayan Shah

King Prithvi Narayan Shah of the Gorkha kingdom conquered the three Malla city-states and unified the many small principalities of the hills into a single Kingdom of Nepal — laying the foundation for the modern nation and beginning the Shah dynasty that would rule for 240 years.

The Kshetri community is one of Nepal’s largest and historically significant ethnic groups, traditionally known as warriors and rulers. They belong to the Kshatriya varna in the Hindu social hierarchy. Kshetris have played key roles in the military, administration, and governance throughout Nepalese history. They predominantly speak Nepali and live in hilly regions. Their culture values bravery, discipline, and loyalty. Festivals, rituals, and religious practices are observed with devotion. Today, Kshetris continue to contribute to Nepal’s social, political, and cultural life, balancing modern professions while preserving traditional customs, martial heritage, and family values that define their identity.

Hill Brahmins are one of the oldest and most respected ethnic groups in Nepal. Traditionally priests, scholars, and teachers, they are responsible for performing religious rituals and preserving sacred knowledge. Following Hinduism strictly, they celebrate festivals and observe cultural practices with devotion. They mainly reside in hilly regions and speak Nepali. Education, spirituality, and ethical conduct are central to their culture. Historically, Hill Brahmins held positions in administration and governance. Today, they continue to serve society through religious guidance, education, and cultural preservation, maintaining their traditions while also participating in modern professions and community leadership.

Magars are an indigenous ethnic group primarily residing in mid-hill regions of Nepal. They have a rich cultural heritage with distinctive music, dance, festivals, and traditional clothing. Many Magars historically served in the Gurkha regiments, earning recognition for bravery and military skill. They practice Hinduism, Buddhism, or indigenous beliefs and speak both Magar language and Nepali. Magar society values community cohesion, courage, and respect for traditions. Agriculture and craftsmanship are common occupations. Today, Magars contribute to Nepal’s cultural, military, and economic life while preserving their language, customs, and traditional festivals, ensuring their heritage continues across generations.

The Thakuri people — known in various contexts as Thakuri, Thakuris, or in some historical texts as Thakuri Rajput — are a primarily Indo-Aryan ethnic community found throughout Nepal, with the highest concentration in the western hills of Nepal (districts of Doti, Bajhang, Bajura, Achham, and surrounding areas). According to Nepal's national census, the Thakuri population numbers approximately 80,000–100,000 individuals. The Thakuri occupy an ambiguous position in Nepali social classification: claiming Kshatriya (warrior) status and connection to broader Indo-Aryan traditions, while also maintaining a distinct ethnic and regional identity rooted in the western Himalayan hills.

Tamangs are an indigenous ethnic group mainly residing in Nepal’s hilly regions. They have a rich cultural heritage, including unique language, traditional music, dances, and Buddhist customs. They speak the Tamang language alongside Nepali. Festivals like Lhosar are celebrated with enthusiasm, reflecting their spiritual and community-oriented lifestyle. Traditionally, Tamangs were farmers, craftsmen, and traders. Their society values family bonds, respect for elders, and community cohesion. Today, Tamangs actively contribute to Nepal’s cultural diversity, economy, and tourism while preserving their language, traditional arts, and religious practices. They maintain a balance between embracing modernity and protecting their heritage for future generations.

The Bishwokarma community, also known as Kami in some regions, is traditionally skilled in metalwork, blacksmithing, and crafting agricultural tools. They are an indigenous occupational caste within Nepal’s Hindu society, often associated with craftsmanship and technical knowledge. Bishwokarmas primarily speak Nepali and reside across hilly and Terai regions. Their culture emphasizes skill, discipline, and service to the community. They observe Hindu rituals and festivals with devotion. Historically, they were vital for producing tools, weapons, and household implements. Today, Bishwokarmas continue to combine traditional skills with modern occupations, contributing to Nepal’s economy and cultural heritage while preserving their artisanal identity.

The Musalman community in Nepal consists of followers of Islam, forming a minority religious and ethnic group. They live mainly in Terai regions and speak languages such as Urdu, Nepali, and Maithili. Musalmans maintain distinct cultural and religious practices, including Islamic festivals like Eid and Ramadan, along with traditional attire and dietary customs. They emphasize family values, education, and social cohesion. Historically, they contributed to trade, business, and local administration. Today, Musalmans continue to play a vital role in Nepalese society, preserving their religious and cultural identity while participating in the country’s economic, educational, and social development.

The Newar community, also called Newa, is an indigenous ethnic group primarily residing in the Kathmandu Valley. Renowned for their rich cultural heritage, they are known for unique festivals, traditional arts, architecture, and cuisine. Newars follow Hinduism and Buddhism, celebrating festivals like Indra Jatra and Bisket Jatra with vibrant rituals. They speak Nepal Bhasa along with Nepali. Historically, Newars were traders, artisans, and administrators, contributing significantly to Nepal’s economy and culture. Today, they continue to preserve their language, art, and traditions while engaging in modern professions, tourism, and business, maintaining their cultural identity across generations.

The Yadav community is traditionally associated with cattle herding, agriculture, and dairy farming in Nepal, particularly in Terai regions. They follow Hinduism and celebrate festivals like Holi, Dashain, and Chhath with devotion. Yadavs speak Maithili, Bhojpuri, and Nepali depending on the region. Their society emphasizes family values, community cooperation, and respect for traditions. Historically, Yadavs contributed to local agriculture and trade, playing key roles in rural economies. Today, they continue to engage in farming, business, and education while preserving traditional customs, religious practices, and social cohesion, contributing to the cultural and economic diversity of Nepal.

The Rai community is an indigenous ethnic group primarily residing in eastern Nepal’s hilly regions. They have a rich culture with traditional dances, music, and festivals, and many speak the Rai languages along with Nepali. Historically, the Rais were known for their bravery and service in the military. Agriculture is a major occupation, along with animal husbandry. Rai society emphasizes community life, family bonds, and respect for elders. They celebrate festivals such as Sakela, which reflects their spiritual beliefs and cultural values. Today, Rais contribute to Nepal’s socio-cultural diversity while maintaining their heritage and practicing traditional customs alongside modern professions.

The Tharu community is among the oldest continuously inhabiting indigenous communities of the South Asian subcontinent — a people whose presence in the Terai lowlands of southern Nepal and northern India predates the arrival of virtually every other population group in the region by centuries, and whose remarkable physiological and cultural adaptations to the malarial jungles of the Terai made them the sole masters of a landscape that proved fatal to outsiders for most of recorded history. The Terai — Nepal's narrow strip of subtropical lowland running east to west along the Indian border, covered by dense sal forest, crossed by swift-flowing rivers descending from the Himalayas, rich in wildlife, and for most of history defined by a virulent strain of malaria that killed non-adapted peoples within weeks of arrival — was the Tharu homeland, and the Tharu people shaped their entire civilization around its rhythms, its dangers, its bounties, and its extraordinary ecological complexity.

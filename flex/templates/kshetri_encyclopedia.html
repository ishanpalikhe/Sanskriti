History and Origin

The Name, The Varna, and the Vedic Root

Map of Nepal showing Kshetri population distribution across all ecological zones

The Khetris — formally written as Kshetri, Chhetri, or Chhatri in various Romanization systems — are the single largest caste group in Nepal, constituting approximately 16.6% of the total population according to the 2011 National Census, which recorded 4,398,053 Kshetris out of a national population of roughly 26.5 million. In the 2021 census, their numbers are estimated to have grown to over five million, making them an undeniable demographic cornerstone of the Nepali nation. They are found in every single district of Nepal, in every ecological zone from the hot Terai plains to the cold high-altitude valleys approaching the Tibetan border, and in every profession from subsistence farming to national political leadership.

Diagram of the Varna system

The word Kshetri is a Nepali and Khas-language adaptation of the Sanskrit Kshatriya (क्षत्रिय), which itself derives from the root kshatra, meaning dominion, power, or rule. In the ancient Hindu Varna system — the four-fold social classification described in the Rigveda and elaborated in texts like the Manusmriti — the Kshatriya occupied the second tier: beneath the Brahmin (priests and scholars) but above the Vaishya (merchants) and Shudra (service workers). The Kshatriya's sacred duty, their dharma, was to fight, to govern, to protect the weak, to uphold the cosmic moral order (Rita), and to sacrifice their bodies in battle if necessary. The god-king Rama, the warrior-philosopher Arjuna, and the legendary emperor Ashoka before his conversion to Buddhism — all are Kshatriyas. To belong to this varna was to belong to a lineage of warriors and rulers stretching back to the dawn of civilization as the ancient Hindu world understood it.

The Nepali adaptation of the word — Kshetri or Chhetri — is phonologically natural: the Sanskrit retroflex consonant cluster kṣ simplifies in Nepali speech, and the terminal -a of Sanskrit words is routinely dropped. The variation Chhatri is found particularly in documents and communities with closer connections to the Parbatiya (hill) dialect traditions. All of these forms refer to the same community.

The Khas People: The Indigenous Foundation

Before the arrival of Rajput warrior clans from India, the hills of western and central Nepal were home to the Khas people (खस) — an Indo-Aryan-speaking population whose origins and antiquity in the region are the subject of ongoing historical and genetic research. The Khas are attested in ancient Indian texts: the Mahabharata mentions the Khasas as a people living in the northern mountains, and the Arthashastra of Kautilya (c. 300 BCE) references them as hill-dwelling warriors. This places Khas habitation in the Nepal hills at a minimum of 2,300 years ago, and possibly much longer.

The Khas people developed their own distinct hill culture, their own proto-Nepali language (Khas Kura), their own agricultural practices adapted to the steep terraced hillsides, and their own pantheon of local deities and ritual practices. They were, in many respects, already practicing a form of Hinduism — or a pre-Hindu animist religion that was later assimilated into Brahminic Hinduism — before the waves of migration from India brought the more systematized Vedic tradition to the hills.

The Khas society was organized around clan lineages called gotra, a Sanskrit term for patrilineal descent groups, suggesting that even the earliest Khas communities in the hills had absorbed elements of the Brahminic social system. When immigrant Rajput and Brahmin families arrived in the hills — fleeing Islamic rule, seeking new kingdoms to carve out, or accompanying earlier migrants as priests — they found in the Khas communities a population already partially prepared for integration into the Hindu varna framework. The Khas warriors were recognized as Kshatriyas; the Khas priests were recognized as or merged with Brahmins. Over generations, the distinction between "original Khas" and "immigrant Rajput" dissolved, and the composite community that emerged became the Kshetri caste as we know it today.

The Rajput Migrations: Warriors from the Plains

Between the 10th and 14th centuries CE, the Indian subcontinent experienced successive waves of political upheaval as Turkic, Afghan, and later Mughal military forces swept across the northern plains. Hindu kingdoms that had flourished for centuries in Rajasthan, Gujarat, Punjab, and the Gangetic plain were conquered, their ruling families displaced or forced to submit. The Rajput clans — those extraordinarily martial families who traced their descent from the Sun (Suryavanshi), Moon (Chandravanshi), or Sacred Fire (Agnivanshi) — were particularly affected. Many chose flight over submission.

The Nepal hills, separated from the plains by the dense Terai forests (a natural barrier that also discouraged military pursuit) and further protected by the Himalayan geography, offered refuge. Wave after wave of Rajput warrior families — Sisodia, Chauhan, Parihar, Rathod, Solanki, and many other clans — moved northward into the hills. They brought with them their genealogical records (the vamsavali or family tree documents), their clan deities (kuldevi), their martial traditions, their Sanskrit literacy, and their fierce pride in Kshatriya identity.

In the hills, these immigrant Rajput families encountered the Khas communities. The inevitable process of intermarriage, cultural exchange, and political alliance produced a new social reality. The resulting population was neither purely Rajput Indian nor purely indigenous Khas — it was something distinctly Nepali. The Shah dynasty itself — the royal family that unified Nepal — exemplifies this synthesis: the Shahs claimed descent from the Sisodia Rajputs of Mewar (the same clan as the legendary Maharana Pratap), yet they ruled a mountain kingdom whose culture, language, and social structure were fundamentally Khas-Kshetri.

The Khas Malla Kingdom: The First Kshetri Empire

Sinja Valley ruins

The Khas Malla Kingdom (c. 1100–1350 CE), also known simply as the Khas Kingdom or the Kingdom of Sinja, was the political apex of the pre-unification Khas-Kshetri world and one of the most powerful states in the Himalayan region during its era. Centered on the Sinja Valley in what is today the Jumla district of Karnali Province, this kingdom at its greatest extent controlled territories stretching from the Karnali River system westward through modern Doti and Darchula, eastward through parts of Dolpa and Mustang, southward into the Terai foothills, and northward into what is today western Tibet.

The Khas Malla kings adopted Sanskrit titles and Hindu royal ceremonies, but their culture was fundamentally the hill-Khas culture. Their language — attested in stone inscriptions found in Sinja and surrounding areas — is the earliest written form of what became the Nepali language. The Sinja inscriptions, dating to the 12th–13th centuries, are therefore among the most precious linguistic monuments in Nepal's history.

The kingdom's power rested on the Kshetri warrior class who served as its military, its administrators, and its landlords. The vast agricultural terraces carved into the steep hillsides of Karnali — a feat of labor organization requiring both technical skill and coercive political power — were the economic foundation of the state. The Khas Malla Kingdom fragmented in the 14th century, splitting into smaller principalities (the Baise Rajya — 22 kingdoms — and the Chaubise Rajya — 24 kingdoms) that continued to be ruled by Kshetri and Rajput-Kshetri lineages until the unification of Nepal by Prithvi Narayan Shah in the 18th century. These small kingdoms were the nurseries of Kshetri military culture, each maintaining its own army of Kshetri warriors and its own court culture.

The Gorkha Unification: The Kshetri Moment in History

Painting of Prithvi Narayan Shah

The unification of Nepal by Prithvi Narayan Shah (1723–1775) was, at its military core, a Kshetri achievement. Prithvi Narayan Shah was himself of Rajput-Kshetri lineage — his Shah clan claimed descent from the Sisodia Rajputs — and the army he built to conquer the Kathmandu Valley and surrounding kingdoms was composed overwhelmingly of Kshetri and Magar soldiers, with a command structure heavily weighted toward Kshetri officers.

The military genius of the Gorkhali campaigns lay not just in tactics but in the extraordinary qualities of the hill soldiers — their physical endurance at altitude, their ability to move rapidly through difficult mountain terrain, their ferocity in close combat, and their cohesion as a warrior community with a shared sense of martial identity and dharmic duty. These qualities were cultivated over centuries of Kshetri military culture. When the Gorkhali army stormed the Kathmandu Valley in 1768, they were demonstrating capabilities honed across generations of hill warfare in the small kingdoms of the middle hills.

The celebrated khukuri — the recurved Nepali blade that has become an icon of Gurkha military identity worldwide — was the personal weapon of the Kshetri and hill-warrior tradition. Every Kshetri household traditionally kept a khukuri, and skill in its use was a marker of masculine identity in Kshetri culture.

After unification, the new Kingdom of Nepal was administered through a system in which Kshetri and Brahmin families held most positions of power — as military officers, district administrators (subba), tax collectors, judges, and court officials. The Rana period (1846–1951), during which the Jung Bahadur Rana family seized effective power and established a hereditary oligarchy of prime ministers, was itself a product of Kshetri-Rajput political culture.

Post-1950 Nepal: Transformation of Kshetri Society

The end of the Rana oligarchy in 1951, followed by the establishment of a constitutional monarchy, the Panchayat system (1960–1990), multi-party democracy (1990), the decade-long Maoist civil war (1996–2006), and finally the establishment of the Federal Democratic Republic (2008) — all of these political transformations profoundly affected the Kshetri community. Their historical dominance of state institutions came under challenge from ethnic and indigenous rights movements demanding representation for Janajati (indigenous nationality) groups, Madhesi communities, Dalits, and women. The new federal constitution of 2015 established an affirmative action system specifically designed to ensure that historically marginalized communities gained access to state institutions previously dominated by Brahmin-Kshetri hill elites.

Today, the Kshetri community spans an extraordinary socioeconomic range: from some of Nepal's wealthiest and most powerful political, military, and business families to some of its poorest subsistence farmers in remote hill villages. This internal diversity makes the Kshetri community one of the most sociologically complex in Nepal.

Interesting Fact: The Gurkha soldiers — Nepal's legendary warriors who have served in the British and Indian armies for over 200 years, earning more Victoria Crosses (Britain's highest military honor) per capita than any other regiment — are drawn primarily from Kshetri, Magar, Gurung, Rai, and Limbu communities. The regimental motto of the Royal Gurkha Rifles — "Better to die than to be a coward" — is a near-perfect expression of classical Kshatriya dharma.


Mystery: The genetic and anthropological relationship between the Khas people and other ancient Indo-Aryan and Indo-Iranian populations of Central and South Asia remains incompletely understood. Some recent genetic studies have found genetic signatures in Nepali hill populations suggesting connections to populations in the Pontic steppe and ancient Iran — consistent with the ancient Indo-Aryan migration theory — but also significant admixture with local populations of possible East Asian affinity. Whether the Khas were a distinct ancient people who arrived thousands of years before any recorded history, or a relatively recent formation produced by the encounter between early Indo-Aryan migrants and indigenous Tibeto-Burman populations, remains an open and actively researched question.

Language

Nepali: The Language of a Nation, Born in Kshetri Hills

Artistic Nepali Devanagari typography

The Kshetri community is the primary historical bearer and carrier of the Nepali language (नेपाली) — the official language of Nepal, a language spoken by over 17 million people as their mother tongue and used as a second language or lingua franca by tens of millions more across Nepal, India, Bhutan, and the global Nepali diaspora. The fact that the language of the Khas-Kshetri hills became the national language of a country containing 125+ distinct ethnic groups and over 123 languages is one of the most remarkable sociolinguistic stories in Asian history.

Linguistic Classification and Family

Indo-European language family tree

Nepali belongs to the Indo-Aryan branch of the Indo-European language family — the same vast family that includes Sanskrit, Hindi, Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, Gujarati, Marathi, Sinhala, and Romani, as well as (through the Iranian branch) Persian, Kurdish, and Pashto, and (through the deeper common ancestor Proto-Indo-European) Greek, Latin, the Romance languages, the Germanic languages including English, the Slavic languages, and many others. This makes Nepali, at the deepest level of linguistic genealogy, a cousin of English — both descended from the same hypothetical Proto-Indo-European ancestral language spoken on the Eurasian steppe roughly 5,000–6,000 years ago.

Within the Indo-Aryan branch, Nepali is most closely related to Kumaoni and Garhwali — the languages spoken in the hill districts of Uttarakhand in northern India, which were historically part of the same Khas-Pahari cultural and linguistic continuum as western Nepal. It is more distantly related to Hindi, which shares its Sanskrit roots but diverged significantly in vocabulary and grammar due to centuries of Persian and Turkic influence. Nepali, by contrast, remained much closer to its Sanskrit source, preserving archaic grammatical features and vocabulary that have been lost or altered in Hindi.

Sanskrit: The Sacred Ancestor and Daily Presence

The relationship between Nepali and Sanskrit is extraordinarily intimate — far more so than the relationship between most modern European languages and Latin. Sanskrit-derived (tatsama) words make up the core of Nepali's formal, literary, and religious vocabulary. A literate Kshetri person reading a Sanskrit religious text will recognize enormous numbers of words, many of them used in daily Nepali speech. Kinship terms, religious terms, natural phenomena, and abstract concepts are all Sanskrit in root and are used unchanged or nearly unchanged in everyday Nepali.

This Sanskrit proximity is not accidental: the Kshetri and Brahmin hill communities were the custodians of Sanskrit learning in Nepal's hill regions. Brahmin priests performed all rituals in Sanskrit; Kshetri patrons funded the learning of Sanskrit and employed Brahmin priests. The Sanskrit tradition was the prestige intellectual culture of the hill-Hindu world, and the Nepali language absorbed it deeply.

The Sinja Valley and the Birth of Written Nepali

The Sinja inscriptions

The earliest attested written records in what linguists recognize as an early form of Nepali — called Old Khas or Early Parbatiya — come from the Sinja Valley in Jumla district, the heartland of the Khas Malla Kingdom. Stone inscriptions found at Sinja dating to the 12th and 13th centuries CE are the oldest documents in the Nepali language. These inscriptions, carved in a Devanagari-like script, record royal proclamations, land grants, and administrative decisions of the Khas Malla kings.

The linguistic features of these early inscriptions show a language already differentiated from Sanskrit and from the plains Indo-Aryan languages — a hill variety that had developed its own phonological patterns while retaining a heavily Sanskrit-influenced vocabulary. From these beginnings in the far-western hills, the Nepali language spread eastward across the middle hills as the Khas cultural and political sphere expanded, and then achieved national status through the military and administrative expansion of the Gorkha Kingdom after 1768.

Devanagari Script: The Writing System of Nepali

Devanagari Script: The Writing System of Nepali

Nepali is written in Devanagari (देवनागरी) — one of the world's most logically designed writing systems. The name means roughly "the script of the city of the gods" (deva = divine, nagari = of the city/civilization). Devanagari is an abugida or alphasyllabic system, meaning that its basic units are consonant-vowel syllables rather than pure consonants or pure vowels. Each consonant symbol carries an inherent short a vowel; when a different vowel follows, a diacritic mark is added; when no vowel follows, the virama or halanta (्) suppresses the inherent vowel.

Devanagari has 47 primary characters (12 vowels and 35 consonants in standard Nepali usage), plus a system of conjunct consonant forms in which two or more consonants appearing together without an intervening vowel are written as fused or stacked forms. The script reads left to right and is notable for the horizontal line (shirorekha or head-line) that runs along the top of most letters, visually connecting them and giving Devanagari text its distinctive appearance. Learning Devanagari is considered a fundamental literacy achievement in Kshetri communities.

Dialects and Regional Varieties

Nepali is not a monolithic language — it has significant dialectal variation across its geographic spread. Far-Western Nepali (Karnali Dialect) is considered by linguists to be the closest surviving form of classical Khas Kura, preserving archaic grammatical forms and a more purely Sanskrit-Khas vocabulary. Central Hill Nepali (Kathmandu Valley) is the prestige standard variety taught in schools and used in media, absorbing significant vocabulary from Newari and influenced by Hindi. Eastern Hill Nepali (Kirat Corridor) reflects contact with Rai, Limbu, and other Tibeto-Burman languages. Terai Nepali shows influence from Hindi, Maithili, Bhojpuri, and Awadhi. Darjeeling and Sikkim Nepali has developed distinctly under the influence of English, Bengali, and Hindi.

The Gaaine Oral Literary Tradition

The Gaaine (गाइने) are a caste community whose traditional hereditary occupation was wandering minstrelsy — roaming the hills from village to village, playing the sarangi (a small bowed lute) and singing ballads (gatha) of historical events, legendary heroes, gods, romances, and moral tales. The Gaaine tradition represents one of Nepal's greatest oral literary heritages and was sustained almost entirely by the patronage and audience of Kshetri and Brahmin hill communities. Gaaine songs preserved historical memory across generations before the age of widespread literacy — ballads about the battles of the Gorkha unification, about legendary Kshetri warriors, about the loves and tragedies of historical figures.

Proverbs and Oral Wisdom

Nepali proverb culture (ukhan-tukka, उखान-टुक्का) is extraordinarily rich and forms an important dimension of Kshetri linguistic heritage. Proverbs are used in daily conversation with a frequency and facility that reflects their deep embeddedness in the culture. Many reflect specifically Kshetri-hill values: the importance of honor and shame, the danger of cowardice, the virtue of hard work, the uncertainty of fate, the power of the gods, and the wisdom of elders. Classic examples include: "Marnu bhanda lajja ramro" (Shame is better than death — reflecting the Kshetri honor code), and "Ghar ko bagh ban ko sher" (A tiger at home, a deer in the forest — criticizing those who are bold only in safety).

Interesting Fact: Nepali is not just Nepal's national language — it is also one of the 22 officially scheduled languages of India, recognized in the Eighth Schedule of the Indian Constitution. This recognition was won in 1992 after decades of campaigning by Nepali-speaking communities in Darjeeling, Sikkim, and the Northeastern states of India — communities composed largely of Kshetri and Brahmin families who migrated into the Indian hills during the 19th and 20th centuries.


Mystery: Linguists have long puzzled over a specific grammatical feature of Nepali: its evidential system — a set of grammatical markers that indicate how the speaker knows what they are saying (whether from direct experience, inference, or hearsay). This system — relatively unusual among Indo-Aryan languages — is strikingly similar to evidential systems found in Tibeto-Burman languages like Tibetan. Did the Khas Nepali language borrow this feature from contact with Tibeto-Burman neighbors? Or did it develop independently? Linguists remain divided.

Music and Dance

Sound as Sacred Act: The Philosophy of Kshetri Music

In the Kshetri-Hindu worldview, sound is not merely aesthetic — it is cosmic. The universe, according to ancient Hindu cosmology as understood through the Upanishads and the Yoga-Sutras, began with sound — the primordial vibration Om (ॐ), which contains within itself all possible sounds and therefore all possible realities. Music is the human approximation of this cosmic sound. To sing a devotional hymn is not merely to perform art — it is to participate in the structure of the universe. To play a sacred drum at a temple ceremony is to echo the heartbeat of Shiva's drum (the damaru) that maintains the rhythm of creation.

This metaphysical understanding means that in Kshetri culture, the boundaries between musical performance, religious ritual, social celebration, and personal spiritual practice are permeable and often nonexistent. A wedding is simultaneously a legal, social, and cosmic event, and its music performs all three functions at once.

Panche Baja: The Five Sacred Instruments

The Panche Baja ensemble — five sacred instruments that have accompanied every Kshetri life-cycle ceremony for millennia

The Panche Baja (पञ्च बाजा — Five Instruments) is the ritual instrumental ensemble that has accompanied every significant Kshetri life-cycle event and religious ceremony for centuries, possibly millennia. The five instruments that compose this ensemble are:

1. Dholak (ढोलक)

A barrel-shaped double-headed drum, typically 40–60 cm long, with one head tuned to a lower pitch and the other to a higher pitch. Played with both hands in complex polyrhythmic patterns, the dholak provides the primary rhythmic foundation. Different ceremonies have their own specific rhythmic patterns — the patterns for a wedding procession are different from those for a sacred thread ceremony, which are different again from those for a funeral.

2. Damaha (दमाहा)

A large kettledrum — a copper or brass hemisphere with a skin membrane — carried on the player's body and struck with a curved wooden beater. The damaha produces a deep, resonant boom that carries over long distances. The sound of the damaha carries a specific cultural weight: hearing it at a distance tells listeners in a village that an important ceremony is underway.

3. Tyamko (त्याम्को)

A smaller kettledrum, also metal-bodied, played in dialogue with the damaha. While the damaha provides the deep foundation, the tyamko fills in higher-pitched rhythmic elaborations and creates the dynamic complexity of the Panche Baja rhythmic texture. The tyamko is often played by the most technically skilled percussion player in the ensemble.

4. Sahanai (सहनाई)

A double-reed wind instrument — the Nepali version of the shehnai — consisting of a conical tube widening to a bell at the end. The sahanai is the melodic voice of the Panche Baja ensemble. Its nasal, penetrating timbre is simultaneously festive and slightly mournful — a quality that makes it appropriate for the emotional complexity of life-cycle ceremonies. The sahanai requires circular breathing technique for sustained playing, a skill that Damai musicians develop over years of practice.

5. Karnal (कर्नाल)

A long, straight or slightly curved ceremonial trumpet — typically 1–1.5 meters long — made of brass or copper, producing a deep, powerful blast used to mark ceremonial moments (the arrival of the groom, the beginning of a ritual, the conclusion of a ceremony). The karnal does not play melodic passages but rather ceremonial signal calls — short, structured calls that have specific conventional meanings known to the community.

The Damai caste — the traditional tailors and musicians of the Kshetri-Brahmin hill community — are the hereditary players of the Panche Baja. The relationship between Damai families and Kshetri-Brahmin households is called jajmani — a system of hereditary service and reciprocal obligation in which the Damai provides music (and tailoring) for all of the Kshetri family's ceremonies, in return for regular payment in grain, cloth, money, and food. The Panche Baja sound remains one of the most emotionally powerful sonic experiences for any Kshetri person — the sound of their community's most important moments, heard throughout childhood, indelibly associated with love, celebration, duty, and belonging.

Deuda: The Ancient Dance-Song of the Western Hills

Deuda (देउडा) is the most ancient, most distinctively Khas-Kshetri, and arguably the most culturally significant musical and dance tradition of the far-western hill communities. It is simultaneously a musical genre, a dance form, a form of oral poetry, a social institution, and a spiritual practice — and it cannot be fully understood if any of these dimensions is separated from the others.

Deuda is performed as a circular or linear group dance accompanied by antiphonal (call-and-response) singing. In its classic form, a group of men and women — typically between 10 and 100 participants, though major festival performances can involve hundreds — form a circle or two facing lines. The dance movement is relatively simple: a rhythmic stepping pattern, often with hand-holding or arm-linking, that moves the group in a slow circle or sways in place. The simplicity of the dance movement is intentional — it allows participants to focus their energy on the singing.

The singing is the heart of Deuda. A lead singer begins a verse — these verses are called deuda geet — typically 4 lines of highly structured meter with specific syllable counts and rhyme patterns. The ability to improvise skillful, witty, moving, or clever Deuda verses is one of the most admired verbal arts in far-western Kshetri culture. After the lead singer completes a verse, the group responds — either with a fixed response chorus or with a counter-verse from the opposite side.

The themes of Deuda poetry are extraordinarily wide-ranging: devotional praise of local deities, romantic love and longing, seasonal descriptions, social commentary, and courtship — young men and women using the Deuda format to exchange poetic expressions of attraction under the socially sanctioned cover of a communal art form. Deuda is performed most intensely during the Gaura festival, and in some communities, Deuda sessions can last all night.

Jhyaure: The Song of Longing

Jhyaure (झ्याउरे) is a folk song genre of the middle hills — particularly associated with the Kshetri and Brahmin communities of central and western Nepal — characterized by a distinctive swinging triple-time rhythm (its name is onomatopoeic, imitating the rhythmic pattern of the beat) and themes of longing, separation, and the emotional life of hill communities. Jhyaure songs address directly the experience of separation (biraha) — the separation of wives from husbands who have gone to work in distant cities or serve in the army, the separation of children from parents, the separation of the heart from its home.

This theme of biraha has deep spiritual dimensions: the beloved can symbolize the divine, and the soul's longing for reunion with God is expressed in the same emotional vocabulary as a wife's longing for her husband. Jhyaure is the dominant musical genre of Teej festival and the two are so associated that "Teej songs" and "Jhyaure" are often used interchangeably. Modern Teej songs increasingly address women's rights, domestic violence, political representation, and gender equality — using the traditional melodic and rhythmic framework to carry radically updated content.

Madal: The Universal Rhythm of the Hills

A madal player — Nepal's most beloved folk instrument, central to Kshetri musical culture

The Madal (मादल) is Nepal's most beloved and widely played folk instrument — a double-headed cylindrical hand drum played horizontally across the lap, struck on both heads with the palms, fingers, knuckles, and heels of the hands in intricate polyrhythmic patterns. The madal is not specific to any single community but is particularly central to Kshetri musical culture and is the primary accompanying instrument for folk songs in the Kshetri-Brahmin hill tradition.

The madal is typically made from a hardwood cylinder with goatskin or cowhide membranes on both ends, tensioned by leather lacing. The two heads are tuned to different pitches — the bass head (left) is lower, the treble head (right) is higher. Black tuning paste (kalo maato) is applied to the center of the bass head to lower its pitch and add a buzzing quality. Learning to play the madal is considered a natural part of growing up in Kshetri hill communities — not a specialized skill but a basic cultural literacy.

Bhajan, Kirtan, and Sorathi

Bhajan and Kirtan are the two main forms of Hindu devotional music practiced in Kshetri communities. Bhajan refers to devotional songs addressed to a specific deity — typically Vishnu (in his forms as Krishna or Rama), Shiva, or the goddess Durga/Bhagwati — accompanied by harmonium, tabla, madal, and manjira. The Bhajan repertoire draws on Sanskrit shloka from the Vedas and Puranas, compositions of medieval North Indian saints, Nepali-language devotional compositions, and local temple traditions. Particularly beloved are the Ram Charita Manas bhajans — songs drawn from Tulsidas's epic retelling of the Ramayana.

Sorathi is a ritual performance tradition found among Kshetri communities in the western hills — a form of dramatic dance performed at festivals dedicated to local protective goddesses and during specific ritual occasions requiring the invocation of divine presence. Sorathi performances typically take place at night, in the open area before a temple, lasting until dawn. As the night progresses and the drumming intensifies, Sorathi dancers may enter states of trance or possession (aavesha) in which they are believed to be temporarily inhabited by the deity they are representing — a living example of how deeply music, dance, religion, and social function are integrated in Kshetri culture.

Interesting Fact: The Deuda tradition of the far-western hills has been submitted to UNESCO for recognition as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. What makes this particularly remarkable is that the entire musical tradition is purely vocal and percussive — there are no melodic instruments whatsoever. All melody is produced by the human voice, sometimes in complex polyphonic textures with multiple simultaneous melodic lines. Ethnomusicologists studying Deuda have noted this as unusually sophisticated for a "folk" tradition, suggesting a very long independent development of vocal technique in the western hills.


Mystery: The Panche Baja tradition's deep antiquity raises the question of why exactly these five instruments — and not others — became the sacred ensemble of hill-Hindu ceremony. Some scholars propose that each instrument corresponds to one of the five elements in Hindu cosmology (earth, water, fire, air, and space), with the damaha representing the earth's deep vibration and the sahanai representing air and breath. However, no ancient textual source definitively confirms this interpretation. The true origin of the Panche Baja ensemble's specific composition remains unknown.

Festivals and Culture

The Festival Calendar as a Map of the Cosmos

The Kshetri festival calendar is one of the densest and most complex in South Asia — a year-round sequence of religious observances, agricultural celebrations, seasonal transitions, deity propitiation rituals, and social gatherings that together constitute a comprehensive map of the community's relationship with time, the divine, the natural world, and each other. The calendar operates according to the Bikram Sambat (BS) — Nepal's official lunar-solar calendar, approximately 56 years and 8.5 months ahead of the Gregorian calendar. The Bikram Sambat calendar governs all significant decisions in traditional Kshetri life: auspicious dates for weddings, sacred thread ceremonies, house-warming rituals, and business ventures are all determined by consulting the Panchang (the traditional almanac).

Dashain: The Great Festival of the Warrior Goddess

Dashain (दशैं), known formally as Vijaya Dashami, is the longest, most important, most expensive, and most emotionally significant festival in the Kshetri calendar. It spans 15 days in the month of Ashwin (September–October), consuming weeks of preparation, days of active celebration, and generating a tidal wave of social activity, family reunion, economic exchange, and devotional intensity unmatched by any other event in the Nepali year.

The mythological basis of Dashain is the Devi Mahatmya — the "Greatness of the Goddess" — describing the goddess Durga's nine-day battle against the armies of the buffalo demon Mahishasura and his eventual destruction on the tenth day. For the Kshetri community specifically, this mythology resonates at an additional level: Durga is pre-eminently a warrior goddess — she carries weapons, she rides a lion, she destroys the demonic enemy. Her victory is a Kshatriya victory. The festival is therefore simultaneously a universal Hindu celebration and a specifically Kshatriya celebration of martial identity and the sacred duty to destroy evil by force.

The fifteen days unfold in precise sequence: Ghatasthapana (Day 1) establishes the sacred clay pot (kalash) in every Kshetri household, with jamara (barley seedlings) grown in darkness for the coming days. Phulpati (Day 7) brings the royal procession from the Gorkha ancestral palace, connecting the festival to Nepal's Kshetri military history. Maha Ashtami and Maha Navami (Days 8–9) are days of intense ritual: animal sacrifices to the goddess, and the Kal Puja / Ayudha Puja (Worship of Weapons) on Navami — the khukuris, swords, rifles, and military equipment of the household cleaned, garlanded, and worshipped. Nepal's military establishments perform elaborate ceremonies on this day, connecting modern Nepali military culture directly to its Kshetri-Hindu roots.

Vijaya Dashami (Day 10) is the climax — the day of tika. Dressed in new clothes, younger Kshetri family members bow before their elders, who apply a large tika of red vermillion paste, yogurt, and rice grains to the forehead, simultaneously placing golden-yellow jamara seedlings behind the ears and whispering a Sanskrit blessing wishing long life, prosperity, and divine protection. The social logistics of Vijaya Dashami are extraordinary: Nepal's highways and airports are overwhelmed as people travel from Kathmandu to home villages, from foreign countries to Nepal, from army postings to family homes. The Dashain homecoming is one of the most powerful social-emotional experiences in Nepali life. Kojagrat Purnima (Day 15) concludes the festival with the worship of Lakshmi and a night vigil — gambling with cards and dice is permitted and encouraged as an auspicious activity honoring the goddess of wealth.

Tihar: Five Days of Light, Life, and the Bond Between Siblings

Tihar (तिहार), known across South Asia as Deepawali or Diwali, is the Festival of Lights — five days in the month of Kartik (October–November). The Nepali celebration has a distinct ritual logic and its own unique culminating ceremony — Bhai Tika — making it significantly different from the Indian Diwali.

Day 1 — Kaag Tihar (Day of the Crow): Crows are worshipped and offered food — honoring death's messenger and requesting that Yamaraj's messengers bring only good news. Day 2 — Kukur Tihar (Day of the Dog): Dogs are garlanded, marked with tika, and offered special food — an acknowledgment of the value of faithfulness and loyalty in all relationships. Day 3 — Laxmi Puja and Gai Tihar: The main day of lights — every room illuminated with oil lamps, candles, and electric lights, with intricate rangoli decorations welcoming Lakshmi. Cows are also worshipped and garlanded. Day 4 — Goru Tihar: Oxen — the working partners of hill agriculture — are worshipped in acknowledgment of the intimate partnership between human labor and animal labor. Day 5 — Bhai Tika: The culminating ceremony, one of the most emotionally powerful rituals in all of Kshetri life.

On Bhai Tika, sisters perform an elaborate protective ritual for their brothers. The ceremony has deep mythological roots: the god Yamaraj (Death) was once persuaded by his sister Yamuna to spare her brother — Yamuna's devotion and love were so powerful that even death relented. The sister creates a large saptagandha tika (seven-colored blessing mark) using seven sacred substances — oil, yogurt, vermillion, dubo grass, unbroken rice grains, flower petals, and others — applied to her brother's forehead. She creates a protective circle around him with oil and flowers, garlanding him with multiple marigold garlands. Her brother gives her gifts of money, clothing, and jewelry in return. The exchange is simultaneously a practical expression of sibling solidarity and a cosmic negotiation with fate.

Teej: The Festival Women Own

Teej (तीज), properly called Haritalika Teej, is the festival most exclusively and uniquely associated with Kshetri and Brahmin women in the hills of Nepal. It is celebrated on the third day of the bright fortnight of the month of Bhadra (August–September), dedicated to the goddess Parvati and her eternal devotion to Shiva. Teej is, in the deepest sense, a women's festival — created by women, organized by women, performed by women, and expressing specifically female religious experience, social reality, and spiritual longing.

The fast of Teej is one of the strictest in the Hindu calendar: many women observe Nirjala Teej — a complete fast without even water, lasting from sunset of the second day until sunrise of the fourth day — more than 36 hours of total food and liquid abstinence. What makes Teej socially extraordinary is the complete inversion of austerity and celebration that structures the festival. The day before the fast — called Dar Khane Din — is a day of intense festive eating and celebration: women gather in groups dressed in their finest red clothes and gold jewelry, eating rich special Teej foods, singing Teej songs, dancing, and celebrating with an intensity that is the exact opposite of the austere fast that follows.

On the fast day itself, Kshetri women dress in red saris and gold ornaments and travel in groups to the nearest Shiva temple — most famously Pashupatinath temple in Kathmandu, which on Teej is transformed into one of the most visually spectacular religious gatherings in South Asia. Teej songs (Teej Geet) have become one of the most commercially significant genres in Nepali folk music, evolving to address domestic violence, gender inequality, women's rights, and political representation.

Gaura: The Ancient Festival of the Western Heartland

Gaura festival — women in procession carrying sacred water from the river, singing Deuda songs

Gaura (गौरा) or Gaura Parva is the festival that most exclusively and distinctly belongs to the Khas-Kshetri communities of the far-western hills — the districts of Humla, Jumla, Mugu, Dolpa, Kalikot, Dailekh, Jajarkot, Salyan, Doti, Bajhang, Bajura, Baitadi, Darchula, Achham, and surrounding areas. In these districts, Gaura is not merely an important festival — it is the defining cultural event of the year, the most eagerly anticipated and most intensely experienced celebration in the community calendar.

Gaura is celebrated in the month of Bhadra (August–September), honoring the goddess Gaura, understood as a local form of Parvati but worshipped in a specifically western-Khas ritual idiom. The festival lasts five days: on the first day, women perform Udoli — a ritual water-fetching procession from a sacred source, accompanied by Deuda singing. Subsequent days involve ritual bathing, special festival foods, ritual dances, and most importantly the extended Deuda singing sessions that can last through entire nights. On the final day, the clay images of Gaura and Mahadev are ceremonially immersed in a river — their dissolution in the water symbolizing the return of the divine to the cosmic source.

Interesting Fact: The Bhai Tika tika of Tihar is uniquely made from seven distinct substances mixed together — a formulation not found in any other Hindu tradition outside Nepal. The specific seven substances typically include: red vermillion (sindur), yogurt (dahi), oil (tel), dubo grass (Bermuda grass, considered sacred), akshata (whole unbroken rice), yellow powder of turmeric or the juice of makhamali flowers, and water from a sacred source. The application of this seven-substance tika is considered to create a barrier of sacred protection — a literal material shield against death — that will last until the following year's Bhai Tika.


Mystery: The Gaura festival contains ritual elements — particularly the clay image-making, the water-fetching procession, and certain dance forms — that appear to be far older than the Brahminic Hindu framework that now surrounds them. Some anthropologists have proposed that Gaura may preserve a pre-Vedic goddess worship tradition that existed among the indigenous Khas people before the arrival of formal Brahminic Hinduism in the western hills. If this interpretation is correct, then Gaura may be a 3,000+ year old religious tradition whose original theology — perhaps a form of animist earth-goddess worship — is now invisible beneath its Hindu surface.

Traditional Dress

Clothing as Identity, Status, and Sacred Marking

In Kshetri culture, clothing is never merely functional — it is a language of identity, a marker of social status, a signal of religious observance, and a vehicle of aesthetic expression simultaneously. The specific garments worn by Kshetri men and women, the materials they are made from, the colors they favor, the jewelry they wear, and the occasions on which different garments are appropriate all encode a complex semiotic system that every member of the community reads fluently from childhood.

Men's Traditional Dress: The Daura Suruwal

Daura Suruwal with Dhaka Topi — Nepal's national dress, born from Kshetri-Brahmin hill culture

The Daura Suruwal (दौरा सुरुवाल) is the national dress of Nepal — a designation that reflects its origin in Kshetri-Brahmin hill culture — and it is the most distinctive and elegant element of Kshetri male dress identity.

The Daura (दौरा) is the upper garment: a long-sleeved, collarless jacket-tunic that extends to approximately mid-thigh length, fastened at the front and sides with ties rather than buttons — a total of eight ties that cross the chest and fasten at the sides. The number eight is not arbitrary: in Hindu numerology, eight is the number of Ashta Mangala (the eight auspicious symbols) and represents completeness and cosmic order. The Daura is typically made from cotton, silk, or a cotton-silk blend, in white, cream, or occasionally other solid colors for formal occasions. The stitching is traditionally done by the Damai tailor-musician community.

The Suruwal (सुरुवाल) is the lower garment: trousers distinctive in their silhouette — extremely wide and gathered at the waist, then tapering dramatically toward a very tight fit around the lower leg and ankle. This shape — generous in the seat and thighs, tight at the ankle — is both practical (allowing freedom of movement for hill walking) and aesthetically distinctive. Completing the traditional outfit are: the Dhaka Topi (a small curved cap made from traditional handwoven Dhaka fabric, the most universally recognized symbol of Nepali national identity), the Patuka (a broad cloth sash wrapped around the waist), and the Khukuri.

The Khukuri: Blade of the Warrior

The khukuri — inward-curved Nepali blade, simultaneously tool, weapon, sacred object, and icon of Kshetri martial identity

No description of Kshetri male dress is complete without the khukuri (खुकुरी) — the distinctive inward-curving Nepali blade that is simultaneously a tool, a weapon, a symbol of Kshetri martial identity, and a sacred object. The khukuri's blade curves inward (the edge is on the inside of the curve), making it effective for both chopping (the curved blade concentrates force at the point of contact) and slashing in close combat. The handle is typically made from horn, bone, or wood. A traditional khukuri comes with two smaller companion knives in the same sheath: the karda (a small utility knife) and the chakmak (a blunt steel for starting fire). In traditional Kshetri households, the khukuri hangs prominently on the wall, is worshipped during Dashain, and is carried by men on journeys and important occasions.

Women's Traditional Dress: Gunyo Cholo, Sari, and Sacred Jewelry

Traditional Kshetri women's dress is a complex system in which different garments signal different life stages, different occasions, and different social statuses. Gunyo Cholo (गुन्यो चोलो) — consisting of a Gunyo (long wrap-around skirt in red or deep pink with a distinctive border) and a Cholo (close-fitting short blouse) — is the classic traditional dress of young Kshetri women, particularly associated with a girl's coming-of-age ceremony in which she is for the first time dressed in the Gunyo Cholo by her mother. For adult women, the Sari became the dominant formal dress during the Shah and Rana periods; red saris are worn on the most auspicious occasions, particularly weddings and Teej festival.

The sacred markers of married status are elaborately codified: the Potey (पोते) — a necklace of small glass beads in deep red and green, given by the husband at the wedding, worn continuously throughout married life; the Tilhari (तिलहरी) — a golden pendant at the center of the potey; the Pote Sindur (सिँदुर) — red vermillion applied to the hair parting as a daily ritual of married identity; and the elaborate gold jewelry system including Dhungri (ear ornaments), Nau-gedi (nine-stranded gold necklace), Bulaki (nose ornament), glass bangles, anklets, and finger rings.

Dhaka fabric — a handwoven textile with intricate geometric patterns in multiple colors — is used for a range of Kshetri traditional garments. Traditional Dhaka weaving is centered in Tansen (Palpa district) and Bhaktapur, and has been recognized as a significant intangible cultural heritage.

Interesting Fact: The eight ties of the Daura have been interpreted in multiple ways. Some traditional authorities explain that the eight ties represent the eight directions (Ashta Dik) of Hindu cosmology, making the Daura-wearing man symbolically oriented and protected in all directions simultaneously. Others explain that the specific fastening sequence of the ties, done correctly, forms a pattern corresponding to the Sanskrit letter Om when viewed geometrically — making the act of dressing in a Daura literally an embodied act of sacred writing.


Mystery: The khukuri's distinctive inward-curved blade shape — unlike virtually any other traditional blade in the world — has an unexplained origin. Various theories propose that it derived from the Greek kopis sword (brought by Alexander the Great's campaigns into northwestern India in the 4th century BCE), from ancient Indian agricultural tools, or from an indigenous Himalayan development. The khukuri appears in ancient Nepalese stone carvings from at least the 7th century CE, but the first khukuri maker and the moment of its invention are lost to history.

Unique Foods

Food as Ritual, Medicine, and Identity

Kshetri cuisine is one of the most misunderstood aspects of the community's cultural life — because Kshetri food, at first glance, can seem "simple" compared to the elaborately spiced cooking of South Asian lowland traditions. But this apparent simplicity is deceptive: Kshetri culinary culture is a highly sophisticated system in which every ingredient, every preparation method, every meal timing, and every combination of foods carries meaning — nutritional, medicinal (in the Ayurvedic sense), ritual, social, and ecological. The cuisine was developed over centuries of adaptation to the specific conditions of hill life: the physical demands of agricultural labor at altitude, the seasonal availability of ingredients, the need to store food through long winters, and the religious dietary regulations of the Hindu tradition.

Dal Bhat: The Sacred Daily Meal

A complete Kshetri Dal Bhat thali — bhat, dal, tarkari, achaar, and ghee in brass vessels

Dal Bhat (दाल भात) — lentil soup with rice — is not merely Nepal's national dish: it is the organizing principle of Kshetri daily life, the fixed point around which all other activities revolve. Traditional Kshetri households eat Dal Bhat twice a day — once in the late morning (around 10–11 AM) and once in the early evening (around 6–7 PM). "Bhat khayau?" (Have you eaten rice?) is the Nepali equivalent of "How are you?" — the daily greeting that checks on a person's fundamental wellbeing by asking whether they have eaten the fundamental food.

A complete Kshetri Dal Bhat thali consists of: Bhat (भात) — perfectly steamed white rice, the sacred grain used in all religious ceremonies; Dal (दाल) — protein-rich lentil soup made from masoor, moong, chana, or local beans, seasoned with ghee, cumin, turmeric, ginger, garlic, and dry red chilies; Tarkari (तरकारी) — seasonal vegetable curry with mustard oil and spices; Achaar (अचार) — spiced condiment such as tomato achaar, gundruk achaar, or radish achaar; and Ghiu (घ्यू) — golden clarified butter, both nutritionally vital for hill labor and ritually significant as the fat of Vedic fire rituals. Meat dishes featuring goat (khasi), chicken, buffalo, pork, and fish are also central to Kshetri cuisine, unlike the strictly vegetarian tradition of many Brahmin households.

Gundruk: The Fermented Soul of Kshetri Cuisine

Gundruk (गुन्द्रुक) is perhaps the most distinctively Nepali of all Kshetri food traditions — a fermented, sun-dried preparation of leafy green vegetables serving as a year-round source of nutrition, a condiment, a soup base, and a flavoring ingredient of unparalleled importance in hill cooking. The making of Gundruk is an ancient preservation technique developed specifically for the conditions of hill Nepal, where the winter months bring scarcity of fresh vegetables.

In late autumn, the large outer leaves of leafy vegetables — most commonly mustard greens (rayo ko saag), radish leaves, and cauliflower leaves — are allowed to wilt, then tightly packed into an earthenware pot or wooden container, sealed, and left in a warm place for 5–15 days to undergo lactic acid fermentation — the same microbial process that creates sauerkraut, kimchi, and yogurt. After fermentation, the greens are removed and sun-dried for 3–7 days until completely desiccated. These dried chips of fermented greens will keep without refrigeration for months or even years, providing nutrition, flavor, and the deeply satisfying sour complexity that Kshetri palates crave throughout winter.

Dhido: The Ancient Alternative to Rice

Dhido (ढिँडो) is a thick porridge made from buckwheat (phapar), finger millet (kodo), or corn (makai) flour, cooked by gradually adding flour to boiling water while stirring continuously until a thick, smooth, non-sticky mass forms. Dhido was historically the primary staple food of Kshetri communities in the mid-hills and high hills where rice cultivation was impossible due to altitude — and it remains widely eaten in remote hill districts as the meal that sustains agricultural labor. Dhido's nutritional profile is excellent: high in complex carbohydrates for sustained energy, moderate in protein, and rich in micronutrients. Buckwheat and finger millet are both cold-tolerant crops that grow at altitudes where rice and wheat cannot.

Sel Roti: The Sacred Ring Bread

Sel Roti being fried for Dashain — the ring of crisp rice bread whose aroma fills the neighborhood on festival mornings

Sel Roti (सेल रोटी) is a traditional Kshetri ring-shaped deep-fried bread made from a semi-liquid batter of rice flour, sugar, butter or ghee, cardamom, and sometimes banana or coconut — fried in large quantities of oil until crisp on the outside and slightly chewy within. The batter is poured in a continuous ring directly into the hot oil, creating the characteristic circular form. Sel Roti is the quintessential festival food of the Kshetri community — made in very large quantities for Dashain, Tihar, Teej, and other major festivals. Making Sel Roti is a social activity — women of the family and neighborhood gather together the night before a festival to grind the rice, prepare the batter, and fry the breads, the work accompanied by singing and conversation.

Kwati: The Nine-Bean Festival Soup

Kwati (क्वाटी) is a thick, nutritious soup made from a mixture of nine different sprouted beans and legumes — including black-eyed peas, soybeans, kidney beans, green mung beans, black chickpeas, pigeon peas, and others — cooked together with spices. Kwati is specifically associated with the festival of Janai Purnima in August. The nine varieties of beans correspond symbolically to the nine days of Navaratri and to the nine planets (navagraha) in Hindu astrology, making the soup a microcosm of cosmic completeness. Nutritionally, the combination of nine legumes provides a complete amino acid profile, and the sprouting of the beans before cooking increases digestibility and vitamin content — reflecting the deep nutritional intelligence embedded in traditional Kshetri food culture.

Sikarni, Ariselu, and Festival Sweets

Sikarni (सिकर्नी) is a traditional Kshetri dessert — thick sweetened strained yogurt flavored with sugar, cardamom, saffron, dried fruits (raisins, cashews, pistachios), and sometimes nutmeg. The yogurt is hung in a cloth bag for several hours to drain most of its liquid, creating a thick, creamy, intensely flavored mass. Sikarni is a festival food — rich, sweet, and labor-intensive, made for Dashain, weddings, and special celebrations. Ariselu (अरिसेलु) is a traditional sweet rice cake specific to Dashain and Tihar — made from soaked and ground glutinous rice, mixed with jaggery (unrefined cane sugar), shaped into small discs, and deep-fried until caramelized, with a sweet, crisp exterior and deeply caramelized flavor from the jaggery.

Raksi and Chhang: Traditional Fermented Beverages

Raksi (राक्सी) is the traditional distilled spirit of the Kshetri and broader hill-Nepali community — a clear distillate produced from fermented grain (most commonly millet, rice, or wheat) using a traditional still, with a typical alcohol content of 25–45%. The production of Raksi is a traditional household skill in Kshetri communities — particularly associated with women, who have traditionally managed the fermentation and distillation process. Tongba and Chhang (छाँग) are traditional fermented beer-like beverages made from fermented millet or barley — consumed at most social gatherings and consumed in higher-altitude Kshetri communities with particular warmth during the cold months.

Interesting Fact: Gundruk has attracted significant attention from food scientists because its fermentation process produces not just lactic acid but also significant quantities of B vitamins — particularly B12, which is typically only found in animal products. This makes Gundruk a rare plant-based source of B12, and researchers have proposed that it may be one of the traditional dietary adaptations that allowed Kshetri communities in high-altitude areas to avoid B12 deficiency. Traditional nutritional wisdom — "always have Gundruk with your meal" — may have been preserving biological health for centuries before B12 was scientifically identified.


Mystery: The origin of Sel Roti's distinctive ring shape is unexplained in any historical document. Why specifically a ring — rather than a disc, a stick, or any other form? Various theories propose: that the ring shape maximizes the ratio of crispy exterior to chewy interior; that it has ritual significance as an unbroken circle symbolizing eternity and divine wholeness; or that it was originally designed for practical transportation — Sel Roti rings can be threaded on a stick and carried easily, which would have been useful in a pre-container culture. The ring shape's origin remains one of the pleasant small mysteries of Kshetri culinary history.

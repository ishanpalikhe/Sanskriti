Section One

History and Origin

The Name, The Occupation, and the Legacy of Marginalization

The Pariyar — also known as Paraiyar, Paraiar, or in the historical record as 'Pariahs' (a term that entered European languages and became a general term for social outcasts) — are a Dalit community historically located primarily in Tamil Nadu and other parts of southern India, with significant communities in Nepal, Sri Lanka, Malaysia, and the global diaspora. In Nepal, the Pariyar are counted among the hill Dalit groups, concentrated primarily in the mid-hills and western regions of the country. According to census data, the Pariyar population in India alone numbers over 3 million individuals, making them one of the larger Dalit communities in South Asia.

The word 'Pariyar' is derived from a Tamil word meaning 'those who are beaten' or 'those who beat drums,' referring to the historical occupation through which Pariyar communities were identified in the traditional caste system: the playing of drums and other percussion instruments for ceremonies and processions organized by higher-caste groups. The history of the Pariyar is fundamentally a history of occupational and social marginalization. In the traditional caste hierarchy that dominated South Asian society — and still influences it despite legal reforms — the Pariyar were classified as outside the four-fold varna system entirely. They were 'untouchable,' a status that meant physical contact with Pariyar was considered polluting to members of higher castes.

Traditional Occupations and Economic Marginalization

The traditional occupations associated with Pariyar communities included the playing of drums and percussion instruments at ceremonies and festivals; the tanning of hides and production of leather goods; the removal of carrion animals (dead cows, buffalo, and livestock); and various forms of manual labor and menial service. In Nepal, the Pariyar are particularly known as skilled Damai — tailors and musicians — who have historically provided essential services to village communities across the hills.

These occupations were defined as polluting or degrading, and the performance of them was used to justify the Pariyar's low status in the caste hierarchy. Economically, the jajmani system (the hereditary relationship between service castes and patron castes) meant that Pariyar communities were bound to specific higher-caste patrons, obligated to perform services in return for grain payments, small amounts of money, or other subsistence rewards. The Pariyar had little ability to refuse work, negotiate for better terms, or shift to other occupations, as the caste system restricted them to their 'traditional' work.

Colonial Period and Political Awakening

The British colonial period (approximately 1760–1947) brought dramatic transformations to Pariyar communities, though not always in clearly progressive directions. Colonial administrative systems were less organized around the Hindu caste system than pre-colonial systems. Colonial law made the most overtly violent expressions of caste discrimination technically illegal — though enforcement was sporadic. It was during the colonial period that Pariyar political consciousness began to develop. In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Pariyar intellectuals and activists began to articulate demands for dignity, justice, and social equality.

Independent Nepal and the Legal End of Untouchability

In Nepal, the landmark Muluki Ain revision of 1963 formally abolished untouchability and caste-based discrimination. The 2007 Interim Constitution and the 2015 Constitution of Nepal explicitly abolished all forms of caste discrimination, declaring the practice of untouchability a criminal offense. Yet the legal abolition of untouchability has not automatically translated into the end of caste oppression. Social discrimination, economic marginalization, and violence against Pariyar and other Dalit communities continue throughout the post-transition period, even as the legal framework that once justified these practices has been removed.

Contemporary Pariyar communities in Nepal remain marked by the historical legacy of untouchability and caste oppression. While some Pariyar have accessed education, professional employment, and upward mobility, many others remain concentrated in poverty, in occupations descended from the 'polluting' work of their historical past, and in situations of continued caste discrimination. The contemporary Pariyar community thus encompasses extraordinary diversity: from musicians and artisans to government officials and activists working for Dalit rights.

Interesting Fact: The word 'Pariah' in English — used to mean a social outcast — is directly derived from 'Pariyar.' The colonial encounter between British officials and South Asian caste realities exported this Tamil word into the English language, where it became a universal term for rejection and exclusion, a linguistic mark of how profoundly the caste system shaped even the colonizers' vocabulary.


Mystery: Historians debate whether the Pariyar's origin as a community distinct from other Dalit groups lies in a specific occupational specialization that pre-dates the formal caste system, or whether the category itself was constructed through the jajmani system's rigid hereditary logic. The drum-playing origin story has deep roots, but archaeological evidence for the antiquity of this occupational boundary remains fragmentary.

Section Two

Language

Nepali as the Primary Language

In Nepal, the Pariyar community speaks Nepali as their primary language — the Indo-Aryan national language that serves as the lingua franca of the country. Unlike the South Indian Pariyar communities whose mother tongue is Tamil, the Nepali Pariyar have been thoroughly integrated into the Nepali-speaking hill culture for centuries, adopting Nepali as their everyday and ceremonial language. Their Nepali speech often carries regional dialectal features specific to the western and mid-hills districts where Pariyar communities are most concentrated.

The Tamil Language and South Indian Pariyar Identity

For the South Indian Pariyar, Tamil is the language of identity. Tamil is one of the world's oldest continuously spoken languages, with a literary tradition going back at least to the early centuries of the Common Era. The Pariyar did not develop a caste-specific language distinct from Tamil; rather, they speak the same Tamil language as all Tamil communities, though their specific variety of Tamil may include elements of community-specific vocabulary and speech patterns that historically marked their caste status.

Like many Dalit communities, the Pariyar were historically excluded from access to formal education and Tamil literacy. While Tamil was developed as a sophisticated literary and administrative language, Pariyar communities were denied access to Tamil literacy, confined to oral transmission of knowledge. This created a situation in which Pariyar were speakers of Tamil but not readers or writers of Tamil — contributing to their marginalization in literate Tamil culture and limiting their access to education and economic opportunities that required literacy.

Language, Caste Identity, and Reclamation

Within both Nepali and Tamil speech, certain linguistic markers historically identified someone as Pariyar or from a low-caste background. Accent, vocabulary choices, forms of address, and particular expressions could mark one's caste status. The Pariyar, particularly, were often identified by the use of certain occupational terms in their speech and by accent features that marked them as coming from lower-caste communities. In contemporary times, educated Pariyar activists have reclaimed their linguistic identity — using language not as a mark of subordination but as a vehicle for asserting Dalit pride and demanding social equality.

Interesting Fact: The Dalit literary movement in Nepal, active since the 1980s, has produced Pariyar poets and writers who have composed powerful work in Nepali that speaks directly to the experience of caste discrimination. These writers have transformed the language of their oppressors into an instrument of liberation, using the very words of dominant culture to demand recognition of Dalit humanity.


Mystery: Whether Nepali Pariyar communities once possessed a distinct community language or dialect — before being absorbed into the broader Nepali-speaking hill culture — is a question that linguistics has not fully resolved. Oral traditions among older Pariyar sometimes reference words and phrases not found in standard Nepali, possibly remnants of an older linguistic layer.

Section Three

Music and Dance

The Parai and the Drum of Resistance

The Parai (பறை) is the traditional drum most associated with Pariyar communities and with Pariyar musical and social identity. The Parai is a circular, cylindrical drum with goatskin or cowhide stretched across the top, played with sticks or hands to produce rhythmic patterns. Historically, the Parai was the instrument that Pariyar were forced to play at ceremonies and processions organized by higher castes — they would beat the drums to announce the arrival of wedding processions, to mark festivals, and to call communities to gatherings.

However, in the 20th and 21st centuries, the Parai has been transformed by Pariyar activists and artists into a symbol of Dalit resistance and pride. Parai performances have become a medium for expressing Dalit identity, for protesting caste oppression, and for asserting Dalit dignity and voice. The rhythm of the Parai is now used not just to serve higher-caste patrons but to speak for Dalit communities themselves, to demand justice, and to celebrate Dalit resistance.

Image: Pariyar musicians performing with traditional Parai drums — the instrument transformed from servitude to resistance

Panche Baja: The Five Sacred Instruments

In Nepal, the Pariyar (Damai) community is specifically associated with the Panche Baja — the ensemble of five sacred instruments traditionally played at Hindu ceremonies, weddings, and festivals. The five instruments typically include the Dhol (large double-headed drum), Damaha (large kettle drum), Tyamko (small kettledrum), Jhyali (cymbals), and Karnal (long ceremonial horn). The Pariyar have been the hereditary masters of this ensemble for centuries, and no major Hindu ceremony in the Nepal hills has traditionally been considered complete without the Panche Baja played by a Pariyar musician.

The Panche Baja tradition represents one of the great paradoxes of the caste system: the Pariyar were defined as 'untouchable' and denied access to the very religious spaces that their music sanctified. A Pariyar musician's Damaha would consecrate a Brahmin's wedding ceremony, yet the same musician could not be invited inside the wedding house to eat. This contradiction — being indispensable but excluded — captures the fundamental injustice of the jajmani system that governed Pariyar lives for centuries.

Oyilattam and Community Dance

Oyilattam (ஒயிலாட்டம்) is a traditional group dance form practiced in Tamil communities, including among South Indian Pariyar. Oyilattam involves rhythmic clapping, singing, and dancing in circular formations, and is performed at festivals, celebrations, and social gatherings. The dance has a joyful, celebratory character and brings communities together in shared movement and song. For Pariyar communities, Oyilattam has become a vehicle for cultural expression and community bonding — a space in which Pariyar can celebrate their culture and identity without the constraints of caste hierarchies that govern so much of social life.

Contemporary Dalit Music and Performance Arts

In recent decades, Pariyar and other Dalit communities have developed new forms of musical and performance expression that explicitly engage with questions of caste oppression, resistance, and dignity. Dalit poets, musicians, and performance artists create work that speaks directly to Dalit experience, that critiques caste oppression, and that celebrates Dalit humanity and struggle. In Nepal, Pariyar musicians have increasingly moved beyond the ceremonial role to perform original compositions addressing social justice, gender equality, and Dalit empowerment.

Interesting Fact: The Pariyar's mastery of the Panche Baja is so essential to Nepali ceremonial life that even during the Maoist civil war (1996–2006), when traditional social hierarchies were being actively dismantled by revolutionary politics, wedding celebrations continued to employ Pariyar musicians — suggesting that this musical tradition runs deeper than political ideologies and remains genuinely irreplaceable in Nepali cultural life.


Mystery: The precise tonal patterns and rhythmic structures of the Panche Baja have been transmitted orally across generations of Pariyar musicians with remarkable consistency — musicians from districts hundreds of kilometers apart produce recognizably similar ceremonial music. How this degree of standardization was maintained without written notation, across a community defined by marginalization and geographic dispersal, remains a fascinating unsolved puzzle of Nepali ethnomusicology.

Festivals and Culture

Dashain and Tihar: Shared and Contested Festivals

The Pariyar observe major Hindu festivals — particularly Dashain and Tihar — alongside other Nepali communities, though historically their participation was marked by the contradictions of caste. During Dashain, Pariyar musicians were summoned to play the Panche Baja at higher-caste households and public celebrations, receiving payment in grain and cash. Yet the same Pariyar families were often denied entry into the homes and temples where their music was required. The festival, which celebrates victory of good over evil, carried an ironic weight for communities experiencing the daily evil of caste discrimination.

Image: A Pariyar family lighting diyas during Tihar — the festival of lights observed across Nepal's communities

Pongal and Tamil Harvest Celebrations

Pongal (பொங்கல்) is the Tamil harvest festival celebrated primarily in January, marking the conclusion of the harvest season. Pongal is celebrated across Tamil communities, including among South Indian Pariyar, though the specific ways Pariyar celebrate it have been shaped by their historical position in Tamil society. The festival involves the preparation of a special rice dish called Pongal — rice cooked with lentils, vegetables, and jaggery — and is traditionally a time of family gathering, celebration, and thanksgiving for the harvest. In contemporary times, Pariyar communities increasingly celebrate Pongal as part of their own cultural and religious identity, reclaiming the festival as part of Tamil cultural heritage that belongs to all Tamil people.

Contemporary Dalit Festivals and Commemorations

In the modern period, Pariyar and other Dalit communities have established new festivals and commemorative occasions that celebrate Dalit identity and resistance. These include commemorations of Dalit leaders and activists — such as B.R. Ambedkar, whose birth anniversary (April 14) is observed as Ambedkar Jayanti across South Asian Dalit communities — celebrations of Dalit cultural achievements, and protests against contemporary manifestations of caste oppression. In Nepal, the Dalit Mukti Diwas (Dalit Liberation Day) has become an important commemorative occasion, marking the legal abolition of untouchability and calling attention to the work still remaining for true social equality.

Kaavadi and Community Devotion

The worship of Murugan (also known as Skanda), a major deity in Tamil Hindu tradition, has been an important part of South Indian Pariyar religious life. The Kaavadi (கவடி) is a portable shrine or framework used in processions celebrating Murugan, particularly during the festival of Thaipusam. The carrying of the Kaavadi — in which participants carry heavily decorated frameworks as acts of devotion — has been a practice in Tamil communities for centuries. For Pariyar communities, participation in these festivals has sometimes been restricted by caste hierarchies, but increasingly Pariyar are reclaiming their right to participate fully in these religious practices.

Interesting Fact: The celebration of Ambedkar Jayanti has become so significant among Nepali Dalit communities that it is now observed in schools, government offices, and public spaces across Nepal — a remarkable transformation in a country where Dalit rights were institutionally suppressed for centuries. The photograph of Dr. Ambedkar has become one of the most displayed images in Pariyar and other Dalit households in Nepal.


Mystery: Many Pariyar communities in Nepal maintain goddess worship traditions — particularly the veneration of local female deities — that appear to predate the formal Hindu religious system. Whether these traditions represent the survival of pre-Vedic religious practices among marginalized communities who were excluded from mainstream temple Hinduism, or whether they are a distinct religious development, remains debated among scholars of Nepali religion.

Section Five

Traditional Dress

Men's Dress: Daura Suruwal and Hill Tradition

Pariyar men in Nepal wear the Daura Suruwal — Nepal's national dress — for formal and ceremonial occasions. The Daura is the long-sleeved collarless jacket fastened with ties; the Suruwal the distinctive trousers voluminous above and tight at the ankle. The Dhaka Topi (Nepali cap woven from traditional Dhaka cloth) is worn to complete the formal ensemble. Historically, Pariyar men were sometimes prevented from wearing certain garments or colors reserved for higher-caste men — restrictions that have been legally abolished but whose social memory lingers in some communities.

In South Indian Pariyar tradition, men wear the dhoti — the wrapped cloth garment worn across South India — and shirt, in styles similar to those worn by other Tamil men. In modern times, many Pariyar men, particularly in urban areas and in younger generations, wear Western-style clothing in daily life, while maintaining traditional dress for festivals, religious ceremonies, and formal occasions.

Image: Pariyar men in traditional Daura Suruwal at a community ceremony in the Nepal hills

Women's Dress: Sari and Tamil Traditions

Pariyar women in Nepal wear the sari, the same garment worn by other Hindu women across South Asia. The specific styles, colors, and the jewelry worn reflect Pariyar participation in broader Nepali Hindu dress traditions. South Indian Pariyar women wear the Tamil saree — typically a nine-yard length of fabric wrapped around the body in a distinctive Tamil style. The colors and quality of the saree have historically been markers of caste status and economic position: wealthy and higher-caste women wore fine silk sarees in expensive dyes, while Pariyar and other low-caste women typically wore cotton sarees in limited color ranges.

Jewelry and Ornament

Tamil women traditionally wear extensive jewelry — bangles, nose ornaments, toe rings, anklets, necklaces, and ear ornaments. For Pariyar women, the wearing of jewelry was historically restricted by economic poverty and by caste restrictions. In traditional hierarchies, the wearing of certain ornaments was restricted to higher-caste women, and Pariyar women were forbidden or discouraged from wearing ornaments that marked status or respectability. In Nepal, Pariyar women wear the pote necklace (red and green glass beads) and sindur (vermillion at the hair parting) as markers of married status, following the same conventions as other Hindu women of the hills. In contemporary times, Pariyar women wear jewelry according to personal choice and economic ability, participating fully in traditions that are part of their heritage.

Interesting Fact: The Dhaka cloth used in Pariyar ceremonial caps and dress in Nepal is itself woven by artisan communities, creating a connection between textile traditions and community identity. Some Pariyar artisan families have developed distinctive weaving patterns that can be identified as coming from particular villages — a silent assertion of craft heritage within the broader Nepali textile tradition.


Mystery: Historical records from the medieval period in Nepal mention sumptuary laws that restricted the dress colors and jewelry available to different caste groups. The extent to which these laws were actually enforced among hill communities — as opposed to being theoretical legal structures — and what resistance Pariyar and other Dalit communities mounted against these restrictions remain only partially documented by existing historical sources.

Unique Foods

Dal Bhat: The Foundation of the Nepali Pariyar Table

Like all hill communities of Nepal, the Nepali Pariyar center their daily diet around Dal Bhat — lentil soup served with rice, vegetable curries, and pickles (achaar). This meal, eaten twice daily, is the organizing rhythm of household life. Pariyar households traditionally made do with lower-quality grains and fewer protein sources due to economic poverty enforced by the caste system — broken rice, cheaper lentil varieties, and limited meat or dairy. In contemporary times, improved economic access has broadened the Pariyar diet considerably, though economic constraints still affect many poor Pariyar families.

The Pariyar are non-vegetarian — unlike upper-caste Hindu communities who observed vegetarianism as a marker of ritual purity. Chicken, goat, and pork are important protein sources in Pariyar cuisine. The preparation of meat dishes has historically been unrestricted for Pariyar households and represents one of the culinary freedoms that stood apart from upper-caste dietary restrictions.

Image: A traditional Pariyar household meal — dal bhat with vegetable curry, achaar, and meat preparation in brass plates

Rice, Sambar, and the Tamil Pariyar Table

For South Indian Pariyar communities, rice is the central staple. The staple meal consists of rice, sambar (a lentil-based vegetable stew), and rasam (a thin, tangy broth made from tamarind and spices). Sambar is made from toor dal (pigeon peas) cooked with vegetables and flavored with the sambar powder — a complex spice blend containing coriander, cumin, dried red chilies, fenugreek, and other spices. Rasam is believed to have digestive and medicinal properties and is often drunk as a soup or poured over rice. For Pariyar communities, rasam has been a staple food that is both economical and nutritious, providing flavor and perceived health benefits to meals.

Idli, Dosa, and Fermented Traditions

Idli (இட்லி) — steamed cakes made from fermented rice and lentil batter — and Dosa (தோசை) — crispy crepes made from the same fermented batter — are traditional Tamil breakfast foods that have become popular across South India and worldwide. These fermented preparations make rice and lentils more digestible and nutritious, reflecting traditional Tamil knowledge of fermentation. Pariyar cuisine also makes extensive use of fermented preparations including various pickles and chutneys made from vegetables, fruits, and spices. The knowledge of fermentation technologies has traditionally been transmitted through families, with recipes and techniques passed down through generations.

Coconut, Meat, and Spice

Coconut is used extensively in Tamil and South Indian Pariyar cooking — both the meat of the coconut and coconut oil. Pariyar communities have historically consumed meat and fish, and these remain important protein sources. Chicken, goat, and fish curries are prepared with tamarind, spices, and coconut milk, creating rich, flavorful dishes. Jaggery (வெல்லம்) — unrefined cane sugar — is used in traditional Pariyar sweet preparations, creating payasam (puddings), laddoos, and other confections for festivals and special occasions.

Interesting Fact: The Pariyar tradition of cooking pork — a meat avoided by upper-caste Hindus — has in recent years been reclaimed by Dalit food activists as a cultural and political statement. 'Dalit food festivals' in Nepal and India have celebrated pork preparations and other traditionally marginalized foods as expressions of Dalit identity, resilience, and resistance to the dietary hierarchies of the caste system.


Mystery: Traditional Pariyar households often maintain a specific set of cooking vessels — typically clay pots or older-style brass — for certain preparations, preserving techniques that may predate the widespread introduction of aluminum and steel cookware. Whether these material choices reflect economic necessity, traditional preference, or the practical superiority of these materials for specific dishes is a question that food historians are beginning to examine through the lens of Dalit culinary heritage.

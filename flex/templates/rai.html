Section One

History and Origin

The Name, The Identity, and the Himalayan Heritage

The Rai people — known in various historical contexts as Rai, Rais, or as members of the Rai nationality or ethnic group — are a Tibeto-Burman ethnic community primarily inhabiting the eastern hills of Nepal and the northeastern regions of India, particularly the states of Sikkim, Darjeeling (in West Bengal), and Assam. According to Nepal's National Population and Housing Census 2021, the Rai population numbers 640,674 individuals in Nepal, making them one of the significant Janajati (indigenous nationality) groups in the country. They are found predominantly in the districts of Okhaldhunga, Khotang, Solukhumbu, Saptari, Udaypur, and surrounding eastern hill regions of Nepal. In India, Rai communities in Darjeeling and Sikkim number in the hundreds of thousands, having migrated from Nepal during the 19th and 20th centuries.

The word 'Rai' itself has uncertain etymology, though various theories propose connections to Sanskrit terms meaning 'king' or 'ruler,' or to local place names and historical titles. Unlike the Hindu caste system's varna classification, the Rai are not traditionally understood as occupying a specific ritual or occupational position within the Hindu hierarchy. Rather, the Rai are understood as an ethnic nationality with their own distinct languages, their own social organization, their own spiritual traditions — historically a syncretic blend of indigenous ancestor worship, animism, and elements of Hinduism — and their own cultural identity that existed independently of the Hindu caste system.

Rai (Kirati) people gathered at a traditional community festival in the eastern hills of Nepal — the ceremonies that bind the community across generations

Ancient Settlement and the Kirant Corridor

The eastern hills of Nepal are sometimes called the 'Kirant Corridor' or 'Kirant region,' referring to the ancient Kirant kingdom mentioned in Buddhist and Hindu texts as existing in the eastern Himalayan region at least 2,500 years ago. Whether the historical Kirant kingdom directly connects to the modern Rai people remains a matter of scholarly debate, but within Rai cultural tradition, the Kirant heritage is claimed as part of Rai ancestral legacy. The Kirant connection provides the Rai with a narrative of ancient political independence and cultural sophistication, linking contemporary Rai identity to a historical kingdom that predated the arrival of Hindu kingdoms from the south and west.

Archaeological and linguistic evidence suggests that Rai and related Tibeto-Burman peoples migrated to the Himalayan hills from the Tibetan plateau and from regions to the northeast — possibly modern-day Myanmar and beyond — over the course of several centuries in the pre-historical period. These migrations were not singular events but rather long-term population movements that resulted in the establishment of distinct ethnic communities: the Rai, the Limbu, the Sherpa, the Tamang, and others — each occupying specific ecological niches in the complex Himalayan landscape.

Terraced Agriculture and Settlement Patterns

The defining characteristic of Rai settlement has been their extraordinary skill in converting steep, forested hillsides into productive agricultural terraces. Over centuries, Rai communities carved hundreds of thousands of small terraced fields into the slopes of their valleys, each terrace supported by retaining walls built from stone without mortar. These terraces — still visible across the eastern Himalayan landscape — represent an extraordinary feat of labor organization, ecological knowledge, and engineering skill. The terraced landscape also shaped Rai social organization: construction and maintenance require coordination among multiple families and lineages, necessitating community governance structures that have persisted to the modern day.

The Gorkha Unification and Rai Responses

The unification of Nepal by Prithvi Narayan Shah (1723–1775) and the Gorkha kingdom's expansion eastward in the late 18th century brought the eastern hill regions — including Rai territories — under central state control for the first time. The Gorkha conquest involved military campaigns, the defeat of local Rai and Limbu rulers, and the imposition of Gorkha administrative structures. Yet the Gorkha rulers maintained many indigenous hill institutions and allowed considerable local autonomy in cultural and religious matters.

The British Colonial Period and the Gurkha Recruitment

The British colonial period (1857–1947), following the Anglo-Nepalese War, represented a transformative moment for Rai communities. The British, impressed by the military performance of Nepali hill soldiers, established the Gurkha regiments — elite military units recruited predominantly from Nepal's hill communities. The Rai became one of the primary recruitment sources for Gurkha soldiers, and over the next 150 years, hundreds of thousands of Rai men served in the British Indian army, and later in the Indian and British armies, fighting in locations ranging from Burma to North Africa to Europe. The Gurkha soldiers, predominantly Rai, Limbu, Magar, and Gurung, became world-famous for their bravery, loyalty, and skill in mountain warfare.

Modern Nepal and the Janajati Movement

The Federal Democratic Constitution of 2015 established provisions explicitly recognizing the Rai as one of Nepal's 'nationalities' (a status distinct from caste) and guaranteeing certain rights and representation protections. Contemporary Rai communities now span an enormous range: from subsistence farmers in remote hill villages to urban professionals in Kathmandu and Indian cities; from monolingual Rai speakers to fluent English speakers; from devotees of indigenous Rai religious practices to converts to various branches of Hinduism, Christianity, and Buddhism.

Interesting Fact: The Rai's traditional land-holding system — known as kipat — was a form of communal ownership that conferred inalienable rights restricted to ethnic Kirati members. This system, originating from pre-unification treaties with Gorkha rulers, allowed Rai clans to manage territories autonomously while paying nominal tribute, linking land stewardship directly to ancestral descent and cultural practice.


Mystery: The specific timeline of the Rai's ancestral migration from the Tibetan plateau and northeastern regions into the eastern Himalayan hills remains archaeologically underdocumented. Whether the Rai's Kirant ancestors were the builders of early settlement complexes identified in eastern Nepal's river valleys — some estimated at over 3,000 years old — or whether these sites predate Kirati presence, is an open question that ongoing archaeological surveys may eventually resolve.

Section Two

Language

The Rai Languages: A Tibeto-Burman Heritage

The Rai speak a family of languages — collectively referred to as Kiranti languages — that belong to the Tibeto-Burman branch of the Sino-Tibetan language family, the same language family as Tibetan, Burmese, and Chinese. There are over 26 distinct Rai dialects and sub-languages, reflecting the clan-based subgroups such as Khambu, Yamphu, Thulung, Bantawa, Khaling, and others. This diversity is so significant that the 2021 Nepal Census controversially listed no single "Rai language," prompting Rai organizations to formally raise concerns that the enumeration form failed to capture the reality of Rai linguistic identity.

The Rai languages are characterized by several distinctive linguistic features: a system of tones (variations in pitch that change meaning), a complex verb conjugation system that encodes information about subject, object, and tense, and a rich vocabulary for ecological and agricultural concepts reflecting the Rai's deep knowledge of hill ecology. The languages were historically transmitted entirely orally, and written records in Rai are a relatively recent development, dating primarily to the 20th and 21st centuries.

The Devanagari script — today the primary writing system used to represent Rai languages in Nepal's schools and publications

The Mundhum: Oral Scripture and Living Encyclopedia

Central to Rai linguistic and cultural identity is the Mundhum — an oral tradition serving as both religious scripture and cultural encyclopedia. The Mundhum outlines cosmology, genealogies, and ethical principles through narratives of primordial deities like Sumnima (the earth mother) and Paruhang (the sky father). This vast oral corpus — transmitted across generations by shamanic practitioners called Nakchong or Bijuwa — represents one of the most comprehensive indigenous oral traditions in all of South Asia. The recitation of Mundhum verses is believed to have healing and protective power, and experienced practitioners may recite for hours without pause.

Script, Literacy, and the Question of Language Preservation

Traditionally, the Rai languages had no standard written form. When Rai people needed to communicate in writing, they used Nepali or other regional languages. Today, Rai is most commonly written using the Devanagari script. Janajati advocates have demanded that Rai language be taught in schools and included in official curricula. However, many Rai parents — particularly in urban areas — choose schools where instruction is in Nepali or English, fearing that Rai-medium education would disadvantage their children in accessing higher education and economic opportunities.

Multilingualism and Language Contact

Most Rai people are multilingual, speaking one or more Rai languages (their mother tongue), Nepali (the national language and the lingua franca of the eastern hills), and often English or Hindi. In diaspora communities in India, Rai people may also speak Bengali or other Indian languages. Each generation of Rai has shown declining fluency in Rai languages as Nepali and English increasingly dominate in education and economic life — making language preservation one of the most urgent cultural challenges facing the community today.

Interesting Fact: The Rai's linguistic diversity is extraordinary even by Himalayan standards — with over 26 distinct speech varieties recognized by linguists, the "Rai languages" constitute more internal linguistic diversity than many entire national language families. Some Rai dialect speakers cannot readily understand the speech of Rai from a neighboring valley — a testament to the centuries of geographic isolation in which each community developed its own linguistic traditions.


Mystery: The Mundhum oral tradition contains cosmological narratives that scholars have noted share structural similarities with Tibetan Bön religious texts and certain Vedic hymns. Whether these similarities reflect ancient shared origins, long-distance cultural contact, or convergent development among Himalayan mountain peoples remains a fascinating open question in comparative religious studies.

Music and Dance

Traditional Music as Community Expression

Music and dance in Rai communities are integral expressions of social life, religious practice, and community celebration. Unlike the formalized devotional music traditions of Hindu communities, Rai music traditionally encompasses a broader range of contexts: work songs accompanied by agricultural labor, dance songs for social gatherings, lullabies for children, and ritual music for ceremonies marking life transitions. The emotional content of Rai music tends to emphasize themes of community solidarity, the beauty of the natural landscape, love and courtship, and the interconnectedness of the human and spiritual worlds.

Rai dancers performing the Sakela (Chyabrung) dance — the rhythmic group dance that is the most recognizable cultural expression of Rai identity

The Chyabrung: The Defining Rai Dance Form

The Chyabrung (also spelled Chyabruk or Chyabrung nritya) is perhaps the most distinctive Rai dance form — a traditional group dance characterized by rhythmic stepping, hand movements, and often circular or line formations. The Chyabrung is typically performed at festivals, weddings, and community celebrations, and its energetic, joyful character makes it one of the most visually striking aspects of Rai cultural expression. The dance is accompanied by singing (often call-and-response patterns), drumming, and sometimes other percussion instruments. The basic steps of the Chyabrung are learned from childhood, and skill in performing the dance is a marker of cultural knowledge and community belonging.

The Chyabrung is particularly central to the Sakela / Ubhauli festival celebrations, where entire communities — men and women, young and old — dance together in large formations. At major celebrations, hundreds of dancers may move in concentric circles, their synchronized steps creating a hypnotic communal experience that has been compared to collective meditation in motion.

The Jhumka: Grace in Motion

The Jhumka (झुम्का) is another traditional Rai dance — a more graceful, flowing dance often performed by women, characterized by swaying hip movements and gentle arm motions. The Jhumka is often performed at celebrations, festivals, and social gatherings, and represents a distinctly feminine expression of joy and celebration within Rai culture. Like the Chyabrung, the Jhumka is accompanied by singing and percussion, and reflects Rai values of community participation and collective joy.

Instruments and Musical Culture

Traditional Rai instruments include the Madal (a two-headed cylindrical drum played across the lap, shared with other Himalayan ethnic groups), the Sarangi (a bowed string instrument capable of extraordinary expressive range), various flutes and pipes, and percussion instruments like cymbals and gongs. The Sarangi, in particular, has a special place in Rai musical culture — its mournful, intensely expressive sound is used to accompany songs of longing, loss, and spiritual yearning, as well as joyful celebratory music. The instrument requires years of practice to master, and accomplished Sarangi players are highly respected in Rai communities.

Interesting Fact: The Chyabrung dance movements are not arbitrary but encode information about the Rai's relationship with the natural world — specific hand gestures represent different animals, plants, and natural phenomena important to Rai agriculture and ecology. In this sense, the dance functions as a living archive of ecological knowledge: elders who know the full vocabulary of Chyabrung hand gestures can 'read' the dance as a text about the Rai's relationship with their Himalayan environment.


Mystery: Certain ancient Rai ritual songs — performed only by Nakchong (shamans) during specific ceremonies — contain archaic vocabulary that is no longer understood even by fluent speakers of modern Rai dialects. Linguists have been unable to fully decode these archaic layers of Rai musical language. Whether they represent an earlier, now-extinct form of Rai language, or borrowings from a completely different pre-Kirati language community that once occupied the eastern Himalayan hills, remains unresolved.

Festivals and Culture

Ubhauli and Udhauli: The Twin Pillars of the Rai Year

Ubhauli (उभाउली) and Udhauli (उधाउली) — also known collectively as Sakela — are the two most important festivals in the Rai cultural calendar. Ubhauli, celebrated in late spring (around May, at the full moon of Baishakh), marks the planting season and upward migration of animals to high pastures; Udhauli, celebrated in late autumn (around November, at the full moon of Mangsir), marks the harvest and downward migration. Together they form a year-shaped pair of festivals that are fundamentally rooted in the Rai's relationship with the seasonal rhythms of Himalayan agriculture and ecology. Both festivals involve the Chyabrung dance, community feasting, offerings to ancestral spirits, and shamanic rituals performed by the Nakchong.

The Ubhauli festival — communities gathering for the Sakela dance, offerings, and the celebration of the planting season

Nwagi: Farewell Ritual and Ancestor Remembrance

Nwagi (न्वागी) or Nwakha is a Rai ritual performed to honor the dead and to facilitate the transition of the deceased's spirit to the ancestral realm. This ritual, typically performed several months or years after a death, involves the preparation of special foods, the gathering of family and community members, music and singing, and offerings made to the ancestral spirit. The Nwagi is both a mourning ritual (expressing grief and loss) and a celebration ritual (expressing joy in the deceased's life and honoring their memory). The performance of Nwagi is considered essential in traditional Rai belief: a properly performed Nwagi ensures that the ancestral spirit is satisfied and continues to protect the living family members.

The Nakchong: Shamanic Ritual and the Spirit World

The Nakchong (also called Bijuwa) is the Rai's traditional shaman — a ritual specialist who mediates between the human world and the spirit world, performs healing rituals, conducts ancestral ceremonies, and recites the Mundhum oral scripture. The Nakchong is not self-selected but is typically identified through signs of spiritual calling — often illness, vivid dreams, or unusual experiences — and trained through apprenticeship with an established practitioner. The Nakchong's ceremonial performance involves elaborate costumes, percussion rhythms, trance states, and extended recitation of Mundhum texts, and is one of the most visually and acoustically extraordinary aspects of Rai spiritual culture.

Dashai and Tihar: Hindu Festivals with Rai Adaptations

While the Rai have their own distinctive festivals, many Rai communities also observe Hindu festivals like Dashain (Vijaya Dashami) and Tihar (Diwali), though often with Rai-specific ritual practices and emphases that differ from Brahmin-Kshetri traditions. For some Rai communities, these Hindu festivals have become integrated into Rai religious practice over the centuries of contact with Hindu communities; for others, the observation of these festivals represents a more recent phenomenon related to Rai engagement with Nepali national identity.

Interesting Fact: The Rai's Sakela festival (Ubhauli/Udhauli) has in recent decades grown from a community-level celebration into a large-scale public cultural event held in Kathmandu, Darjeeling, and in Rai diaspora communities as far away as the United Kingdom and Hong Kong. In Kathmandu's Tundikhel ground, Sakela celebrations now attract tens of thousands of participants — making it one of the largest indigenous cultural festivals in Nepal — a remarkable example of how migration and urbanization have paradoxically strengthened certain aspects of Rai cultural identity.


Mystery: The Rai's traditional Nakchong shamanism bears structural and cosmological similarities to shamanic traditions found across a vast geographic arc from Siberia through Mongolia, Tibet, and into Southeast Asia. The Rai cosmological narrative of a spirit world structured by layers above and below the human realm, mediated by a specialist who can travel between them in trance, appears to be part of a very ancient stratum of Himalayan-Tibetan spiritual culture — but precisely how these traditions relate to one another, and whether they share a common origin or represent convergent development, remains a major open question in the anthropology of religion.

Traditional Dress

Men's Traditional Dress: Dhaka and Hill Innovations

Traditional Rai male dress typically includes a dhoti (the wrapped lower cloth used across South Asia), a kurta or shirt, and often a vest or jacket. The distinctive feature of Rai dress is the use of Dhaka fabric — the traditional handwoven textile with geometric patterns in multiple colors — for certain garments, particularly caps, scarves, and decorative elements. The Dhaka cloth connects Rai dress to broader Himalayan cloth traditions and represents a mark of cultural pride. Many Rai men, particularly in rural areas, continue to wear this traditional dress for daily wear, while in urban areas and among younger generations, Western clothing has largely replaced traditional dress in everyday contexts.

Traditional Rai attire — the Gunyo Cholo ensemble with its distinctive woven patterns, jewelry, and headwear marking regional and community identity

Women's Traditional Dress: Gunyo Cholo and Regional Variations

Traditional Rai women's dress typically includes a Gunyo (a wrapped skirt) and a Cholo (a short blouse), often with Rai-specific variations in color, weaving pattern, and ornamentation. The Gunyo Cholo ensemble is commonly red or in deep colors, often with distinctive woven patterns that indicate regional origin and community identity. Rai women also wear extensive jewelry — bangles, necklaces, ear ornaments, and finger rings — which are markers of status, prosperity, and cultural identity. In modern contexts, many Rai women have adopted the sari as formal dress while maintaining the Gunyo Cholo for festive occasions and cultural celebrations.

Headwear, Jewelry, and Adornment

Both Rai men and women traditionally wear distinctive headwear, often made from Dhaka fabric or other woven materials. The cap worn by men is similar to the Nepali Dhaka topi but may have Rai-specific variations in color and pattern. The elaborate jewelry worn by Rai women — particularly bangles, nose ornaments, and necklaces — are important markers of cultural identity and are often the most valuable possessions that a woman owns, representing family wealth and social status accumulated across generations.

Interesting Fact: The geometric patterns woven into traditional Rai Dhaka cloth are not merely decorative but encode information — different patterns are associated with different clans, different geographic regions, and different occasions. An experienced elder can identify the approximate geographic origin and clan affiliation of a weaving from the specific combination of geometric elements used, making traditional Rai textiles a form of cultural identification card woven in thread.


Mystery: The Rai's most elaborate ceremonial adornment — the full set of gold and bead jewelry assembled for weddings and major festivals — often incorporates specific amber, coral, and turquoise beads traded from Tibet and across the Himalayan trade routes. The presence of these high-value trans-Himalayan materials in Rai ceremonial jewelry suggests that even rural Rai communities maintained access to the long-distance Himalayan trade networks, but the specific mechanisms through which these goods reached Rai communities — whether through direct trade, marriage gifts, or intermediary merchants — are not well documented historically.

Unique Foods

Staple Grains and the Foundation of Rai Diet

The traditional Rai diet is based on staple grains that thrive in the hill ecology: rice (in lower-altitude regions), millet (finger millet, foxtail millet, and barnyard millet in middle altitudes), corn, and wheat. These grains are often ground into flour and made into various breads, porridges, and other preparations. Millet, in particular, is deeply embedded in Rai food culture and is used to make traditional dishes like Dhido (a thick grain porridge) and to ferment into traditional alcoholic beverages that play a central ceremonial role in Rai social life.

Kinema — the traditional Rai fermented soybean preparation, now sold commercially across Nepal. Its earthy, umami-rich flavor is central to Rai cuisine.

Kinema: The Rai Fermented Soybean Treasure

Kinema (किनेमा) is a traditional Rai and broader Himalayan fermented soybean preparation that has become increasingly popular across Nepal and is now sold commercially. Kinema is made by fermenting soybeans (or sometimes other beans) in a warm, moist environment for several days, allowing beneficial bacteria to break down the beans and create a distinctive, pungent, umami-rich flavor. The fermented beans can be eaten as a side dish, cooked into curries, or used as a flavoring ingredient. Kinema is extremely nutritious — providing complete protein and probiotics — and reflects the Rai tradition of using fermentation to both preserve foods and enhance their nutritional value.

Bamboo Shoots and Forest Foods

A distinctive aspect of Rai cuisine is the extensive use of bamboo shoots and other forest-gathered foods. The eastern Himalayan hills are covered in extensive bamboo forests, and young bamboo shoots (in season) are harvested and prepared in various ways — fresh, fermented, dried, and cooked into curries. Bamboo shoots are considered a delicacy and are featured prominently in Rai festival meals. Other forest foods that traditionally supplemented Rai agriculture include wild mushrooms, fiddlehead ferns, wild leafy greens, and various roots and tubers gathered from the forest.

Jand and the Rice Beer Tradition

The brewing and consumption of rice beer — variously called Jand, Jandu, or other local names — is a deeply embedded part of Rai cultural and social life. Traditional rice beer is made by fermenting rice or millet in a controlled process that produces a mildly alcoholic beverage (typically 4–8% alcohol content) with a slightly sweet taste. The brewing of rice beer is traditionally a women's skill, and the ability to make high-quality beer is a matter of family pride. Rice beer is consumed at social gatherings, festivals, and ceremonial occasions, and plays an important role in community bonding and ritual practice. The sharing of rice beer with guests is considered a sign of hospitality and welcome.

Meat, Protein, and Festive Preparations

Although Rai communities engage in agriculture as their primary occupation, they have traditionally supplemented their diet with hunting and raising of animals. Chicken, goat, and pork are eaten in Rai communities (unlike some Hindu communities that avoid pork), and these meats are featured in festive meals and ceremonial feasts. Fish from the rivers and streams of the eastern hills is also an important protein source. Special festival foods are prepared for Ubhauli and other celebrations — elaborate curries made with meat, fish, or vegetables, specially prepared rice dishes, and sweets made from rice flour, jaggery, and sometimes ghee. The preparation of festival foods is a community activity, with women of multiple families gathering together to prepare large quantities of food for celebrations that may last several days.

Interesting Fact: Kinema — the Rai fermented soybean preparation — is now recognized by food scientists as one of the richest natural sources of certain probiotic bacteria found in Himalayan traditional foods. Recent nutritional research has found that traditionally prepared Kinema contains bacterial strains beneficial to gut health that are distinct from those found in Japanese natto (a similar fermented soybean food) — suggesting that Himalayan and East Asian fermentation traditions, while similar in technique, developed independently and produced different microbial cultures.


Mystery: The specific recipes for Rai festival foods — particularly the elaborate curries prepared for Nwagi (ancestor feast) and Ubhauli celebrations — vary significantly from village to village and clan to clan, with specific spice combinations, preparation methods, and ingredient ratios considered the 'correct' ancestral way of making each dish. Who originally established these specific culinary traditions, and whether the differences between village food traditions reflect ancient clan-level distinctions or simply pragmatic local adaptation to available ingredients, is an unrecorded aspect of Rai cultural history.

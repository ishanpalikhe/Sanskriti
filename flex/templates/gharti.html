History and Origin

The Name, The Hills, and Their Ancestral Legacy

The Gharti — also known as Bhujel, and in census records as Gharti/Bhujel — are a Tibeto-Burman ethnic group native to the mid-hill districts of Nepal, particularly concentrated in Tanahu and Syangja districts [citation:9]. According to recent estimates, the Gharti population in Nepal numbers approximately 136,000, with a global population of around 163,000 people [citation:3][citation:7]. The name "Bhujel" is believed to derive from the Bhuji Khola river, near which the community historically settled [citation:9]. The Gharti are divided into four traditional subcastes: Bhujyal, Gharti, Nisel, and Khawas [citation:9].

The Gharti people have a complex and contested history. According to some sources, they are considered an offshoot of the Indian Chettri who, due to poverty, served royal families and nobles as bonded laborers, palanquin-bearers, and water-carriers [citation:1]. However, academic scholarship suggests that the term "Gharti" was used in a broader sense to denote people of "notorious" origin, including those born of hypogamous unions between higher castes and members of lower categories [citation:5]. An origin myth of the Tarangpur clans describes the Gharti descending from the marriage between a Bhotia woman and a Thakuri man who killed a cow and served its meat at his wedding — a union attended by enough cumulative degradation (in terms of caste) to confer notoriety on its issue [citation:5].

Traditional Occupations: From Palanquin-Bearers to Farmers

Traditionally, Gharti men worked as palanquin-bearers (carrying enclosed litters for nobles and royal families) and Gharti women worked as water-carriers [citation:1][citation:7]. This occupation placed them in close service to the upper castes of Nepali society, reflecting their historically marginalized status. Today, however, most Gharti people labor as farmers on terraced agricultural land in the mid-hills, cultivating rice, maize, millet, and vegetables [citation:1]. The shift from service-based occupations to independent farming represents a significant transformation in Gharti economic life over the past century.

Interesting Fact: The Gharti are one of the Tibeto-Burman ethnic groups of Nepal who speak Nepali as their primary language — a linguistic shift that reflects generations of integration with the dominant Khas-speaking population while maintaining a distinct ethnic identity. Only about 16,000 people speak the Bhujel language (a Tibeto-Burman language) as a secondary language [citation:1].


Mystery: The exact relationship between the Gharti and the Bhujel remains ambiguous — the terms are used interchangeably in some contexts, while in others Bhujel is considered a subcaste of Gharti. The 2021 Nepal census classifies them together as "Gharti/Bhujel" within the Mountain/Hill Janajati social group, reflecting the unresolved nature of this classification [citation:9].

Section Two

Language

Nepali as Primary Language, Bhujel as Heritage

The primary language spoken by the Gharti people is Nepali, the national language of Nepal, with approximately 99,000 Gharti speakers using Nepali as their mother tongue [citation:1]. However, the Gharti also have a heritage language — Bhujel (also known as Bujhel or Gharti Bhasa) — which is a Tibeto-Burman language spoken by approximately 16,000 people [citation:1]. The Bhujel language is distinct from Nepali and reflects the community's pre-Indo-Aryan origins before centuries of language shift.

Multilingualism and Language Endangerment

The Gharti community is notably multilingual. In addition to Nepali and Bhujel, many Gharti speakers also use Bantawa (1,100 speakers), Maithili (500 speakers), Thulung (500 speakers), Bhojpuri (400 speakers), Chamling (200 speakers), Khaling (200 speakers), Dotyali (100 speakers), and even Raute (40 speakers) — a remarkable linguistic diversity that reflects the Gharti's integration into the complex ethnic mosaic of eastern and central Nepal [citation:1]. However, the Bhujel language is considered endangered, as younger generations increasingly use only Nepali in daily life. The language is written in the Devanagari script, and there are scripture portions available in Bhujel, but active intergenerational transmission is declining [citation:3].

The old language of the Gharti people that they used before Nepali.

Interesting Fact: The Gharti people have maintained their distinct ethnic identity despite almost complete language shift to Nepali — a rare phenomenon in ethnolinguistics, where ethnic groups typically lose their identity when they lose their language. This suggests that other markers of identity (such as clan membership, ritual practices, and occupational history) have been powerful enough to sustain Gharti distinctiveness across generations.


Mystery: The linguistic relationship between Bhujel and other Tibeto-Burman languages of Nepal remains understudied. Some linguists classify it within the Kiranti group, while others suggest it may form its own branch. The limited documentation of the language makes definitive classification difficult.

Section Three

Music and Dance

Folk Traditions of the Mid-Hills

The musical and dance traditions of the Gharti people reflect their integration into the broader cultural landscape of Nepal's mid-hills while maintaining distinctive local variations. Like other Janajati (indigenous nationalities) groups of the region, Gharti musical culture includes the use of traditional Nepali folk instruments such as the madal (a hand drum), the dholak (a barrel drum), and the jhyamta (cymbals). Folk songs are typically performed during festivals, agricultural celebrations (such as harvest time), and life-cycle rituals including weddings and birth ceremonies.

Dance as Community Expression

Traditional Gharti dances are performed in circular formations, with men and women dancing separately or in alternating patterns. The dances often mimic agricultural activities — the planting of rice, the harvesting of crops, and the grinding of grain — connecting movement to the subsistence farming that sustains the community. The rhythmic patterns of the madal guide the dancers' steps, with faster tempos for celebratory occasions and slower, more deliberate rhythms for ritual contexts. While the Gharti do not have a distinct, uniquely identifiable dance form like the Newar's Devi Nritya, their participation in the broader Nepali folk dance tradition (including the popular Dhan Naach, or rice planting dance) serves as an expression of their integration into Nepal's multi-ethnic cultural fabric.

The Gharti's dancing at the traditional tune of madal, dholak and jhyamta showing Naumati song.

Interesting Fact: The Gharti community, like many mid-hill Janajati groups, maintains a tradition of "work songs" — rhythmic vocalizations sung while performing agricultural labor. These songs coordinate group activity, relieve the monotony of repetitive tasks, and encode practical knowledge about seasonal cycles and farming techniques.


Mystery: Little formal documentation exists of specifically Gharti musical traditions, as most ethnographic studies have focused on larger groups like the Gurung or Magar. Whether the Gharti possess unique musical forms or have fully assimilated to the dominant Nepali folk tradition remains an open question requiring further fieldwork.

Section Four

Festivals and Culture

Hindu Rituals and Deity Worship

The Gharti people are predominantly Hindu, with approximately 99% of the population adhering to Hinduism [citation:6]. They worship a range of Hindu deities including Kali (the goddess of power and destruction), Lakshmi (the goddess of wealth and prosperity), and Kanchyaga (a local deity of specific significance to the Gharti tradition) [citation:1][citation:7]. The services of a Brahmin priest are sought for performing birth, marriage, and death rituals, reflecting the community's integration into the Hindu ritual hierarchy despite their historically marginalized status [citation:1].

Life-Cycle Rituals and Brahminical Influence

Key life-cycle events among the Gharti include birth purification ceremonies (jatakarma), naming ceremonies (nwaran), rice-feeding ceremonies (pasni), and the sacred thread ceremony (bartabandha) for boys — though the latter is less universally observed than among the twice-born Tagadhari castes. Marriage is arranged through negotiation between families, with the exchange of vermilion powder (sindur) and a bead necklace (mangalsutra) serving as the key rituals marking a woman's transition to married status [citation:1]. Death rituals follow Hindu patterns, including cremation (for most adults) and ancestral offerings (shraddha) performed by the eldest son.

The traditional culture of the Gharti people being performed.

Interesting Fact: The Gharti worship a deity called Kanchyaga — a figure not found in the standard Hindu pantheon — suggesting the persistence of pre-Hindu, Tibeto-Burman religious elements within their ostensibly Hindu practice. This syncretism is typical of many Janajati groups who adopted Hinduism while retaining indigenous spiritual beliefs.


Mystery: The historical process by which the Gharti, who are of Tibeto-Burman origin, came to adopt Hinduism so completely while maintaining a distinct ethnic identity remains underexplored in scholarly literature. The role of the 1854 Muluki Ain (Nepal's national legal code) in codifying caste hierarchies and incentivizing Sanskritization may have been a factor, but specific evidence for the Gharti case is lacking.

Section Five

Traditional Dress

Simple Attire of the Mid-Hill Farmer

The traditional dress of the Gharti people reflects their status as a historically marginalized, laboring community of the mid-hills. Gharti men traditionally wear a dhoti (a rectangular cloth wrapped around the waist and passed between the legs) or a lungi, paired with a simple bhoto (waistcoat) or a cotton shirt. A topi (Nepali cap) — often the national Dhaka topi — is worn on formal occasions. For agricultural work, men wear minimal clothing to allow freedom of movement in the terraced fields.

Gharti women traditionally wear a cotton sari — typically in bright colors (red, green, or blue) with simple borders — draped in the style common to mid-hill Nepali women. The blouse (choli) is typically short-sleeved. The most distinctive markers of married Gharti women are vermilion (sindur) applied in the parting of the hair and a bead necklace (mangalsutra) [citation:1]. Women also wear glass bangles, silver or gold earrings, and nose rings (bulaki) as traditional adornments. Today, many Gharti, especially younger generations and those who have migrated to urban areas, have adopted Western-style clothing (t-shirts, jeans, and dresses), leading to the gradual decline of traditional attire.

A group of Ghati men and women in traditional attire.

Interesting Fact: The vermilion (sindur) and bead necklace markers of married Gharti women are identical to those used by upper-caste Hindu women — reflecting the community's adoption of Hindu symbols of marriage even while remaining socially distinct from the Tagadhari (twice-born) castes.


Mystery: Unlike many other Janajati groups (such as the Gurung or Magar, who have distinctive ethnic costumes), the Gharti lack a uniquely identifiable traditional dress. Whether this reflects historical assimilation to Khas-Nepali dress patterns or the loss of earlier distinctive clothing traditions is unknown.

Section Six

Unique Foods

Subsistence Agriculture and Traditional Cuisine

Gharti cuisine is typical of Nepal's mid-hill farming communities, based on the crops they cultivate on terraced fields. The staple meal is dal bhat tarkari — rice (bhat) served with lentil soup (dal) and vegetable curry (tarkari) — typically eaten twice daily. The lentils used include mas ko dal (black lentil), mung ko dal (mung bean), and rahara ko dal (pigeon pea). Vegetables are seasonal and locally grown: leafy greens (saag), potatoes (alu), cauliflower (kauli), pumpkin (pharsi), and beans (simi).

Maize, Millet, and Fermented Foods

In addition to rice, Gharti farmers cultivate maize (makaï) and millet (kodo) on rain-fed upland terraces. Maize is consumed roasted, boiled, or ground into flour for dhido — a thick porridge that serves as a staple alternative to rice, particularly for poorer households. Millet is primarily used to produce jand (raksi) — a traditional fermented alcoholic beverage that plays a central role in Gharti social and ritual life. Jand is offered to deities during worship, served to guests as a sign of hospitality, and consumed during festivals and celebrations. The preparation of marcha (the fermented starter cake containing wild yeasts and herbs) is typically the knowledge of elder women in the community.

The unique food of the Ghartis called dhido prepared from maize and has enough softness.

Interesting Fact: The Gharti, like other mid-hill communities, prepare gundruk — a fermented leafy green vegetable that is dried and stored for months, providing a source of nutrients during the winter when fresh vegetables are scarce. Gundruk is prepared as a tangy soup or pickle and is considered a delicacy rather than a food of necessity.


Mystery: The Gharti community's traditional foodways remain almost entirely undocumented in ethnographic literature. Whether the community possesses unique cooking techniques, recipes, or food-related rituals that distinguish them from neighboring ethnic groups is unknown and represents a significant gap in the documentation of Nepal's culinary heritage.

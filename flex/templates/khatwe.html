History and Origin

The Name, The Terai, and Their Ancestral Heritage

The Khatwe (also spelled Khatwe) are a Madhesi Dalit community native to the southeastern Terai plains of Nepal, particularly concentrated in districts such as Saptari, Siraha, and other parts of Madhesh Province [citation:3]. According to the National Dalit Commission of Nepal, the Khatwe are recognized as one of the 26 distinct Dalit sub-castes in the country, belonging specifically to the Madhesi Dalit category — communities that have historically resided in the southern Terai region and faced systematic social marginalization under the traditional Hindu caste hierarchy [citation:3].

The Khatwe surname is quite common in Nepal, where it is found as a widespread family name, with additional populations in India, Malaysia, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia [citation:4]. In the 1996 Nepal Family Health Survey, the Khatwe caste was assigned code number 25 in the official caste classification system, placing them within the broader enumeration of Madhesi communities alongside groups such as Chamar, Dushad, and Musahar [citation:2]. This classification reflects the Khatwe's historical position within the complex social stratification of the Nepali Terai.

The Khatwe community has a documented presence in specific villages of southeastern Nepal. For example, in Didhawa (now part of Rajbiraj Municipality Ward No. 10 in Saptari District), the Khatwe caste is found in significant numbers alongside other Maithili-speaking communities including Yadav, Chamar, Musahar, Bania, Barai, Hajam, Haluwai, Teli, and Natt Muslims [citation:5]. At the time of the 2011 Nepal census, Didhawa had a total population of 4,249 residents, with Khatwe families forming an integral part of this diverse Maithili-speaking settlement [citation:5].

Traditional Occupation: Agricultural Labor

Traditionally, the Khatwe people have been primarily engaged as agricultural laborers, working the fertile fields of the Terai plains [citation:1]. As a landless or land-poor community, Khatwe families typically work as sharecroppers or daily-wage laborers on lands owned by higher-caste farmers. Their diet consists mainly of maize, rice, and wheat — the staple grains they help cultivate [citation:1]. This economic dependence on agricultural labor has historically reinforced their marginalized social position, as lack of land ownership limited their economic mobility and social standing within the village hierarchy.

Interesting Fact: The Khatwe are recognized as one of the 26 Dalit sub-castes by the National Dalit Commission of Nepal, placing them within the official framework of historically marginalized communities entitled to affirmative action protections and targeted development programs under Nepali law [citation:3].


Mystery: The specific etymology of the name "Khatwe" remains uncertain. Like many Dalit community names in South Asia, it may derive from a traditional occupation — possibly related to weaving, leatherwork, or another artisanal craft — but the occupational origin of the Khatwe name has not been definitively established in the available historical or ethnographic literature.

Language

Maithili: The Primary Language of the Khatwe

The primary language spoken by the Khatwe people is Maithili, an Indo-Aryan language with a rich literary tradition dating back to the 14th century, spoken by millions across the Madhesh region of Nepal and the neighboring Indian state of Bihar [citation:1]. Approximately 97,000 people speak Maithili as their primary language within the Khatwe community, reflecting the community's deep integration into the broader Maithili cultural sphere of southeastern Nepal [citation:1]. Maithili is recognized as a constitutional language of Nepal and is written in the Devanagari script, with a robust literary heritage including poetry, folk songs, and religious texts.

Multilingualism and Secondary Languages

The Khatwe community demonstrates remarkable multilingualism, with members speaking a range of additional languages that reflect the diverse linguistic environment of the Madhesh Terai. Secondary languages spoken by Khatwe include Nepali (2,200 speakers), the national language of Nepal; Bhojpuri (1,300 speakers), a widely spoken language of the western and central Terai; Magahi (1,300 speakers), another major language of the Bihar-Nepal border region; Angika (300 speakers), a language closely related to Maithili; Awadhi (200 speakers), spoken in the far-western Terai; and even Bantawa (30 speakers), a Kiranti language of the eastern hills [citation:1]. This linguistic diversity testifies to the Khatwe's position within the complex, multi-ethnic tapestry of the Madhesh region.

Maithili: The native language of the Khatwe people.

Interesting Fact: The Maithili language has a written literary tradition that dates back over 700 years, with the 14th-century poet Vidyapati being the most celebrated figure in Maithili literature. The Khatwe community's primary language thus connects them to one of South Asia's oldest and most distinguished regional literary traditions.


Mystery: While approximately 97,000 Khatwe speak Maithili as their primary language, the total Khatwe population may be larger, with some members reporting Nepali or Bhojpuri as their mother tongue. The precise demographic distribution of the Khatwe community across language boundaries remains incompletely documented.

Music and Dance

Maithili Folk Traditions of the Terai

The musical and dance traditions of the Khatwe people are part of the broader Maithili cultural heritage of the Madhesh Terai. Traditional Maithili folk music includes genres such as Jhumar, a lively dance-song form performed during festivals and celebrations; Vidyapati geet, devotional songs based on the poetry of the medieval Maithili saint-poet Vidyapati; and Sama Chakeva songs, performed during the unique bird-worshipping festival of the same name. These musical traditions are accompanied by traditional Terai instruments including the dholak (a double-headed barrel drum), the nagara (kettledrum), the shehnai (a wind instrument), and the manjira (small hand cymbals).

Dance as Community Expression

Traditional Khatwe dances, like those of other Maithili-speaking communities, are performed in circular formations with men and women dancing separately or in alternating patterns. The Jhumar dance is particularly associated with the autumn harvest season and wedding celebrations, with dancers moving in a slow, graceful circle while singing songs that often tell stories of love, separation, and the beauty of the Terai landscape. During the Sama Chakeva festival, young women perform dances dedicated to the Sama bird (a type of cuckoo), celebrating the bond between brothers and sisters. These dance traditions serve both as entertainment and as a means of preserving and transmitting Maithili cultural identity across generations.

The Khatwe people performing Jhumar dance to associate with the autumn harvest season.

Interesting Fact: The Sama Chakeva festival, which is observed by Maithili-speaking communities including the Khatwe, involves the creation of clay idols of birds by young women, accompanied by songs and dances. The festival celebrates the legendary love story of Sama (daughter of Lord Krishna) and Chakeva, and is unique to the Mithila region of Nepal and India.


Mystery: Whether the Khatwe community maintains distinct musical or dance traditions that differentiate them from other Maithili-speaking groups (such as the Yadav, Teli, or Barai) is not documented in available ethnographic sources. The degree of cultural distinctiveness within the broader Maithili sphere remains an area requiring further research.

Section Four

Festivals and Culture

Hindu Religious Practice

The Khatwe people practice Hinduism, the ancient religion of the Indian subcontinent [citation:1]. At the popular level, Khatwe Hindus worship and serve the gods of the Hindu pantheon, visiting Hindu temples and offering prayers, food, flowers, and incense to their deities in hopes of gaining protection and benefits [citation:1]. The Hindu religious calendar — with its cycle of festivals, fasts, and observances — structures the ritual year for the Khatwe community, as it does for other Hindu communities of the Madhesh Terai.

Major Festivals of the Khatwe Calendar

The Khatwe observe the major festivals of the Maithili Hindu calendar, including Chhath (the sun-worshipping festival celebrated with great devotion on the banks of the Koshi and other Terai rivers), Dashain (the great autumn festival of Durga worship), Tihar (the festival of lights dedicated to Lakshmi), Holi (the spring festival of colors), and Sama Chakeva (the unique bird-worshipping festival of the Mithila region). The Chhath festival, which involves ritual bathing, fasting, and offerings to the setting and rising sun, is particularly significant for Maithili-speaking communities of the Terai and is observed with great devotion by Khatwe families.

Marriage and Family Structure

Family leaders arrange marriages within the Khatwe community, following traditional Maithili customs [citation:1]. The Khatwe practice monogamous marriage — meaning that marriage is between one man and one woman — and divorce is not traditionally permitted, though the bereaved may remarry after the death of a spouse [citation:1]. Sons inherit property upon the death of the father, with the oldest son taking charge of the household [citation:1]. This patrilineal, patrilocal family structure is typical of Terai Hindu communities, with the extended family often residing together in a single household compound.

The women celebrating the Chhath festival by dipping themselves into the holy water.

Interesting Fact: The Chhath festival, which is among the most important festivals for Maithili-speaking communities including the Khatwe, involves four days of rituals including holy bathing, fasting, and offering arghya (offerings of water and milk) to the setting and rising sun. The festival is unique to the Mithila region and the neighboring Terai areas of Nepal and India.


Mystery: The Khatwe, like other Madhesi Dalit communities, have historically been subject to caste-based discrimination and untouchability practices by higher-caste groups. While such practices are now illegal under Nepali law, their persistence in social practice and the mechanisms by which Khatwe families navigate these social barriers remain under-documented in the scholarly literature.

Section Five

Traditional Dress

Terai Attire of the Khatwe Community

The traditional dress of the Khatwe people reflects their position within the broader Maithili cultural sphere of the Madhesh Terai. Khatwe men traditionally wear a dhoti — a rectangular cloth wrapped around the waist and passed between the legs — paired with a kurta (a long, loose shirt) or a simple cotton vest. A gamcha (a cotton towel or scarf) is often worn on the shoulder or head, used for wiping sweat during agricultural labor. For formal occasions and festivals, men may wear a topi (cap) and a waistcoat.

Khatwe women traditionally wear a cotton sari — typically in bright colors such as red, green, blue, or yellow — draped in the Maithili style, with the pallu (the decorative end of the sari) worn over the right shoulder. The blouse (choli) is typically short-sleeved. Married women traditionally wear vermilion (sindur) in the parting of their hair and a bead necklace (mangalsutra) as markers of their married status. Gold and silver jewelry — including earrings (kundal), nose rings (nath or bulaki), and bangles (chura) — is worn on festive occasions, with the quantity and quality of jewelry reflecting the family's economic status.

The traditional dress dhoti, kurta and gamcha worn by the males of Khatwe community.

Interesting Fact: The Maithili draping style of the sari differs from the styles common in other parts of Nepal and India, with specific variations in how the pallu is pleated and over which shoulder it is worn. These regional differences in sari draping serve as subtle markers of cultural identity within the diverse landscape of South Asian dress.


Mystery: Whether the Khatwe community historically possessed distinctive dress elements (such as specific colors, patterns, or ornaments) that differentiated them from other Maithili-speaking groups is not documented. Like many marginalized communities, the Khatwe may have adopted the dress patterns of the dominant regional culture, possibly erasing earlier distinctive traditions.

VI
Section Six

Unique Foods

Staple Grains of the Terai

Khatwe cuisine is based on the staple grains they cultivate as agricultural laborers — primarily maize (makaï), rice (dhan), and wheat (gahum) [citation:1]. The typical daily meal consists of rice (bhat) served with lentil soup (dal) and a vegetable curry (tarkari), often accompanied by a spicy pickle (achar) made from mango, lemon, or chili. Maize is consumed roasted, boiled, or ground into flour for flatbreads. Wheat flour is used to make roti (unleavened flatbread) and purï (deep-fried bread).

Maithili Culinary Traditions

The Khatwe are not vegetarians, and their diet includes fish, goat meat, and chicken, particularly on festive occasions [citation:1]. Given the Khatwe's location in the Terai — a region of rivers, ponds, and wetlands — fish plays a significant role in the diet. Machh ka jhol (fish curry) is prepared with mustard oil, turmeric, ginger, garlic, and a blend of ground spices. During festivals, elaborate sweets are prepared, including peda (sweetened condensed milk patties), khaja (a flaky fried sweet), and thekua (a deep-fried wheat flour cookie flavored with cardamom and jaggery). These sweets are offered to deities and shared among community members as part of festival celebrations. For special occasions, jand (fermented rice beer) or raksi (distilled alcoholic beverage) may be consumed as part of social and ritual hospitality.

The unique food of Khatwe's: The Fish Curry with different and unique seasonings.

Interesting Fact: Maithili cuisine includes a unique preparation called dhuska — a deep-fried flatbread made from fermented rice and lentil batter, served with spicy potato curry or yogurt. Dhuska is considered a festive food and is prepared especially during the Chhath festival and other major celebrations.


Mystery: The specific food traditions of the Khatwe community — whether they possess distinctive recipes, cooking techniques, or food-related rituals that differentiate them from other Maithili-speaking groups — remain almost entirely undocumented in available sources. This gap in ethnographic knowledge represents a significant lacuna in the documentation of Nepal's culinary diversity.

History & Origins

Who Are the Mizar? Identity Within the Sarki Community

The Mizar (also spelled Mijar) are a sub-clan of the broader Sarki community — one of the most historically significant occupational caste groups of Nepal's hill regions. The term "Sarki" broadly identifies the hereditary leather-working and cobbling community of the Himalayan hills, but within this larger identity exist several distinct sub-clans defined by regional settlement, lineage, and subtle variations in craft specialization. The Mizar sub-caste is one of these internally recognized groups, alongside other sub-clans such as Bayalkoti, Bogati, Ramtel, Surkheti, Bisunkhe, and Roka — all falling under the wider umbrella of the Sarki Hill Dalit community. The National Museum of Nepal formally records the community as "Sarki/Mijar," acknowledging the equivalence and close connection between the two names.

The Sarki people — and by extension the Mizar — are found throughout the hills and mountains of Nepal, from Ilam in the east to Kanchanpur in the far west, as well as across the hills of Darjeeling and Kalimpong in India and in the Terai-bordering Dooars region. Their presence across this vast arc of Himalayan hill territory reflects centuries of settlement, trade, and service-based relationships with the surrounding Khas, Gurung, Magar, and Tamang communities whom they traditionally served as cobblers and leatherworkers.

A Sarki/Mizar craftsman working with traditional leather tools — the centuries-old craft of cobbling that defines the community's identity and historical role in Himalayan society.

The Caste Codification: Muluki Ain and the Malla Legacy

The formal categorization of the leather-working Sarki/Mizar community into a hereditary occupational caste has its roots in the reign of King Jayasthiti Malla, who in approximately 1422 CE reorganized Nepalese society by assigning distinct occupational castes to different groups of people. Under this system, those who worked with leather — processing hides, tanning skins, and crafting footwear — were designated as a specific community defined by this work. This was formalized even more rigidly in the Muluki Ain of 1854, the great legal code of Prime Minister Jung Bahadur Rana, which codified Nepal's social hierarchy in exhaustive legal detail. The Sarki/Mizar were classified as pani nachalne — "those from whom water cannot be accepted" — placing them in the lowest rung of the hill Hindu caste hierarchy alongside the Kami (blacksmiths) and Damai (tailors and musicians).

This classification as "untouchables" (achut) brought centuries of institutionalized social exclusion: the Mizar could not enter the homes of higher-caste families, could not use public water sources shared by upper castes, and were restricted to their own settlement quarters (tole) at the periphery of villages. Despite this, the Mizar's skills were indispensable — no Himalayan community could function without footwear, leather straps for carrying loads, saddles for pack animals, or the dozens of other leather goods that formed the backbone of hill life. This paradox — essential yet excluded — defines the historical experience of the Mizar and the wider Sarki community.

Abolition of Caste Discrimination and the Modern Era

The government of Nepal legally abolished the caste system and criminalized caste-based discrimination, including untouchability, in 1963. With Nepal's eventual transformation into a secular federal republic in 2008, the legal framework of caste oppression was dismantled. The Mizar/Sarki are today officially classified as Hill Dalit — one of the nine broad social groups recognized by the Government of Nepal — and are legally entitled to the same rights, opportunities, and reservations as all other citizens. The Civil Service Act of 1993 introduced a reservation system allocating nine percent of civil service vacancies to Dalit groups. Despite these legal changes, social attitudes in rural areas have been slow to transform, and many Mizar communities continue to face informal discrimination in housing, education, and social interaction.

Interesting Fact: The very name "Sarki" is believed by some scholars to derive from the Sanskrit word charmakar — meaning "one who works with leather" (from charma, skin, and kara, maker). The shift from charmakar to sarki reflects centuries of linguistic evolution through Prakrit dialects. The sub-name "Mizar/Mijar" is thought by community elders to refer to a specific ancestral lineage or geographic origin, though no single definitive etymology has been established in academic literature.


Mystery: Despite the Sarki/Mizar community's presence across the entire length of Nepal's hill belt, the precise mechanism by which different sub-clan names — Mizar, Bogati, Bayalkoti, Ramtel and others — emerged and became attached to specific lineages is not fully documented. Whether these names reflect ancient clan divisions that predate the Malla caste codification, or whether they emerged as geographic and occupational identifiers after the 1422 CE reorganization, remains an open question in Nepalese social history.

Language & Identity

Language: Nepali as the Mother Tongue of the Hills

Unlike many of Nepal's indigenous communities who maintained distinct mother tongues through centuries of isolation, the Sarki/Mizar community adopted Nepali (Khas-Kura) as their primary language, which reflects their deep integration into the hill Khas social world from which the broader Parbatiya culture emerged. The Sarki are explicitly mentioned alongside the Kami and Damai as having migrated eastward across the hills of Nepal in the company of Khas Brahmin and Chhetri communities, forming an inseparable part of the Parbatiya social structure as its occupational service providers. In some areas, particularly in Thakali-dominated regions, the Sarki community also speaks Thakali as a second or working language. In the Darjeeling and Kalimpong hills of India where Sarki/Mizar communities settled, variants of hill Nepali dialects are spoken.

The Mizar community does not possess a separate written literary tradition, as the historical denial of education to the Dalit community under Nepal's caste hierarchy prevented the formation of a literate class within these communities for centuries. Oral tradition — through song, folk story (lok katha), proverb, and ritual verse — has therefore served as the primary medium of cultural transmission for the Mizar. The community's oral literature, while not systematically collected or documented, encodes knowledge of traditional crafts, the community's historical experiences of discrimination and resilience, and the narratives of community deities and ancestral figures.

Names, Identity, and Caste Mobility

The names and surnames of the Mizar/Sarki community reflect their hill Khas cultural environment. Common surnames include clan-linked names such as Mizar, Mijar, Sarki, Bogati, and Ramtel, which function as both caste identifiers and clan names. In recent decades, as caste-based discrimination has been legally abolished and social mobility has become more possible, some members of the Sarki/Mizar community have begun adopting surnames less overtly tied to their caste identity — a process of social repositioning that mirrors what scholars have observed across other Dalit communities in the Subcontinent and which in Nepal is sometimes compared to the broader process of Sanskritization.

"The Sarkis are also very rich in their culture and traditions. As the society is changing and becoming more complex day by day, Sarkis are facing big challenges to protect their social customs and traditions and existence of themselves." — Academic Research, Tribhuvan University Library

Geographic Distribution: From Ilam to Kanchanpur

The Sarki/Mizar community is scattered across virtually the entire hill and mountain belt of Nepal — from the eastern district of Ilam all the way to Kanchanpur in the far west. According to the 2021 Nepal census, the total Sarki population stands at 452,229 persons, constituting 1.55% of Nepal's total population. The community's density is highest in the western and mid-western hill regions: Gorkha (7.6%), Jumla (7.1%), Parbat (6.7%), Arghakhanchi (6.1%), and Baglung (5.6%) show the highest concentrations of Sarki by district. In Gandaki Province as a whole, the Sarki make up 4.1% of the provincial population — reflecting the community's particularly strong historical presence in the mid-western hills.

Interesting Fact: The Sarki/Mizar community's wide geographic distribution across the entire hill belt of Nepal — unlike many Janajati communities whose settlements are concentrated in specific ecological zones — reflects the service-based nature of their traditional occupation. Because every village needed a cobbler and leatherworker, the Sarki settled wherever Khas communities settled, creating a pan-hill presence that no other single occupational caste matched. This widespread distribution means that the Sarki/Mizar community has absorbed enormous regional cultural diversity while maintaining a core occupational identity.


Mystery: The 2021 census counted 452,229 Sarki — a significant increase from 374,816 in 2011, representing growth of over 77,000 persons in a decade. Whether this growth reflects genuine demographic increase, improved census coverage of previously undercounted Dalit communities, or a combination of both factors is an open question. Some community advocates argue that Sarki/Dalit populations have historically been systematically undercounted in Nepal's census operations due to the reluctance of enumerators to enter Dalit settlement areas.

Music & Dance

The Madal: Heartbeat of the Sarki/Mizar Community

The defining musical instrument of the Sarki/Mizar community is the Madal — a double-headed hand drum of medium size, fashioned from a hollowed wooden or clay cylinder with goat-skin or buffalo-skin membranes stretched across both ends and tuned with leather lacing. The Madal is the primary folk percussion instrument of Nepal's hill region, used across communities, but its craft production — stretching and tanning the leather drum-heads, cutting and lacing the tuning straps — is specifically a Sarki/Mizar craft tradition. The community's expertise in working with animal hides thus extended naturally into the creation of musical instruments, linking the Mizar's leather craft identity to their musical identity in a single material tradition.

The Madal — Nepal's most beloved folk drum, whose leather drum-heads are crafted by the Sarki/Mizar community whose leatherworking skills make this instrument possible.

Khayali Marooni: The Community's Signature Dance

The most distinctively Sarki/Mizar folk performance tradition is the Khayali Marooni — a group dance performed by multiple participants together, combining rhythmic footwork, call-and-response singing, and the collective energy of community celebration. The Khayali Marooni is performed at festivals, weddings, and community gatherings, and its vigorous, participatory character reflects the Sarki/Mizar community's tradition of collective celebration despite — and perhaps in response to — their historical marginalization. The dance creates a social space of joy, belonging, and collective identity that cuts through the hierarchies of the surrounding caste world.

Thali Nach and Sarange Nach: Other Dance Forms

Beyond the Khayali Marooni, the Sarki/Mizar community maintains two other significant dance traditions: the Thali Nach — a dance performed with brass plates (thali) used as percussive props, struck rhythmically as the dancers move in patterns — and the Sarange Nach, a dance performed to the accompaniment of the sarangi, a small four-stringed bowed instrument historically associated with the Gandarbha/Gaine community of wandering musicians but also played in some Sarki/Mizar cultural contexts. These dance forms represent a rich performative tradition that expresses community identity, seasonal celebration, and ceremonial participation.

Interesting Fact: The Sarki/Mizar community's deep connection to the Madal drum extends beyond playing — they are historically responsible for the craft of making the drum itself, particularly its leather components. A well-made Madal requires precisely tanned leather of exactly the right thickness and suppleness for the drum-head, laced with leather cords cut and finished to specific dimensions. This means that every time a Nepali musician plays a Madal anywhere in the hill belt, they are playing an instrument whose existence depends on the Sarki/Mizar leather tradition — a connection that is rarely acknowledged in mainstream narratives of Nepali music.


Mystery: The origins of the Khayali Marooni dance — its name, its choreographic logic, and the specific occasions on which it was traditionally performed — have not been systematically documented by ethnomusicologists or cultural historians. The dance exists primarily as living community practice, transmitted from generation to generation through participation rather than written record. Whether the dance has ancient roots predating the Malla-era caste codification, or whether it developed as a community response to social exclusion during the centuries of formal untouchability, is an open and important question in Nepali intangible cultural heritage studies.

Section Four

Festivals & Culture

Religious Life: A Blend of Shamanism and Hinduism

The religious life of the Mizar/Sarki community reflects the broader spiritual landscape of Nepal's hill communities — a layered blending of formal Hinduism with deep and older shamanic and animist traditions. Officially, approximately 95.46% of the Sarki community identified as Hindu in the 2011 census, with 1.7% following Buddhism and 2.84% Christian. However, in practice, the community's actual religious life is far more complex. The Mizar/Sarki maintain a rich tradition of shamanic healing and ritual through the jhankri (shaman) or lama — a community ritual specialist who performs healing ceremonies, conducts communication with spirits and ancestors, and manages the community's relationship with the unseen world.

A jhankri (shaman) of the Nepal hills — the ritual specialist who forms the spiritual backbone of Sarki/Mizar community life, blending animist and Hindu traditions in healing and ceremony.

Community Deities: Santala Devi, Maidevta, and Dat Kali

The Mizar/Sarki community maintains its own specific set of tutelary deities alongside the broader Hindu pantheon. Their primary community deities include Santala Devi — a protective goddess who watches over the community and its members — Maidevta, a village deity associated with the community's locality and agricultural wellbeing, and Dat Kali, a fierce goddess in the Kali tradition whose worship is associated with community protection and the management of dangerous spiritual forces. These deities are propitiated at community ceremonies and at times of crisis, illness, or important life transitions. The jhankri (shaman) of each settlement maintains the relationship with these deities through periodic ritual and trance-based communication.

Festivals and the Sarki/Mizar Cultural Calendar

The Mizar/Sarki community participates fully in the broad calendar of Hindu festivals observed across Nepal's hill communities — Dashain, the great autumn festival of Durga worship, is celebrated with particular community significance, as it involves the ritualized slaughter of animals whose hides the Sarki community would traditionally process. Tihar, the festival of lights, is observed with the full range of its five days. The spring festival of Holi and the harvest festival of Maghe Sankranti mark the agricultural calendar. Within these shared festivals, the Mizar community enacts its own specific forms of celebration — particular songs, dances, and communal gatherings that express Mizar identity within the broader festival framework.

Interesting Fact: The jhankri tradition among the Sarki/Mizar community represents one of the oldest layers of Himalayan spiritual practice — a form of shamanism with close parallels to the Bon shamanic tradition of Tibet and the shamanic practices of Siberia. The jhankri's drum, the ritual sound that anchors his trance and communication with spirits, is itself a leather instrument — creating yet another link between the Mizar's leather-working identity and the deepest layers of their spiritual life. The jhankri's drum-beating and the Madal-playing musician thus share a common material foundation in the Mizar's craft.


Mystery: The deity Dat Kali, worshipped specifically by the Sarki/Mizar and related Dalit communities of Nepal's hills, is not widely documented in Nepal's broader religious literature. Whether Dat Kali represents a very ancient pre-Vedic goddess tradition that survived within the Dalit communities precisely because their social exclusion kept them outside the Brahminic mainstream long enough to preserve archaic religious forms, or whether she represents a more recent local development, is a question that has not been adequately investigated by scholars of Himalayan religion.

Traditional Dress

Men's Dress: Bhoto, Kachada, and Dhakatopi

The traditional dress of Sarki/Mizar men follows the classic pattern of Nepal's hill communities, reflecting both the Khas cultural environment in which the Mizar have always lived and the practical demands of hill agriculture and physical labor. Traditional men's dress consists of the bhoto — a short, collarless vest or sleeveless jacket worn over the chest — paired with the kachada, a wrap-style lower garment similar to a dhoti but adapted to the hill style of wearing. Over this is worn the dhakatopi — the distinctive Nepali cap woven from Dhaka fabric in colorful geometric patterns — which functions as both a cultural marker of Nepali hill identity and a practical head covering. An ascot or scarf around the neck completes the traditional male ensemble, providing warmth in the cool hill climate.

The Dhakatopi — the traditional cap of Nepal's hill communities, woven from Dhaka fabric, worn as part of the Sarki/Mizar men's traditional dress ensemble.

Women's Dress: Cholo, Fariya, and Potuka

Traditional Sarki/Mizar women's dress centers on the fariya — a length of woven cloth wrapped as a skirt in the hill style — worn with the cholo, a fitted, short-sleeved blouse that is the standard upper garment for hill women across Nepal's Parbatiya communities. The potuka is a long cloth band worn tightly around the waist and stomach, serving both as a physical support for the back during heavy agricultural work and as a form of adornment. Together these three garments constitute the distinctively hill-Nepali female dress tradition that the Mizar share with Brahmin, Chhetri, and other hill communities — the common dress of the Parbatiya world, worn across caste lines.

Jewelry and Adornment

Sarki/Mizar women traditionally wear the standard forms of hill Nepali jewelry — glass bead necklaces (pote), simple gold or silver earrings, nose rings (nathiya), and bangles. The red and green glass bead necklace (pote) worn by married hill women is common across all hill communities, including the Mizar. Because the Sarki community's traditional economic status was low, gold and silver jewelry of the kind worn by wealthier Brahmin and Chhetri women was historically beyond reach for most Mizar households — glass beads and simple silver were the practical ornamental materials of the community. Contemporary Mizar women in urban areas have adopted modern dress with increasing frequency, while some community members maintain elements of traditional dress for festival and ceremonial occasions.

Interesting Fact: There is an unspoken but significant cultural connection between the Mizar/Sarki's leather-working tradition and their dress: the very footwear worn across Nepal's hill communities — the simple leather sandals and shoes that protected feet on steep mountain trails — was made by Sarki/Mizar hands. The irony of their social exclusion was thus complete: the community classified as "untouchable" and forbidden from sharing water or entering upper-caste homes was nonetheless the community that clothed the feet of every household in the village, from the highest Brahmin to the Chhetri farmer.


Mystery: The potuka — the waist-wrapping cloth worn by hill Nepali women — has no documented single origin story across the hill communities that use it. Whether it entered Nepal's hill dress culture from Indian textile traditions, developed independently within the Khas hill world, or reflects even older pre-Aryan dress practices absorbed into the Parbatiya tradition, is unclear. The Sarki/Mizar community's use of the potuka is simply one instance of this widespread hill textile practice whose ultimate origins are obscure.

Craft & Livelihood

The Leather Tradition: Centuries of Himalayan Craftsmanship

The core identity of the Mizar/Sarki community is inseparable from their leather-working tradition — a craft heritage stretching back centuries across the hills of Nepal. Working with the most elementary of hand tools, Sarki craftsmen traditionally produced a remarkable range of leather goods entirely by hand: shoes and sandals of multiple regional styles, bags and carrying straps, saddles and harnesses for pack animals, knife sheaths, rugs and floor coverings, ropes, head straps for load-bearing, and specialized ritual items such as the deer-skin asana (meditation seat) used by Hindu religious practitioners. The tanning of hides was equally part of the tradition — processing the skins of buffalo, goat, sheep, the Himalayan chauri (high-altitude cow), and yak into usable leather of specific qualities for specific applications.

Traditional hand-crafted leather goods produced by the Sarki/Mizar community — shoes, bags, and straps made using techniques passed down across generations in the hills of Nepal.

Regional Craft Variations: Palanchoke Shoes and Mustang Bags

The Mizar/Sarki leather tradition shows significant regional variation reflecting the different hides available and the needs of different ecological communities. In the eastern Kathmandu Valley area — particularly in Palanchok — the community was historically known for producing distinctive shoes from deer-skin, lined with fur and sewn with bamboo strips, that created a peculiar creaking sound when compressed. These shoes, known as palanchoke after their place of origin, were crafted for the hill communities of the region using techniques now largely lost. In Mustang district in the high Himalayan north, Sarki craftsmen used the thick hide of the chauri — the high-altitude Himalayan cow — to make bags, shoes, jackets, and other goods adapted to the extreme cold and altitude of the region's environment.

Contemporary Challenges and the Village Leather Initiative

The decline of traditional leather craftsmanship among the Mizar/Sarki community has been dramatic over the past three decades. The arrival of mass-produced plastic and rubber footwear at low prices made handmade leather shoes economically uncompetitive in most rural markets, depriving the community of its primary livelihood at a structural level. Many Sarki/Mizar households abandoned cobbling entirely, shifting to agricultural labor, load-carrying, and eventually foreign employment. To address this, the Village Leather Training Centre (VLTC), a non-governmental organization, launched an initiative to retrain Sarki leather-workers in production techniques oriented toward urban consumers and the export market — briefcases, backpacks, wallets, belts, and purses in naturally tanned village leather. This initiative both improved community incomes and preserved the leather-craft tradition in updated form.

Naturally tanned village leather produced by Mizar/Sarki artisans is today recognized as a distinct and valuable craft product — available in natural tan color and in black, praised for its durability and the distinctive quality that hand-tanning with traditional methods produces. Organizations such as Imartisan explicitly promote and support these communities, identifying the Mizar alongside Sarki, Bogati, Ramtel, and other sub-clans as producers of authentic village leather goods whose craft deserves recognition and market access.

Interesting Fact: Deer-skin leather — traditionally processed by the Sarki/Mizar community in the hills — holds special ritual significance in Hindu religious practice. The krishnajina (deer-skin mat) is considered sacred and is used for meditation, for wearing during the upanayana (sacred thread ceremony), and as a seat for yogis and hermits. This means that the Sarki/Mizar — classified as "untouchable" and therefore theoretically incapable of participating in Brahminic ritual — were in practice the craftspeople whose hands produced the very ritual objects that Brahminic religious ceremonies required. This contradition was never resolved within the caste system's logic; it was simply ignored.


Mystery: The tanning techniques used by the Sarki/Mizar community — natural vegetable tanning with bark extracts, fat-liquoring with animal fats, and smoke-tanning — are ancient processes whose specific formulations and proportions are held as community knowledge transmitted orally from master to apprentice. As fewer young Mizar enter the leather trade, this knowledge is at severe risk of disappearing entirely within a generation or two. Whether these techniques — some of which may produce leather properties (flexibility, durability, specific surface qualities) that industrial tanning cannot replicate — can be systematically documented and preserved before they are lost is one of the most pressing intangible heritage questions facing the Sarki/Mizar community today.

History and Origin

The Name, The Population, and Their Diverse Origins

The Muslims of Nepal — called Musalman (मुसलमान) in Nepali, or collectively referred to as Muslim or Islam-dharmi — constitute approximately 5.09% of Nepal's total population according to the 2021 National Population and Housing Census, a figure representing over 1.48 million people. The Musalman is also enumerated as a distinct caste/ethnic group constituting 4.9% of Nepal's population, making it the 7th largest ethnic community in the country. Nepal's Muslim community is not a monolithic group with a single origin or a single cultural tradition — rather, it comprises several distinct sub-communities who arrived in Nepal through different historical routes, speak different languages, follow somewhat different cultural practices, and inhabit different geographic zones within the country.

The largest and most historically established Muslim community in Nepal is the Terai Muslim population — concentrated in the southern lowlands along the Indian border, particularly in the districts of Parsa, Bara, Rautahat, Sarlahi, Mahottari, Dhanusa, Siraha, Saptari, Kapilvastu, Rupandehi, Nawalparasi, and Banke. These Terai Muslims speak varieties of Maithili, Bhojpuri, and Urdu — the same Indo-Aryan languages spoken by the broader Terai population — and their culture shares significant features with the broader South Asian Islamic tradition of the Gangetic plain. The second significant Muslim community consists of the Churaute Muslims of the middle hills — Muslim families of Kashmirian, Central Asian, and Indian merchant origin who settled in hill towns along trade routes, particularly in Palpa, Gorkha, Kathmandu, and other administrative centers.

Image: A Terai Muslim community gathering — the mosque, men in traditional white kurta and topi, the call to prayer resonating across the plains

Historical Islam in Nepal: Trade Routes and Royal Courts

Islam arrived in the Nepal hills primarily through two channels: the trade routes connecting Nepal with the Islamic world through Tibet and Central Asia, and the political relationships between Nepali hill kingdoms and the Mughal Empire of northern India. Historians believe that the first Muslims settled in Kathmandu during King Ratna Malla's reign in the late 15th century. These Muslims were Kashmiri merchants who were given permission by Ratna Malla to settle in Kathmandu, trading in rugs, carpets, shawls, and woollen goods between Kashmir, Ladakh, and Lhasa. These merchants brought with them not only goods but also a cosmopolitan Islamic culture connecting Nepal to the wider world of the Silk Road and the Indian Ocean trade network.

The Shah dynasty's relationship with Islam was complex. Prithvi Narayan Shah also encouraged Muslim traders to settle down with their families, and employed Muslim experts in manufacturing guns, cartridges, and cannons, as well as in international diplomacy — due to their knowledge of Persian and Arabic. Nepal was formally declared a Hindu kingdom (Hinduadhirajya) in 1962 under the Panchayat constitution — a designation that formally excluded Nepal's Muslim minority from the national identity narrative. The Federal Democratic Republic established in 2008 formally declared Nepal a secular state, one of the most significant constitutional changes for Nepal's religious minorities.

Terai Muslims and the Political Geography of Faith

The concentration of Nepal's Muslim population in the Terai creates a specific political geography — Muslims are a significant minority in most Terai districts (ranging from 5% to over 17% of district populations, with Rautahat reaching 17.2%) but are distributed across dozens of districts without any single Muslim-majority administrative territory. This distribution means that Muslim political representation requires coalition-building and inclusion within broader Madhesi identity politics. The Madhesi political movement — which advocates for the rights and representation of the entire Terai population — has included significant Muslim participation, and Muslim political leaders have been elected to parliament and served in government under both the first-past-the-post and proportional representation systems.

Interesting Fact: Nepal's Muslim community includes a small but historically significant group called the Churaute — Muslims who are physically and culturally indistinguishable from the hill-Hindu communities around them, who speak Nepali as their first language, wear the Daura Suruwal and Dhaka Topi, and were so integrated into hill-Nepali society that their Muslim identity was sometimes unknown to their non-Muslim neighbors. These Churaute Muslims represent the remarkable degree of cultural assimilation that can occur when religious minority communities integrate into a dominant host culture over multiple generations while maintaining their faith tradition.


Mystery: The origins of the Churaute Muslim community remain disputed — some family traditions trace descent from Kashmiri merchants, others from Afghan traders, and still others from converts from hill-Hindu communities. The absence of written genealogical records for most Churaute families makes their historical origins difficult to establish, and genetic analysis has not yet been conducted on a scale sufficient to resolve the question.

Language

Urdu: The Sacred and Literary Language of Nepali Muslims

While Nepali Muslims speak various first languages depending on their community of origin — Maithili, Bhojpuri, Urdu, Nepali, or Tharu for Terai Muslims; Nepali for hill Churaute Muslims — Urdu serves as the pan-community religious and literary language connecting Nepal's diverse Muslim communities to the broader South Asian Islamic world. Urdu — written in the Nastaliq form of the Arabic script, reading right to left — is the language of Muslim religious education (madrasa instruction), of the mosque sermon (khutba), of Islamic literature and poetry, and of much of the Islamic intellectual tradition accessible in South Asian languages. According to the 2021 census, Urdu is spoken as a mother tongue by approximately 1.4% of Nepal's total population.

Image: Children learning Arabic and Urdu script at a madrasa in Nepal — the Quranic text open before them, connecting them to a millennium-old tradition of Islamic literacy

Arabic: The Language of the Quran

Every Muslim child in Nepal, regardless of their first language, learns to read Arabic — the language of the Quran — at the local mosque school (maktab) or at a more formal madrasa. The ability to recite the Quran in Arabic is a fundamental Islamic literacy — not necessarily understanding the text linguistically, but being able to reproduce its sounds with accuracy and reverence. The Quran is believed to be the literal word of God in Arabic, and its recitation in the original Arabic is an act of worship regardless of the reciter's linguistic understanding. The distinctive phonological system of Arabic — its emphatic consonants, pharyngeal sounds, and long-short vowel distinctions — presents significant challenges for children whose first languages are Nepali, Maithili, or Bhojpuri, requiring dedicated study.

Maithili, Bhojpuri, and the Terai Muslim Linguistic Heritage

Terai Muslims are fully embedded in the linguistic cultures of the Terai — speaking Maithili in the eastern Terai districts, Bhojpuri in the central Terai, and Awadhi in the western Terai, alongside Urdu and often Nepali as additional languages. The Maithili and Bhojpuri literary traditions include specifically Muslim contributions — poets, storytellers, and lyricists from Muslim communities who have added their distinct voice to these largely Hindu-associated literary cultures. The oral poetry traditions of the Terai — including the Jat-Jatin folk songs, the Sohar birth songs, and the Biha wedding songs — include Muslim variants that adapt these shared cultural forms to Islamic themes and values.

Interesting Fact: The Urdu language that Nepal's Muslims use for religious and literary purposes is the same language that was historically the court language of the Mughal Empire, the language of the great Persian-influenced Indo-Islamic literary tradition, and is today the national language of Pakistan. A Nepali Muslim child who learns to read Urdu fluently gains access not just to religious texts but to one of the world's great literary traditions — the ghazal poetry of Ghalib and Iqbal, the prose of Saadat Hasan Manto, and the literary heritage of Lahore, Delhi, Lucknow, and Hyderabad.


Mystery: The degree to which Nepal's Terai Muslim communities maintained connections with the broader South Asian Islamic world during the Rana period (1846–1951) — when Nepal was largely closed to foreigners — is incompletely documented. Some Muslim families in the Terai maintain oral traditions of connections to dargahs (Sufi shrine complexes) across the border in India that date from before the political border hardened, suggesting a pre-national religious geography that treated the Terai-Bihar-UP borderland as a single Islamic cultural zone.

Music and Dance

Qawwali: The Sufi Music of Devotion

The most distinctive and beloved musical tradition of Nepal's Muslim communities is Qawwali — the devotional music of the Sufi tradition, in which groups of musicians perform long compositions of praise poetry (primarily in Urdu and Persian) with increasing intensity and repetition, aiming to induce a state of spiritual ecstasy (hal) in the performers and listeners. Qawwali is performed primarily at Sufi dargahs (shrines of Sufi saints) and at Urs celebrations (death anniversaries of Sufi saints, which are treated as celebrations of their union with God). Qawwali performances in Nepal — particularly at the major dargahs of the Terai — can attract large, diverse crowds of both Muslim and Hindu devotees, reflecting the cross-religious appeal of the Sufi tradition.

Image: A Qawwali performance — the lead singer (qawwal) with harmonium, supported by the chorus clapping hands and responding in the classical Sufi devotional tradition

Islamic Devotional Poetry: Naat and Hamd

Beyond the specifically Sufi context of Qawwali, the broader Islamic devotional musical tradition in Nepal includes Naat (praise poetry in honor of the Prophet Muhammad) and Hamd (praise of God/Allah). These forms are performed at mosques during religious events, at private gatherings during Islamic festivals, and on occasions of community celebration. Naat and Hamd are performed in Urdu, Arabic, and increasingly in Nepali — the development of Islamic devotional poetry in the Nepali language is a significant cultural development that connects Nepal's Muslim minority to the national linguistic culture.

Folk Music: Terai Muslim Songs and Shared Traditions

Terai Muslims participate in and contribute to the broader Terai folk music tradition — sharing many musical forms with the Hindu and other communities of the Terai. The Jat-Jatin dance-song — a monsoon-season performance tradition representing the interaction of a husband and wife negotiating domestic tensions through dance — is performed by Muslim communities with Islamic-inflected lyrical content alongside the broader non-Muslim Maithili tradition. Wedding music (biha geet) in Terai Muslim communities blends Islamic vocal traditions with the local folk music aesthetics of Maithili and Bhojpuri culture, creating a distinctive synthesis that is specific to Nepal's Terai Muslim communities.

Interesting Fact: The dargah of Hazrat Mian Saheb in Parsa district — one of Nepal's most important Sufi shrines — attracts both Muslim and Hindu pilgrims throughout the year and hosts one of Nepal's most significant Urs festivals annually. The cross-religious pilgrimage tradition of this and other Terai dargahs reflects the historical syncretic culture of the Terai, where the boundaries between Hindu and Muslim devotional practice were considerably more permeable than orthodox representatives of either tradition would prefer.


Mystery: The specific musical style of Qawwali performed at Nepal's Terai dargahs differs subtly but measurably from both Indian Qawwali traditions (particularly the Lahori and Delhavi styles) and from each other across different dargahs. Whether these local variations represent distinct musical lineages descending from different master musicians, or regional adaptations of a single imported tradition, has never been systematically studied.

Festivals and Culture

Eid ul-Fitr: The Festival of Breaking Fast

Eid ul-Fitr (ईद उल फितर) — the celebration marking the end of the month-long Ramadan fast — is the most joyful and socially significant festival of Nepal's Muslim community. Celebrated on the first day of Shawwal (the month following Ramadan) according to the Islamic lunar calendar, Eid ul-Fitr in Nepal is a public holiday — formally recognized under Nepal's secular constitution — and a day of intense community celebration. The morning begins with the communal Eid prayer (salat al-Eid) at the nearest mosque or at a large open prayer ground (Eidgah) — with thousands of worshippers gathering in neat rows for the two-rakat prayer and the subsequent sermon. The sight of thousands of Muslims in white clothing prostrating together in perfectly synchronized rows is one of the most visually powerful expressions of Muslim community in Nepal.

Image: Eid ul-Fitr morning prayer at Tundikhel, Kathmandu — thousands of worshippers in white, the imam at the front, the festive atmosphere of community and faith

Eid ul-Adha: The Festival of Sacrifice

Eid ul-Adha (ईद उल अजहा) — the Festival of Sacrifice, celebrating Abraham's willingness to sacrifice his son as commanded by God — is the second great Eid, celebrated approximately 70 days after Eid ul-Fitr. The defining practice of Eid ul-Adha is the ritual slaughter of an animal (typically a goat or sheep in Nepal, as cow slaughter is religiously sensitive) in commemoration of Abraham's sacrifice. One-third of the meat is kept for the family, one-third given to relatives and neighbors, and one-third given to the poor — a built-in redistribution mechanism that makes the festival simultaneously a religious observance and a social welfare practice. Both Eid ul-Fitr and Eid ul-Adha are recognized as public holidays under Nepal's secular republic.

Ramadan: The Month of Fasting, Prayer, and Community

Ramadan (रमजान) — the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar, during which Muslims fast from dawn to sunset — is the most spiritually intense and communally significant period of the Muslim year. During Ramadan, Nepal's Muslim communities transform their daily rhythms: waking before dawn for the Suhoor meal, fasting through the day, breaking the fast at sunset with the Iftar meal (traditionally with dates and water, followed by a full meal), and staying awake for extended Tarawih prayers at the mosque through the night. The social dimension of Ramadan — the communal Iftar meals where families and neighbors share the breaking of the fast, the late-night mosque gatherings, the heightened generosity toward the poor — creates a month of extraordinary community cohesion across Nepal's Muslim communities.

Interesting Fact: Nepal's Muslim communities observe both Eid ul-Fitr and Eid ul-Adha as public holidays — formal state recognition of Islamic festivals established in the secular republic after 2008. This represents a significant change from the Hindu Kingdom era when Muslim festivals had no official state recognition. Nepal's secular constitution now formally protects the right of all citizens to practice their religion, and the public holiday recognition of Eid is a concrete expression of this protection, reflecting the spirit of interfaith harmony that has historically characterized Nepal's social fabric.


Mystery: The spread of Sufi orders (tariqa) into the Nepal Terai — the specific lineages of Sufi initiation that connect Nepali Muslims to Sufi masters across northern India — has never been comprehensively documented. Nepal's Terai dargahs are connected by chains of Sufi initiation to major shrines in Ajmer, Delhi, and Lucknow, but the specific historical individuals who brought these lineages across the border and established them in Nepal are largely unknown.

Section Five

Traditional Dress

Islamic Dress in the Nepali Context

The dress of Nepal's Muslim communities reflects the synthesis of Islamic dress traditions with the local aesthetic cultures of their respective regions. Terai Muslim men's everyday dress consists of the Kurta Pyjama — a long tunic (kurta) worn over loose trousers (pyjama/salwar) — in white or other solid colors, with a white taqiyah (Muslim prayer cap) for prayer and formal occasions. This dress tradition is shared with Muslim communities across northern India and Bangladesh and represents the standard South Asian Islamic male aesthetic. For Eid prayers and major occasions, men typically wear their finest white kurta pyjama, often with an embroidered cap.

Image: A Muslim family dressed for Eid — men in white kurta and taqiyah cap, women in colorful salwar kameez with dupatta, traditional henna adorning the hands

Women's Dress: Salwar Kameez, Dupatta, and Modest Fashion

Terai Muslim women traditionally wear the Salwar Kameez — a tunic (kameez) worn over loose trousers (salwar) with a matching dupatta (long scarf) for hair and chest covering. The specific styles of salwar kameez in Nepal's Terai Muslim communities share aesthetic features with the broader eastern UP-Bihar-Terai textile tradition — embroidered necklines, specific regional color preferences (bright greens, pinks, and yellows are favored), and specific textile patterns. For the most formal occasions including Eid and weddings, women wear elaborately embroidered or zari-worked (gold thread) salwar kameez in silk or high-quality cotton.

The question of hijab — the Islamic head covering — varies within Nepal's Muslim communities. Some Terai Muslim women wear full dupatta coverage of the hair; others wear the hijab (a more structured head covering); others wear the dupatta loosely; and some — particularly younger urban women — do not observe hair covering. The trend in recent decades has been toward more consistent hijab observance, influenced by increased religious education and by international Islamic media.

Interesting Fact: The specific embroidery patterns used on Terai Muslim women's clothing — particularly the chikan embroidery associated with Lucknow and the eastern UP Islamic textile tradition — are among the most refined handcraft traditions in South Asia, requiring years of training to master. Chikan embroidery was historically a product of the Mughal court culture of Lucknow, and both Hindu and Muslim women in Terai communities share the appreciation for chikan-embroidered cloth, demonstrating how aesthetic traditions cross religious boundaries in South Asian material culture.


Mystery: The specific taqiyah (Muslim prayer cap) styles worn in different Terai districts vary subtly in shape, height, and embroidery pattern — variations that experienced Muslim community members can use to identify a man's home district. Whether these local variations reflect distinct craft traditions, deliberate community identity markers, or simply the accumulated effect of different local tailoring practices remains unstudied.

Unique Foods

Halal Cuisine: The Islamic Dietary Framework

Muslim food culture in Nepal is governed by the halal framework — the Islamic dietary laws that specify which foods are permissible (halal) and which are forbidden (haram). The primary halal distinctions: all meat must come from animals slaughtered according to Islamic procedure (bismillah — invoking God's name — followed by a single cut to the jugular); pork and pork products are strictly forbidden; alcohol is forbidden; blood and carrion are forbidden. These dietary restrictions mean that Nepal's Muslim communities maintain separate kitchen and dining spaces in the most orthodox households, purchase meat only from halal butchers, and avoid restaurants that cannot certify halal preparation.

Image: A festive Muslim kitchen preparation — aromatic biryani, kebabs, and seviyan (vermicelli dessert) being prepared for the community Eid feast

Biryani and Rice Dishes: The Celebratory Staples

Biryani — the aromatic layered rice dish of South Asian Islamic cuisine — is the defining celebratory food of Nepal's Muslim communities. Terai Muslim biryani draws on the eastern UP-Bihar-Terai culinary tradition — using long-grain basmati rice (or local fragrant varieties), goat or chicken meat marinated in yogurt and spices (saffron, cardamom, cloves, cinnamon, bay leaves, ghee), caramelized onions, and the specific slow-cooking technique (dum pukht) in which the sealed pot allows the flavors to develop through steam pressure. A well-made Terai Muslim biryani — its rice perfectly cooked, each grain separate and fragrant, the meat tender and deeply flavored, the whole suffused with the warm glow of saffron — is one of Nepal's supreme culinary achievements.

Seviyan, Sheer Khurma, and Festival Sweets

The sweet foods of Nepal's Muslim festivals are as specific and beloved as the savory dishes. Seviyan (ईद की सेवैयां) — thin vermicelli cooked in milk with sugar, cardamom, and dried fruits — is the defining Eid sweet, prepared in large quantities and distributed to neighbors and guests. Sheer Khurma — 'milk with dates' — is an even richer version in which dates, vermicelli, milk, and various dry fruits including pistachios, almonds, and cashews are combined in a dessert of extraordinary richness and fragrance. These milk-and-vermicelli sweets are made by virtually every Muslim household on Eid morning and are among the first things consumed after the Eid prayer.

Kebabs, Korma, and the Mughal Culinary Inheritance

The broader culinary tradition of Nepal's Terai Muslim communities reflects the Mughal-derived food culture of northern India. Seekh kebab (spiced minced meat grilled on skewers), Boti kebab (cubed marinated meat grilled over charcoal), Korma (meat braised in yogurt and spice paste), and Nihari (the slow-cooked overnight meat stew) are all part of the Terai Muslim culinary repertoire for special occasions. The spice vocabulary of this cuisine — nutmeg, mace, saffron, kewra (screwpine essence), rose water — connects it to the Persian and Central Asian culinary traditions that entered South Asia with the Mughal conquest.

Interesting Fact: The slow-cooking technique (dum pukht) used in biryani preparation was reportedly developed at the Lucknow court of Nawab Asaf-ud-Daula in the 18th century, when the nawab organized a public works project to feed his starving subjects — thousands of workers cooking rice and meat in large sealed pots over slow fires. The nawab tasted the result and found it so delicious that he had the technique refined for his court kitchen. This story illustrates how the most refined aspects of South Asian Islamic cuisine often emerged from practical necessity before becoming markers of culinary sophistication.


Mystery: The specific spice blends (masalas) used by Muslim families in the Nepal Terai — passed down within families as guarded culinary secrets — show remarkable variation from household to household and district to district, yet all trace to the same Mughal-derived culinary tradition. The mechanisms by which this variation developed — and the degree to which local Hindu Terai food traditions have influenced Muslim masala composition — represent a fascinating microcosm of culinary cultural exchange across religious boundaries.

History & Origins

The Name and Its Ancient Roots

The word Teli derives from the Sanskrit taila (तैल) — meaning oil — and its variant talika, both referring to vegetable oil extracted from seeds such as mustard (sarson) and sesame (til). This etymology places the Teli's identity at the very center of their historic trade: they were, for centuries, the people who extracted, refined, and distributed edible and lamp oil across the Indian subcontinent and into the Himalayan lowlands of Nepal. The Teli are part of the broader Madheshi social world of Nepal's Terai plains — a strip of flat, fertile, subtropical land stretching along Nepal's southern border with India — and their history is inseparable from the agricultural civilization of the Ganges basin, which stretches from Bihar and Uttar Pradesh northward into the plains of Nepal.

In Nepal, the Teli are classified by the Central Bureau of Statistics as a subgroup within the broader category of Madheshi Other Caste — a legal and administrative designation that groups the numerous caste communities of the Terai who do not belong to the Brahmin, Kshatriya, or Dalit categories. Under the old Muluki Ain (Legal Code) of 1854, the Teli were placed in the category of service castes who dealt in materials considered ritually borderline — oil being associated with both purity (as a sacred offering in lamps before deities) and impurity (as a greasy, commercial substance handled in large quantities). This ambiguous ritual status — simultaneously sacred and ordinary — has shaped the Teli's complex position in Nepal's caste hierarchy for generations.

The Ghani: The Engine of Teli Civilization

The central instrument of traditional Teli life was the ghani (also called kolhu) — a large wooden or stone oil press powered by a bullock walking in a circular track around a central wooden mortar and pestle. The ghani was not merely a tool but a social and economic institution: it defined the Teli family's place in the village economy, attracted customers from all surrounding communities, and represented the household's primary inherited asset. A traditional ghani required significant skill to operate — the seeds had to be cleaned, sorted, and sometimes roasted to specific temperatures before pressing; the press itself required careful maintenance; and the resulting oil had to be strained, stored in clay or metal vessels, and traded at appropriate seasons.

Image: A traditional bullock-powered ghani (oil press) of the type historically used by Teli communities — the central technology of their civilization for over a millennium

Mustard oil was the primary product of the Terai Teli's ghani — the pungent, golden oil that remains the defining cooking fat of Madheshi cuisine and of the broader north Indian culinary tradition. Sesame oil, groundnut oil, and linseed oil were also pressed, each with specific culinary and ritual uses. Sesame oil was especially prized for religious ceremonies — it was used in lamps offered before deities, in the abhisheka (sacred bathing) of temple idols, and in specific ritual preparations. This sacred use of their product gave the Teli a direct connection to the religious institutions of the communities they served — they were not merely tradespeople but suppliers of materiel for divine worship.

Settlement in the Terai and the Madheshi World

The Teli of Nepal are overwhelmingly concentrated in the Terai plains — the flat, fertile lowlands stretching across Nepal's southern fringe. The major districts of Teli concentration include Saptari, Siraha, Dhanusha, Mahottari, Sarlahi, Rautahat, Bara, Parsa, Nawalpur, Rupandehi, Kapilbastu, Dang, Banke, and Bardiya — the full length of the Terai from east to west. Within these districts, Teli communities are found in both rural villages organized around agricultural land and small-town market settings where the traditional oil trade operated. The Teli's Madheshi cultural world is deeply connected to the adjacent states of Bihar and Uttar Pradesh across the open border — and for centuries, the Teli community moved freely across this border for marriage, trade, and religious pilgrimage.

Census Data: The Teli in Modern Nepal

Nepal's 2011 National Population and Housing Census recorded 369,688 Teli, comprising 1.4% of Nepal's total population of 26.5 million. By the 2021 census, this number had grown to 431,347 individuals, representing 1.48% of Nepal's population. The Teli are thus one of the larger minority caste groups of Nepal, comparable in size to the Limbu and Sarki communities, and significantly larger than many of the hill ethnic groups that receive far greater political and media attention. The province-wise distribution of the 2011 census showed the Teli overwhelmingly concentrated in Province 2 (Madhesh Province) — the heart of the Terai — with smaller communities in Lumbini Province and Bagmati Province.

Interesting Fact: The mythological origin of the Teli traces directly to Lord Shiva — the supreme deity of the Hindu tradition. One tradition holds that Shiva created the first Teli to anoint him with sacred oil, establishing an intimate connection between the Teli's occupation and divine service from the very beginning of cosmic time. Another strand of the origin myth holds that Shiva created the Teli caste to destroy five demons through the power of oil — connecting the Teli's product to both sacred unction and to the destruction of evil. This double mythological role — as servants of the divine and as agents of purification — has given the Teli community a sacred justification for their trade that transcends ordinary commerce.


Mystery: The precise historical pathway by which Teli communities entered the Terai of Nepal — and the degree to which they were already present before the Shah expansion of the late 18th century versus arriving with the wave of Bihari and UP migrants encouraged by Shah and Rana rulers to develop the Terai's agricultural potential — is incompletely documented. The community's own oral traditions suggest considerable antiquity of settlement, but demographic historians note that much of the Terai's caste population expanded significantly in the 19th and early 20th centuries as the region was cleared of malaria-bearing forest and opened to agriculture.

Language & Identity

The Languages of the Teli: Maithili, Bhojpuri, and Awadhi

Unlike the Newa community — who possess a single, distinctive language of their own — the Teli of Nepal speak the same languages as the broader Madheshi communities of their respective Terai regions. The dominant Teli languages correspond to the regional linguistic geography of the Terai: Teli communities in the eastern Terai (Saptari to Mahottari districts) predominantly speak Maithili — one of the major literary languages of the Indian subcontinent with a tradition stretching back to the 14th-century poet Vidyapati; communities in the central Terai (Sarlahi to Nawalpur) tend toward Bajjika and Maithili; and communities in the western Terai speak Bhojpuri and Awadhi. All these languages belong to the Indo-Aryan family and share deep structural similarities with Hindi, while maintaining their own distinct vocabularies, literary traditions, and cultural identities.

Maithili-speaking Teli communities tend to identify with the broader Maithil cultural world — one of the oldest and most refined literary civilizations of northern India, associated with the ancient Videha kingdom described in the Ramayana as the home of Sita. Bhojpuri-speaking Teli share in the robust folk culture of the Bhojpuri world — a culture famous for its energetic folk music, its emotionally expressive oral poetry, and its strong community identity. For the Teli, language is thus not a separate community possession but a shared inheritance with the broader Madheshi world — yet within each linguistic community, Teli families maintain specific vocabulary, songs, proverbs, and oral traditions that carry the cultural memory of their oil-pressing heritage.

The Surname and the Sahu Identity

Within Nepal's Terai, many Teli families use the surname Sahu (also spelled Saw or Sahoo) — a title historically associated with the merchant and moneylender class in the Ganges plains. The adoption of Sahu as a surname reflects the Teli community's historic self-identification as traders and merchants rather than merely artisanal workers — the Teli's product, oil, required trading networks, market knowledge, and commercial relationships that positioned successful Teli families as genuine merchants. In Chhattisgarh (India), Telis are known exclusively as Sahu — and the same mercantile identity is carried by Teli communities in Nepal who use this title. Some Teli families have also adopted surnames like Gupta or Prasad as part of broader processes of social mobility and identity negotiation in the post-Muluki Ain period.

Social Organization: The Endogamous Community

The Teli are a strictly endogamous caste — marriage occurs within the Teli community, arranged by family elders who negotiate matches according to considerations of family reputation, economic compatibility, and astrological compatibility. Marriage negotiations typically involve the payment of a dowry (dahej) from the bride's family to the groom's family — a practice common across the Madheshi Hindu world that has come under increasing criticism for its role in gender inequality. Family structure among Nepal's Teli is predominantly nuclear in contemporary urban settings, though joint family structures — multiple generations living and eating together under one roof — remain common in rural Terai villages, particularly where agricultural land or a ghani oil press represents the family's shared economic resource.

Image: The Terai plains of Nepal — the fertile, subtropical lowland belt that is the ancestral homeland of the Teli community

Caste, Status, and Contemporary Identity

Under Nepal's old Muluki Ain, the Teli occupied an intermediate position in the hierarchy — above the Dalit communities who were classified as untouchable, but below the Brahmin, Kshatriya, and higher Vaishya categories. This positioning was reflected in restrictions on intercaste dining and in the social distance maintained by higher-caste Madheshis from Teli households. Nepal's 1963 New Muluki Ain formally abolished caste discrimination, and the 2015 Constitution of Nepal explicitly prohibits untouchability and caste-based discrimination. In practice, however, caste consciousness remains significant in the rural Terai's social life — particularly in matters of marriage, temple access, and informal social hierarchy. Contemporary Teli community organizations across Nepal have increasingly advocated for inclusive development policies, better education access, and recognition of their community's cultural and economic contributions to the Madheshi and national heritage.

Interesting Fact: The Maithili literary tradition — the primary literary language of eastern Terai Teli communities — is one of the oldest continuously written literary languages in South Asia, with a formal literary corpus beginning with the 14th-century court poet Vidyapati whose devotional songs to Lord Shiva and Radha-Krishna remain sung at Terai festivals to this day. Maithili was recognized as one of the 22 scheduled languages of India in 2003, and is taught in schools across Bihar. In Nepal, Maithili is the second most spoken mother tongue (after Nepali) with over three million speakers — and the Teli community of the eastern Terai contributes significantly to this linguistic heritage.


Mystery: The question of whether the Teli community should be classified as Vaishya (as their own mythological tradition and historical commercial role suggests), as Other Backward Class (as Indian government policy designates most Teli communities), or as a distinct Madheshi social category (as Nepal's administrative framework places them) remains unresolved and politically contested. The community's self-identification has shifted across different censuses and political periods — a phenomenon that the sociologist M.N. Srinivas described as "Sanskritization" — and the deeper question of how caste identity interacts with changing economic conditions and democratic politics in the Terai is one of the most fascinating unresolved questions in Nepalese social history.

Music & Folk Arts

The Musical World of the Terai Teli

The Teli community's musical life is embedded in the broader and extraordinarily rich folk music traditions of the Terai — a region whose musical heritage spans the entire spectrum of human emotion and social occasion, from the devotional bhajan to the ribald wedding song, from the seasonal harvest celebration to the lament of departure. The Teli participate in the Madheshi folk music world as both performers and patrons — engaging with the same repertoire of Maithili, Bhojpuri, and Awadhi folk songs that form the living musical culture of the Terai plains. Several folk genres are especially significant in Teli cultural life: the Jat-Jatin song-and-dance tradition, the devotional bhajan and kirtan cycles, the wedding songs (vivah geet), and the seasonal chaiti and kajri song cycles.

Image: Maithili folk performance in the Terai — the same musical tradition that forms the cultural context for Teli community celebrations across Nepal's eastern plains

Jat-Jatin: The Monsoon Dance of the Eastern Terai

The most distinctive folk performance tradition of the eastern Terai — in which many Teli communities participate — is the Jat-Jatin tradition: a call-and-response song-and-dance form performed by men and women in pairs during the monsoon season. Jat-Jatin performances enact a narrative of a married couple navigating the strains of monsoon season — the husband's absence on trading journeys, the wife's longing and management of the household, their reunion and quarrels and reconciliation. The songs are sung in Maithili and Bajjika with great emotional directness — expressing longing, jealousy, humor, and tenderness in lyrics that have been refined over generations. Jat-Jatin is performed in open courtyards or village squares during the monsoon nights, with the community gathered around the performing pairs, and its seasonal association connects it directly to the agricultural cycle of the Terai's rice cultivation.

Bhajan, Kirtan, and Devotional Music

Devotional music occupies a central place in Teli cultural life — reflecting the community's deep religiosity and their historic connection to temple service through the supply of oil for sacred lamps. The bhajan tradition — devotional songs addressed to specific deities, particularly Shiva (the Teli's mythological patron), Ram, Krishna, and the goddess Durga — is practiced in Teli households and community spaces throughout the year, with particular intensity during festivals. Kirtan — the collective, antiphonal singing of devotional songs, often involving the playing of the dholak (double-headed drum), harmonium, and cymbals — is performed at major festival occasions and at religious observances connected to the life cycle. The dholak is the central instrument of Madheshi folk and devotional music, and its steady, warm rhythmic texture underlies virtually all Teli musical performances from weddings to religious gatherings.

Wedding Songs: The Vivah Geet Tradition

The most elaborate musical tradition in Teli community life is the vivah geet — the extended cycle of songs performed by women throughout the wedding process, which in traditional Teli communities can extend over multiple days. Each stage of the wedding ceremony has its associated songs: songs for the initial engagement (tilak), songs for the turmeric-application ceremony (haldi), songs sung during the barat (groom's wedding procession), songs for the sacred fire (saptapadi), songs of farewell when the bride departs for her new home (bidai), and songs of welcome when the bride arrives at her husband's household. These songs are composed in Maithili or Bhojpuri and are performed exclusively by women — the senior women of the household leading and the younger women learning — representing one of the primary mechanisms by which folk musical knowledge is transmitted across generations in Teli communities.

Interesting Fact: The kajri songs of the Bhojpuri and Awadhi tradition — performed during the monsoon season and specifically associated with the month of Sawan (July–August) — contain some of the most ancient surviving folk poetry of northern India. The kajri genre is referenced in Sanskrit literary texts of the early medieval period, making it over a thousand years old as a recognized song form. Teli women of the western Terai participate in the kajri tradition, which is associated with the pining of women for absent husbands — a deeply resonant theme in a community whose men historically spent months away on oil-trading journeys.


Mystery: The Jat-Jatin tradition contains within its repertoire certain songs whose lyrics describe specific occupational details of the oil-pressing trade — references to the turning of the ghani press, the smell of fresh mustard oil, the weight of oil vessels carried to market — that appear in no other folk genre of the region. Whether these songs preserve a genuine memory of the time when Jat-Jatin was specifically a Teli community performance before becoming a broader Madheshi tradition, or whether the occupational references are coincidental inclusions from a time when the ghani was a universal village feature, is an unresolved question in Terai folk studies.

Festivals & Culture

The Teli Festival Calendar: Madheshi Hindu Devotion

The Teli festival calendar is the festival calendar of Madheshi Hindu culture — a rich, densely layered cycle of observances that follows both the pan-Hindu festival cycle and the specific regional traditions of the Terai's Maithili, Bhojpuri, and Awadhi worlds. The Teli observe all major Hindu festivals — Dashain, Tihar, Holi, Shivaratri, Krishna Janmashtami, Ram Navami — with additional observance of specifically Madheshi festivals that are largely unknown in the Kathmandu Valley or the Pahadi hills. The result is a festival calendar that, across the twelve months of the Hindu calendar, leaves virtually no week without some occasion for communal gathering, ritual, feast, or song.

Chhath: The Supreme Festival of the Terai Teli

The single most important and culturally distinctive festival of the Madheshi Hindu world — and thus of the Teli community — is Chhath Puja: a four-day festival of worship offered to the sun god (Surya) and his wife Usha (the dawn goddess), conducted at riverbanks or ponds, involving ritual fasting, bathing in sacred water, and the offering of arghya (sacred offerings of fruits, sugarcane, rice, and lamps) at sunset and sunrise. Chhath is unique among Hindu festivals in that it is conducted entirely outdoors, in direct contact with natural water bodies, and places women — especially mothers — at the center of the ritual as the primary worshippers and fasters. A woman who observes Chhath for her family undertakes a rigorous thirty-six-hour fast without water, followed by standing waist-deep in a river at sunset and sunrise to offer prayers to the sun directly.

Image: Chhath Puja at a riverbank in Nepal — the supreme festival of the Madheshi world, in which Teli families gather to offer arghya to the sun god at sunset and sunrise

Shivaratri: The Teli's Sacred Night

Maha Shivaratri — the great night of Shiva, observed in the month of Phalgun (February–March) — carries special significance for the Teli community above and beyond its pan-Hindu importance. Shiva is the Teli's mythological creator and patron deity, and Shivaratri is the night on which Shiva's divine energy is most accessible to his devotees. Teli households observe Shivaratri with particular devotion: the night-long fast (upvas), the offering of Bilva leaves, milk, and — especially — sesame oil in the sacred lamp (diya) before the Shiva lingam. The use of sesame oil rather than ghee in the Shivaratri lamp is a specifically Teli practice that enacts the community's mythological role as divine oil-providers — offering to Shiva the very substance he created their caste to produce.

Holi: The Festival of Color and Release

Holi — the spring festival of color — is celebrated with particular exuberance in the Terai, where the Bhojpuri and Maithili folk traditions have developed an especially elaborate musical and performative tradition for the occasion. The weeks preceding Holi are marked by phaguwa singing — bawdy, humorous, emotionally unrestrained Bhojpuri songs performed at community gatherings that provide a licensed period of social release before the more regulated behavior of the rest of the Hindu calendar year. Teli communities participate in the phaguwa tradition with great enthusiasm — the songs' themes of playful romance, the mixing of colors, and the victory of good over evil through the burning of Holika are woven together with the community's specific folk repertoire into a festival of extraordinary vitality.

Interesting Fact: Chhath Puja is believed by scholars of religion to be one of the few surviving Vedic rituals in which the sun is worshipped directly — without the intermediary of a temple idol, a priestly intermediary, or a prescribed textual liturgy — placing the worshipper in direct, personal relationship with the solar deity. This direct, unmediated character of Chhath worship is highly unusual in the Hindu devotional landscape, and gives the festival a character of ancient austerity that contrasts sharply with the elaborate temple rituals of the broader Hindu tradition. That the Teli — an occupational caste associated with lamp oil (a product that mediates between darkness and the gift of light) — should be especially devoted to the worship of the sun itself carries a poetic resonance that has not been lost on community tradition.


Mystery: The origin of Chhath Puja as a distinct Terai festival — separate from the pan-Hindu solar worship traditions of other regions — is imperfectly documented. Some scholars trace it to the Mahabharata period and connect it to Karna (the son of the sun god) who is said to have performed the first Chhath; others argue that it represents a survival of pre-Aryan solar worship by the indigenous communities of the Gangetic plains, later absorbed into the Hindu framework. The specific rituals of Chhath — the use of bamboo baskets, the specific fruits offered, the standing in water at sunset and sunrise — do not appear in any Sanskrit textual tradition, suggesting origins outside the mainstream Sanskrit-Brahminic religious tradition.

Traditional Dress

Women's Dress: The Sari and Madheshi Textile Tradition

Traditional Teli women's dress follows the Madheshi Hindu women's sartorial tradition, centered on the sari — typically in cotton or silk, in colors varying from festive reds and oranges (for married women) to white (for widows) to the full spectrum of colors for everyday wear. Unlike the specifically Newa Haku Patasi with its fixed colors, Teli women's sari traditions do not involve a single defining color or style — rather, the sari functions as a marker of Madheshi femininity and Hindu marital status, with specific colors and designs chosen for specific occasions. A married Teli woman's festive sari for Chhath is typically in deep red or vermillion — colors associated with auspiciousness, fertility, and the sun's energy. For everyday work, cotton saris in lighter colors are preferred, suited to the physical labor of managing households in the Terai's tropical climate.

Image: Madheshi women in Nepal's Terai in traditional sari dress — the sartorial tradition shared by Teli women across the plains

The Sindoor, Pote, and Markers of Married Status

The most significant elements of a married Teli woman's appearance are the sindoor (vermillion powder worn in the parting of the hair) and the pote (glass bead necklace in green and gold) — both markers of married status that are applied at the Hindu wedding ceremony and maintained throughout the marriage. The sindoor — applied daily to the hair parting by the husband or by the woman herself — is the most visually immediate marker of married status in the Madheshi Hindu tradition, and its presence or absence immediately signals a woman's marital status to the community. The pote or mangalsutra (sacred thread necklace) worn by married Teli women varies in design by region, but typically involves glass beads in auspicious colors combined with gold or gold-plated pendant elements. Gold jewelry — earrings, nose rings (nathiya), bangles, and toe rings — completes the traditional married Teli woman's ornamental ensemble.

Men's Dress: Dhoti, Kurta, and the Gamchha

Traditional Teli men's dress in the Terai consists of the dhoti — a length of unstitched white or off-white cotton cloth wrapped around the waist and legs — worn with a kurta (a long, loose cotton shirt). For formal and ceremonial occasions, the dhoti is worn in the more elaborate Bihari style with the cloth brought between the legs and tucked at the back. The gamchha — a thin, lightweight cotton towel in red-and-white check or striped pattern — is the quintessential everyday accessory of Terai men across all castes and communities, worn over the shoulder, around the neck, or wrapped around the head as protection from the sun. In contemporary Teli communities, younger men and urban residents have largely shifted to modern clothing — trousers, shirts, and occasionally salwar-kameez — while the dhoti and gamchha persist among older men and in rural settings.

Interesting Fact: The traditional Teli ghani worker had a highly specific work dress — a minimal cotton loincloth that allowed freedom of movement during the physically demanding work of guiding bullocks around the press and managing the heavy wooden mortar — together with a cotton gamchha wrapped around the head to absorb sweat in the Terai's intense heat. The smell of fresh mustard oil was so deeply associated with this work dress that it became a cultural marker of Teli identity in the mixed-caste village economy — one could identify a Teli oil-presser by scent as well as by sight in the village marketplace.


Mystery: Traditional Madheshi textile production — including the hand-woven cotton fabrics of the Terai that formed the basis of traditional Teli dress — was closely connected to specific weaver castes whose work supplemented the oil-pressing economy of the Terai village. Whether the Teli community maintained any specific textile traditions of their own — particular patterns, colors, or weaving conventions associated specifically with their community — or whether they entirely relied on the weaver castes' general output is not well documented. Community elders in some Terai districts recall specific patterns of gamchha associated with Teli families, but no systematic ethnographic study has been conducted.

Unique Foods

Teli Cuisine: The Mustard Oil Kitchen of the Terai

The defining characteristic of Teli cuisine — and of the broader Madheshi cooking tradition in which it is embedded — is the primacy of mustard oil as the universal cooking medium. Where hill Nepali cooking relies on refined vegetable oil or ghee, and where Western cooking relies on butter or olive oil, the Madheshi Teli kitchen is organized around the pungent, golden heat of sarson ka tel — mustard oil. Mustard oil must be heated to near-smoking point before use (a process called chaunka) to drive off the volatile compounds that give raw mustard oil its sharpness — and this heating creates the distinctive aroma of Madheshi cooking that permeates every Teli household kitchen. The oil's deep, slightly bitter, warming quality imparts a distinctive flavor to everything cooked in it — a flavor so foundational to Teli and Madheshi cuisine that dishes cooked in refined oil are described as tasting phika (flat, lacking character).

Image: Mustard fields in bloom in Nepal's Terai — the golden crop whose seeds the Teli community pressed for centuries to produce the defining oil of Madheshi cuisine

Thekua: The Sacred Festival Sweet

The most distinctive and culturally specific food preparation associated with the Teli's primary festival — Chhath Puja — is Thekua (also written Thekwa or Khajuria): a dense, sweetened wheat or rice flour cookie fried in ghee or oil, flavored with jaggery (gur), anise, and coconut, and pressed into decorative molds before frying. Thekua is prepared specifically as an offering to the sun god during Chhath Puja — it is placed in the bamboo soop (winnowing tray) along with sugarcane, seasonal fruits, and earthen lamps when the arghya is offered at the riverbank. The preparation of Thekua is a ritual act as much as a culinary one — it is made during the period of ritual purity maintained by the fasting woman (or her household) and is handled with the care appropriate to a sacred offering. After the ritual, the Thekua is distributed as prasad (divine food blessing) to family members and community.

Sattu: The Nutritious Ancient Staple

Sattu — roasted and ground chickpea (or mixed grain) flour — is one of the most ancient and nutritionally concentrated foods of the Gangetic plains, and remains a significant element of Teli and broader Madheshi food culture. Sattu is consumed in multiple ways: stirred into cold water with salt and lemon as a refreshing, protein-rich summer drink; mixed with water into a dense paste and eaten with pickles as a quick meal; incorporated into flatbreads (sattu paratha); or used as a stuffing for litti (a specific Bihari preparation in which dough balls stuffed with sattu are roasted over a wood fire). Sattu's importance in Teli diet relates to the practical demands of the ghani oil-presser's working day — it is light to carry, requires no cooking in its basic drink form, provides sustained energy, and is easily consumed between the physically demanding cycles of bullock-driving and pressing.

Dal, Rice, and the Madheshi Feast

The daily Teli household diet centers on the Madheshi version of the South Asian staple: steamed rice (bhat) eaten with lentil soup (dal) cooked in mustard oil with a tadka (tempering) of cumin, dried chili, garlic, and onion. The specific lentils preferred vary by household and region — split pigeon peas (arhar dal), split black lentils (mas dal), and split mung beans (moong dal) are all used. Vegetable curries — particularly aloo (potato), tori (ridge gourd), lauki (bottle gourd), kathal (jackfruit, treated as a meat substitute in vegetarian preparations), and saag (mustard greens and spinach) — complete the everyday meal. For Teli households that practice non-vegetarianism (which varies by specific family tradition and religious vow), goat meat, fish from the Terai's rivers, and chicken are prepared on festival days and for guests — always cooked in mustard oil with the bold spicing of Madheshi cuisine: cumin, coriander, turmeric, chili, and garam masala.

Interesting Fact: The Thekua offered during Chhath is prepared using a specific wooden mold (sancha) carved with decorative patterns — flowers, fish, geometric designs — that is typically a treasured household possession inherited across generations. These molds are not mass-produced but made by traditional woodcarvers to specific family preferences, and the pattern impressed on the Thekua thus becomes a subtle identifier of which household prepared it. In communities where families have maintained the same mold design for multiple generations, the Thekua itself becomes a form of family heritage — the same pattern pressed into the sun-offering cookie as was pressed by the family's great-grandmother at Chhath generations ago.


Mystery: The nutritional profile of mustard oil — which contains high levels of erucic acid, a compound that some mid-20th-century nutritional research linked to cardiac concerns — has been the subject of ongoing scientific debate. However, populations of the Gangetic plains and Nepal's Terai who have consumed mustard oil as their primary cooking fat for centuries show no elevated rates of the cardiac conditions that erucic acid was theorized to cause, and more recent research suggests that traditional cold-pressed mustard oil may contain beneficial compounds that partially offset its erucic acid content. Whether the traditional Teli method of heating mustard oil to smoking point before cooking — which breaks down some of its volatile compounds — also affects its nutritional profile is an open question in food science that has not been systematically studied.

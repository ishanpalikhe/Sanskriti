History and Origin

The Name, The Terai, and Their Ancient Craft

The Nuniya — an indigenous ethnic community primarily residing in the eastern Terai districts of Nepal, including Jhapa, Morang, Sunsari, and Saptari — are renowned for their traditional occupation of weaving and cloth production. According to the 2021 National Census, the Nuniya number approximately 25,000, constituting a small but culturally significant minority within Nepal's diverse ethnic landscape. Unlike the hill-dwelling communities of Nepal, the Nuniya are part of the Terai's complex mosaic of indigenous and migrant groups, with a distinct identity rooted in their mastery of the handloom and their deep connection to the agricultural plains of eastern Nepal.

The eastern Terai — a flat, fertile alluvial plain stretching from the Koshi River in the east to the Bagmati in the west — has been a crossroads of culture and commerce for centuries. The Nuniya settled in this region approximately 500 years ago, migrating from various parts of northern India. They established themselves as specialized weavers, producing the distinctive Dhaka fabric that has become an iconic symbol of Nepali national identity. The name "Nuniya" itself is believed to derive from the Sanskrit word "Lavanika," meaning salt maker or trader, though over generations their identity became primarily associated with textile production rather than salt trading.

The Handloom Legacy: Masters of Dhaka Weaving

The Nuniya community's most significant contribution to Nepali culture is their mastery of Dhaka weaving — a traditional handloom technique that produces a distinctive geometric patterned fabric, most famously used for the Dhaka topi (the national cap of Nepal) and the exquisite Dhaka sarees worn by women across the country. The term "Dhaka" refers not to a place but to the woven pattern itself: intricate checks, stripes, and geometric motifs created by carefully arranging colored threads on the loom. The Nuniya weavers, working on traditional pit looms or frame looms, produce fabrics that are prized for their durability, intricate designs, and cultural authenticity. A single Dhaka saree can take weeks to complete, with patterns so complex that they require the weaver to memorize hundreds of thread sequences.

Interesting Fact: The Dhaka topi, which is considered the national cap of Nepal and is worn by government officials, university graduates, and ordinary citizens alike on formal occasions, was historically woven exclusively by Nuniya artisans in the eastern Terai. The distinctive black-and-white geometric pattern with red or gold borders is directly traceable to Nuniya weaving traditions that predate the unification of Nepal.


Mystery: The exact origins of the Nuniya as a distinct ethnic group remain contested among scholars. Some argue they are a sub-group of the broader Teli (oil presser) caste who specialized in weaving, while others claim they are a distinct community with origins in the weaving guilds of Mughal-era Bengal. The absence of pre-19th century written records makes definitive classification impossible.

Language

Maithili and Hindi: The Voices of the Terai

The Nuniya community primarily speaks Maithili — a rich Indo-Aryan language with a literary tradition dating back to the 14th century — as well as various dialects of Hindi and, increasingly, Nepali. Maithili is the dominant language of the eastern Terai, spoken by millions across the Madhesh region, and is recognized as a constitutional language of Nepal. The Nuniya dialect of Maithili includes specialized vocabulary related to weaving, dyeing, and textile commerce that is not found in other Maithili-speaking communities — a linguistic testament to their occupational specialization.

Oral Traditions and the Language of the Loom

The Nuniya have a rich oral tradition, including folk songs (gazals and jhumar), proverbs, and stories that are passed down through generations. Many of these oral texts encode practical knowledge about weaving — the properties of different threads, the preparation of natural dyes, and the rhythm of the loom. The language of weaving itself is highly specialized: different types of looms, weaving patterns, and stages of fabric production have distinct names that are unknown outside the community. However, like many minority languages in Nepal, Maithili among the Nuniya faces pressure from the spread of Nepali as the national language, and younger generations in urban areas increasingly use Nepali or English in daily life.

Maithili: The native language of the Nuniya people.

Interesting Fact: The Nuniya have a distinct counting system used while weaving — numbers are called out in a rhythmic chant that coordinates the movement of the shuttle, the beater, and the foot pedals. This "weaving meter" is so precise that experienced weavers can produce complex patterns entirely from auditory memory without looking at a pattern diagram.


Mystery: The Nuniya vocabulary contains several words for types of looms and weaving techniques that are not found in any other Indo-Aryan language, leading some linguists to speculate that the community may have preserved vocabulary from a pre-Maithili substrate language. The origins of this technical lexicon remain uninvestigated.

Music and Dance

Jhumar: The Rhythm of the Terai

The Nuniya musical tradition is part of the broader Maithili cultural sphere, with distinctive local variations. The most characteristic Nuniya dance form is the Jhumar — a graceful, circular dance performed by both men and women during festivals and weddings. Jhumar is accompanied by traditional Terai instruments including the dholak (a double-headed barrel drum), the madal (a hand drum), the nagara (kettledrum), the shehnai (a wind instrument similar to an oboe), and the manjira (small hand cymbals). The songs sung during Jhumar often tell stories of love, separation, and the beauty of the Terai landscape — the monsoon rains, the ripening of paddy fields, and the flight of birds over the Koshi river.

The Sama Chakeva Tradition

One of the most unique musical traditions observed by the Nuniya is Sama Chakeva, a festival in which young women sing songs dedicated to the Sama bird (a type of cuckoo), celebrating the bond between brothers and sisters. The songs are sung in Maithili, with complex call-and-response patterns and lyrics that have been passed down for centuries. The festival includes the creation of clay idols of birds, which are worshipped and then immersed in rivers — a practice that reflects the Nuniya's deep connection to the natural environment of the Terai.

The women of nuniya people singing during Sama Chakeva tradition.

Interesting Fact: The Nuniya have a tradition of "weaving songs" — rhythmic work songs sung while operating the handloom. The tempo of the song matches the pace of the shuttle, and different patterns are associated with different melodic phrases. These songs serve both to coordinate group work in communal weaving spaces and to pass on technical knowledge to apprentices.


Mystery: Some Nuniya folk songs contain references to specific historical events — including famines, floods, and migrations — but these events are not recorded in any written historical source. Ethnomusicologists have attempted to date these songs through linguistic analysis, but the results remain inconclusive.

Section Four

Festivals and Culture

The Nuniya Festival Calendar: Colors of the Terai

The Nuniya observe a festival calendar that reflects both their Maithili cultural heritage and their occupational identity as weavers. Major festivals include Chhath (the sun-worshipping festival celebrated with great devotion on the banks of the Koshi and other rivers), Teej (women's festival with fasting and singing), Dipawali (the festival of lights), Holi (the spring festival of colors), and Sama Chakeva (the unique bird-worshipping festival). Unlike the complex urban festivals of the Newar, Nuniya festivals are characterized by their connection to the agricultural cycle and the natural environment of the Terai plains.

The Weavers' Rituals: Deities of the Loom

The Nuniya community maintains specific rituals related to their weaving occupation. Before beginning a new weaving project — particularly a Dhaka saree of great complexity — a weaver will perform a small puja (worship) to the loom, offering incense, flowers, and rice to the deity believed to reside in the weaving tools. The loom itself is considered sacred, and there are prohibitions against stepping over it or using it for purposes other than weaving. The goddess Bhagwati (a form of Durga) is particularly revered as the protector of weavers, and her blessings are sought before the start of the main weaving season following the monsoon.

The community of Nuniya people celebrating the Teej festival.

Interesting Fact: During the Chhath festival, Nuniya women weave small decorative pieces of fabric as offerings to the sun god, which are then floated on the river along with the traditional offerings of fruits and sugarcane. The floating fabric is believed to carry the weaver's prayers to the deity.


Mystery: The Nuniya maintain a secret oral tradition regarding the preparation of natural dyes — including recipes for a particularly vibrant red derived from the roots of a specific Terai plant. This knowledge is transmitted only within families, and the identity of the plant is not disclosed to outsiders, making it impossible to scientifically analyze.

Section Five

Traditional Dress

The Dhaka Saree and the Mark of the Weaver

The traditional dress of the Nuniya community is intimately connected to their weaving heritage. Nuniya women traditionally wear the Dhaka saree — a handwoven saree made of cotton or silk, characterized by its geometric patterns in black, white, red, and gold threads. The Dhaka saree is draped in the traditional Maithili style, with the pallu (the decorative end of the saree) worn over the right shoulder and the fabric arranged in precise pleats at the waist. The saree is typically paired with a matching blouse (choli) and an odhni (a dupatta-like scarf) for additional coverage. Gold and silver jewelry — including nose rings (nath), earrings (kundal), and anklets (pajeb) — complete the ensemble.

Nuniya Men: Dhoti and Dhaka Topi

Nuniya men traditionally wear a white or cream dhoti (a rectangular cloth wrapped around the waist and passed between the legs) paired with a kurta (a long loose shirt) or a simple cotton vest. The most distinctive element of male Nuniya dress is the Dhaka topi — the national cap of Nepal, which, as noted, was historically woven by Nuniya artisans. The Dhaka topi is made from the same geometric-patterned fabric as the women's sarees, creating a visual unity between men's and women's traditional attire. For special occasions, men may wear a waistcoat made of Dhaka fabric over the kurta.

Dhaka Saree: The traditional homemade attire of the women of the Nuniya community.

Interesting Fact: The specific geometric pattern woven into a Dhaka saree can indicate the weaver's village of origin — different Nuniya communities in Jhapa, Morang, and Sunsari have distinct pattern preferences that are recognizable to connoisseurs of the fabric.


Mystery: Some very old Dhaka sarees in museum collections feature patterns that are no longer woven by any living Nuniya weaver. The knowledge of how to create these patterns appears to have been lost within the past century, and no written records of the techniques survive.

Unique Foods

Maithili Cuisine: The Flavors of the Terai

Nuniya cuisine is part of the broader Maithili culinary tradition, characterized by the abundant use of rice, lentils, vegetables, and fish from the Terai's many rivers and ponds. The staple meal is bhat-dal-tarkari (rice, lentil soup, and vegetable curry), often accompanied by achar (pickle) made from mango, lemon, or chili. The Nuniya, like other Terai communities, prepare a distinctive fermented dish called khari — made from rice or lentil batter left to ferment overnight, then steamed into a soft, sour cake that is eaten with mustard oil and green chilies.

Fish Curry and Festival Sweets

Given the proximity to the Koshi River and numerous ponds, fish plays a prominent role in Nuniya cuisine. Machh ka jhol (fish curry) is prepared with mustard oil, turmeric, ginger, garlic, and a blend of ground spices, simmered until the flavors meld into a rich, aromatic gravy. During festivals, elaborate sweets are prepared, including peda (sweetened condensed milk patties), khaja (a flaky fried sweet), and thekua (a deep-fried wheat flour cookie flavored with cardamom and jaggery). These sweets are offered to deities and shared among community members as part of festival celebrations.

Daal bhat-tarkari: The traditional staple meal of the Nuniya community.

Interesting Fact: The Nuniya prepare a unique flatbread called dhuska — made from fermented rice and lentil batter, deep-fried until golden and crispy, then served with spicy potato curry or yogurt. Dhuska is considered a festive food and is prepared especially during the Chhath festival.


Mystery: Some Nuniya families maintain the tradition of preparing a medicinal herbal wine called jagran using over 20 different Terai herbs, the identities of which are known only to senior family members. The recipe is believed to have been brought from northern India at the time of the community's migration, but its origins remain undocumented.

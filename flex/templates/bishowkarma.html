Section One

History and Origin

The Name, The Deity, and the Artisan Heritage

The Bishowkarma — also written Vishwakarma, Biswokarma, or Bisho-karma — are the artisan castes of Nepal whose hereditary occupations include metalworking (blacksmithing, goldsmithing, coppersmithing), woodworking (carpentry, woodcarving), and stone-cutting. The name Vishwakarma (विश्वकर्मा) means 'maker of all' or 'architect of the universe' in Sanskrit — Vishwakarma is the divine architect and craftsman of the Hindu pantheon, the god who built the palaces of the gods, forged the weapons of the divine warriors, and created the celestial vehicles of the deities. For the Bishowkarma community, this name is not merely occupational identification — it is a theological claim: they are the earthly manifestations of the divine artisan, continuing the creative work of the cosmic craftsman in the human realm.

In Nepal's caste hierarchy as codified in the Muluki Ain of 1854, the Bishowkarma were classified as Dalit (untouchable) communities — specifically as pani nachalne (water-impure) or tagai lagaune (sacred-thread-ineligible) communities. This classification placed them at the bottom of the caste hierarchy despite — or perhaps because of — the extraordinary skill and cultural indispensability of their work. The Bishowkarma community comprises several sub-caste groups defined by their specific craft specialization: Kami (blacksmiths and metalworkers), Sunar (goldsmiths and silversmiths), Sarki (leatherworkers and cobblers), Damai (tailors and musicians), and in some regional classifications, Chunara (woodcarvers) and others. Each sub-caste has its own hereditary craft tradition, its own endogamous marriage practices, and its own specific jajmani relationships with patron families.

The Kami: Masters of Iron and Fire

The Kami blacksmiths are perhaps the most historically essential of all the Bishowkarma sub-groups — the metalworkers whose skills were foundational to the agricultural, military, and domestic life of Nepal's hill communities. Every implement of hill agriculture — the phalo (plowshare), the kodalo (hoe), the hasiya (sickle), the khurpa (small digging tool) — was made by the Kami. Every weapon of the hill military tradition — the khukuri, the sword, the spear point, the arrowhead — was forged in the Kami smithy. Every domestic iron utensil — cooking pots, knives, needles, nails — was a Kami product. Without the Kami smith, Nepali hill civilization as it developed over the past two thousand years would have been physically impossible.

The Kami smithing tradition is extraordinarily sophisticated — a mastery of iron metallurgy developed over generations that rivals the technical achievements of any blacksmithing tradition in the world. Traditional Kami smiths work without precise thermometers, standardized steel grades, or metallurgical testing equipment — they judge the temperature of metal by its color, the carbon content of steel by its spark pattern when struck, and the quality of a forging by its sound and feel. The khukuri blade — the famous inward-curved Nepali knife — is a particular achievement of Kami metallurgy, requiring complex heat treatment processes to achieve the combination of edge hardness and spine toughness that makes the blade simultaneously a precision cutting tool and a battlefield weapon.

The Sunar: Goldsmiths of Sacred Adornment

The Sunar goldsmiths and silversmiths are the artisans who create the elaborate jewelry systems that are central to Brahmin, Kshetri, Newar, and many other Nepali communities' material culture. Every piece of wedding jewelry — the potey beads, the tilhari pendant, the Nau-gedi necklace, the Dhungri ear ornaments, the Bulaki nose ring, the gold bangles — that marks a married woman's status in hill-Hindu culture is the product of Sunar goldsmithing skill. The Sunar tradition encompasses not just jewelry production but also the creation of ritual objects — the kalash (ceremonial water vessel), the diya (ritual lamp), the puja thali (worship platter), and other brass, copper, and gold items used in Hindu worship.

Caste Discrimination and the Dalit Rights Movement

The historical classification of Bishowkarma communities as Dalit — untouchable — and the practice of caste-based discrimination against them has been the defining political reality of Bishowkarma social experience. Nepal's Discrimination and Untouchability Act of 2011 formally criminalized caste-based discrimination, following the 2007 Interim Constitution's explicit inclusion of Dalit rights provisions. However, the gap between formal legal rights and social reality remains significant — caste-based discrimination in access to drinking water, public temples, social interaction, and marriage continues in many parts of rural Nepal despite its formal illegality. The Dalit rights movement — in which Bishowkarma communities alongside other Dalit groups are prominent — is one of Nepal's most important ongoing human rights struggles.

Interesting Fact: The khukuri blade forged by Kami blacksmiths has attracted the attention of materials scientists who have analyzed the traditional Nepali steel used in high-quality khukuris. Traditional Kami steel — made from specific local iron ores smelted and processed using wood charcoal, with specific tempering and hardening processes — has been found to have a carbon gradient structure (harder at the edge, tougher at the spine) that is extraordinarily difficult to achieve intentionally in a modern workshop.


Mystery: The theological claim of the Bishowkarma community — that they are the earthly manifestations of the divine craftsman Vishwakarma — is embedded in specific ritual practices. Each Bishowkarma craft begins with a prayer to Vishwakarma; each tool is treated as potentially sacred; the workshop itself has shrine-like qualities. Whether this theological framework represents the original meaning of the tradition or was later added as a response to Dalit social marginalization — a claim to divine dignity in the face of social degradation — remains an unresolved question.

Section Two

Language

Nepali and the Bishowkarma Oral Tradition

The Bishowkarma communities speak Nepali as their primary language — their centuries of residence in the Nepal hills alongside Brahmin, Kshetri, and other communities has produced full integration into the Nepali linguistic environment. Like other Nepali-speaking communities, the Bishowkarma carry the full richness of Nepali oral literary tradition — proverbs, folk songs, narrative poetry, and ritual chant. However, the Bishowkarma communities also carry a specifically craft-related oral tradition: the technical language of their specific trades, the songs sung while working, and the ritual invocations made to Vishwakarma during craft processes.

A Kami blacksmith teaching his son the craft — the intergenerational transmission of technical knowledge and the oral tradition of the smithy

The Craft Language: Technical Oral Knowledge

Each Bishowkarma craft specialization has its own technical oral vocabulary — hundreds of specialized terms for specific tools, materials, processes, measurements, and quality characteristics that exist only within the craft tradition and have no written documentation. The Kami blacksmith's vocabulary includes distinct terms for different temperatures of heated metal (identified by color), different grades of iron and steel (identified by spark pattern and hammer response), different forging techniques (different hammer strokes and anvil positions), and different qualities of finished products (judged by sound when tapped, flexibility when bent, and sharpness when tested). This technical vocabulary is entirely oral — transmitted from master to apprentice in the workshop — and represents accumulated technical intelligence of immeasurable practical value.

Interesting Fact: The Bishowkarma Damai sub-caste is Nepal's traditional tailor-musician community — the hereditary players of the Panche Baja (Five Sacred Instruments) ensemble that accompanies all major hill-Hindu life-cycle ceremonies. The Damai are thus both artisans (tailors who sew the Daura Suruwal national dress) and musicians (whose music marks all the most emotionally significant moments of Brahmin and Kshetri life). This dual role makes the Damai perhaps the most structurally central artisan community in hill-Hindu civilization — present at every birth, marriage, and funeral — yet paradoxically placed at the bottom of the caste hierarchy they musically celebrate.


Mystery: The Sahanai (the double-reed wind instrument of the Panche Baja) requires the circular breathing technique — breathing in through the nose while blowing out through the mouth to maintain continuous sound. This technique is found in musical traditions across a vast geographic area from Egypt to the Balkans to Central Asia to Nepal. Whether circular breathing was invented once and spread, or was invented independently multiple times, remains unknown — and the specific pathway by which it reached Nepal and became associated with the Damai musical tradition remains undocumented.

Music and Dance

The Panche Baja: The Bishowkarma Gift to Hill-Hindu Ceremony

The most profound and culturally significant Bishowkarma contribution to Nepali music is the Panche Baja — the Five Sacred Instruments ensemble performed by Damai musicians at all major life-cycle ceremonies of the Brahmin and Kshetri hill communities. The five instruments — Dholak (barrel drum), Damaha (large kettledrum), Tyamko (small kettledrum), Sahanai (double-reed oboe), and Karnal (ceremonial trumpet) — together create the sonic landscape of the most important moments of hill-Hindu social life: weddings, sacred thread ceremonies, house-warming rituals, and funerals.

A Damai Panche Baja ensemble performing at a wedding — the five instruments visible, the musicians in traditional dress, the ceremonial sound filling the hill village

Panche Baja Performance: The Sacred Schedule

Each ceremony has its own specific Panche Baja repertoire — rhythmic patterns (taals), melodic tunes played on the sahanai, and specific sequences of karnal calls that have specific conventional meanings. The patterns for a wedding procession (janti) are different from those for a sacred thread ceremony (bratabandha), which are different from those for a house-warming ritual (griha pravesh), and different again for a funeral (antyesti). A highly trained Damai musician knows dozens of ceremonial compositions, each associated with a specific moment in a specific ceremony, and can play the correct composition for each moment without being told.

The training of a Damai musician traditionally began in childhood — boys learned to play simple percussion parts, then more complex rhythmic patterns, then melodic lines on the sahanai, then the full ceremonial repertoire — under the teaching of their fathers or senior community musicians. This apprenticeship system produced musicians of extraordinary technical competence and ceremonial knowledge, who could perform complex music for hours in demanding outdoor conditions without written scores, amplification, or reference materials.

Vishwakarma Puja: The Festival of Artisans

The most specifically Bishowkarma festival is Vishwakarma Puja (विश्वकर्मा पूजा) — the annual celebration of the divine artisan Vishwakarma, observed on the last day of the Nepali month of Bhadra (the day the sun enters Virgo). On this day, all tools, machines, and implements of craft are cleaned, garlanded with flowers, and worshipped as sacred objects embodying the divine creative energy of Vishwakarma. Smiths, carpenters, goldsmiths, mechanics, and artisans of all kinds cease work for the day and conduct elaborate puja ceremonies in their workshops.

Interesting Fact: Modern Nepal's automotive, industrial, and construction sectors have adopted Vishwakarma Puja enthusiastically — factories, garages, and construction sites across Nepal decorate their machinery with marigold garlands and conduct puja on this day, regardless of the religious backgrounds of the workers. This secular adoption of a specifically Bishowkarma Dalit festival by mainstream industrial Nepal is a remarkable example of cultural transmission in which a stigmatized community's specific religious observance became a broadly valued national tradition.


Mystery: The Panche Baja's extraordinary longevity — versions of these five specific instruments in combination appear in stone carvings from Nepal dating to the 7th century CE or earlier — raises the question of why this particular combination was settled on and has remained stable while individual instruments in other musical traditions have changed dramatically. The stability may reflect a cosmological significance — the five elements, the five directions — or simply the pragmatic acoustic fact that this specific combination works exceptionally well for outdoor ceremonial music in the hill terrain.

Section Four

Festivals and Culture

Dashain and Tihar: Craft at the Center of Celebration

The Bishowkarma community participates in Dashain and Tihar as part of the broader hill-Hindu community — but with specific roles determined by their jajmani relationships with patron families. The Damai musicians' most intense period of work coincides with Dashain — the festival season when the maximum number of ceremonies requiring Panche Baja music are performed. The Kami blacksmiths' khukuri expertise is specifically invoked at the Ayudha Puja (Weapon Worship) on Navami, when the tools and weapons of every household — including the khukuris crafted by Kami smiths — are cleaned, garlanded, and worshipped.

Bishowkarma artisans conducting Vishwakarma Puja — tools and implements arranged before the deity, flowers and incense on the workshop shrine

The Jajmani System: Reciprocal Obligation and Social Integration

The jajmani system — the hereditary service relationship between artisan and patron families — is the social institution that defines Bishowkarma community life and their integration into the broader hill-Hindu social fabric. Each Bishowkarma family is bound by hereditary obligation to provide its specific services (metalwork, music, tailoring) to a defined set of patron (jajman) households, in return for regular payment in grain, cloth, money, and cooked food at festivals. This relationship is inheritable — the children of a Kami smith inherit both the skill and the client relationships of their father, as do the children of the patron Brahmin or Kshetri family.

The jajmani system created a social interdependence in which Bishowkarma artisans and their Brahmin-Kshetri patrons were bound to each other across generations. The Kami smith needed the grain payment of his jajman families; the Brahmin family needed the Kami's metalworking and the Damai's music. This mutual dependence is why the caste hierarchy, despite its injustice, was relatively stable for centuries — both sides had material interests in maintaining the relationship. The decline of the jajmani system in the 20th century, as market economies replaced subsistence agriculture and artisans could sell their products commercially, is one of the major social transformations of modern Nepali society.

Interesting Fact: The Kami blacksmith's traditional forge (dhungro or dhaunsilo) uses a double-piston bellows design — two alternating air chambers pumped by hand to maintain a continuous airflow — that is virtually identical to traditional forge designs found across West Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and parts of Central Asia. This remarkable convergence of design may reflect a common ancient ancestor technology that spread across the Old World with the spread of iron-smelting knowledge, or may represent independent inventions of the optimal design for a hand-pumped forge.


Mystery: Traditional Kami blacksmiths report that specific prayers and invocations to Vishwakarma, performed at specific stages of the forging process, are necessary for the khukuri to be 'alive' — to have the quality of aliveness (jiva) that distinguishes a blade of exceptional quality from a technically competent but spiritually inert product. Experienced Kami smiths claim they can feel the difference between a 'living' and 'dead' blade through their hands during forging — a phenomenological claim that has never been rigorously tested.

Traditional Dress

The Working Dress of the Artisan

Bishowkarma traditional dress reflects the practical demands of their crafts alongside the broader hill-Nepali aesthetic tradition. Kami blacksmiths traditionally work in a minimum of clothing — the heat of the forge and the physical demands of metalworking make heavy clothing impractical, and specific protections (aprons, head coverings) were used rather than full formal dress. In everyday and social contexts, Bishowkarma men wear the Daura Suruwal and Dhaka Topi — the same national dress as Brahmin and Kshetri men — demonstrating their full participation in the broader hill-Nepali cultural tradition.

Damai Musicians' Ceremonial Dress

The Damai musicians who perform the Panche Baja at ceremonies wear a specific ceremonial dress: typically the Daura Suruwal in white or cream, with a distinctive Dhaka topi, and sometimes specific ornamental additions appropriate to the ceremonial context. In the old jajmani system, patron Kshetri and Brahmin families were obligated to provide their Damai musicians with cloth for festival clothing — a responsibility that the Damai, as hereditary tailors, would then fashion into their own ceremonial garments. This provision of cloth was a significant part of the jajmani payment and represented the patron family's acknowledgment of the Damai's indispensable ceremonial role.

Interesting Fact: The Sunar goldsmith's traditional tools — small hammers, drawing plates, files, and soldering equipment — are largely unchanged from examples found in archaeological sites across South Asia dating to the 2nd millennium BCE. The goldsmithing techniques used by Nepali Sunar artisans today — wire drawing, granulation, filigree work, repoussé (hammering from behind to create raised designs), and stone setting — are all attested in ancient jewelry from the Indus Valley Civilization and early historical South Asian sites. This technological conservatism is not backwardness but mastery: these techniques, refined over 4,000 years, produce results that modern industrial jewelry manufacturing cannot replicate.


Mystery: The Bishowkarma community's classification as Dalit (untouchable) despite their work being considered the earthly manifestation of the divine Vishwakarma is one of the most contradictory aspects of the Hindu caste system as applied in Nepal. The theological explanation — that the impurity comes from contact with fire (for Kami) or animal materials (for Sarki) regardless of the sacredness of the craft — is logically strained and widely regarded as a post-hoc justification for an economically motivated hierarchy. The historical process by which craftspeople who were once celebrated as divine artisans came to be socially marginalized is a question that historians of South Asian society have not fully resolved.

Unique Foods

Bishowkarma Cuisine: Shared Traditions and Specific Practices

The Bishowkarma community's food culture shares the Dal Bhat foundation of hill-Nepali cuisine — lentil soup and rice as the twice-daily staple, with vegetable tarkari, achaar, and ghee as accompaniments. As non-vegetarian communities, Bishowkarma families consume goat, chicken, buffalo, and pork (pork being particularly associated with Dalit communities in the western hills as an affordable protein source). Sel Roti is made for Dashain and Tihar with the same enthusiasm as in Brahmin and Kshetri households, and the Kwati nine-bean soup is made for Janai Purnima.

A traditional dal bhat thali — the shared staple of hill-Nepali life, central to Bishowkarma family meals and communal feasting

Feast Foods and the Jajmani Economy of Food

A distinctive dimension of Bishowkarma food culture is the jajmani economy of cooked food — the system in which patron Brahmin and Kshetri families provide cooked food (bhoj) to their Bishowkarma artisans as part of the ceremonial payment for services. At every major Brahmin-Kshetri household ceremony — a wedding, sacred thread ceremony, or Dashain feast — the Kami smith, Damai musician, and Sarki cobbler who serve that household receive portions of the ceremonial feast. This food economy meant that the quality of the meal a Bishowkarma family ate at festival time was directly tied to the prosperity of their patron families — a dependence that, like all aspects of the jajmani system, had both economic security and social subordination dimensions.

Raksi and Local Brewed Beverages

The production and consumption of Raksi — the traditional distilled spirit made from fermented grain — is particularly associated with Bishowkarma and other Dalit communities in the western hills, where the social prohibition against alcohol that orthodox Brahmin communities maintain does not apply. Bishowkarma households produce Raksi for home consumption and for sale, using traditional copper stills of simple but effective design — a technology of distillation that the Kami metalworking tradition produces in the form of the still itself, creating a remarkable synthesis of the craft skill and the food tradition within the same community.

Interesting Fact: The Kami blacksmith's forge-side snack tradition — specific foods eaten quickly during work breaks at the forge, chosen for their sustaining quality and resistance to contamination by metalworking materials — includes specific preparations found nowhere else in Nepali food culture. Forge-side foods are typically dry preparations — dried beans, corn (makai), roasted grain, hard cheeses — rather than the liquid-based preparations of the main meal, chosen because they can be eaten with fingers without contaminating the work environment with food residue.


Mystery: The Bishowkarma community's traditional knowledge of medicinal metallic compounds — specific preparations of copper, iron, and other metals used in traditional healing practices related to Ayurvedic medicine — is entirely undocumented in academic literature. Traditional Kami and Sunar healers have claimed knowledge of specific metallic preparations for treating anemia, wound infections, and other conditions, using a system that appears to parallel the Ayurvedic concept of Bhasma (purified metal ash) without any direct connection to the Sanskrit Ayurvedic texts. Whether this represents an independent indigenous metallurgical medicine or a folk transmission of Ayurvedic knowledge through the artisan community is unknown.

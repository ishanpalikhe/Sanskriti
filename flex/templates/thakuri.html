History and Origin

The Name, The Claim to Kshatriya Status, and the Hill Kingdom Heritage

The Thakuri people — known in various contexts as Thakuri, Thakuris, or in some historical texts as Thakuri Rajput — are a primarily Indo-Aryan ethnic community found throughout Nepal, with the highest concentration in the western hills of Nepal (districts of Doti, Bajhang, Bajura, Achham, and surrounding areas). According to Nepal's national census, the Thakuri population numbers approximately 80,000–100,000 individuals. The Thakuri occupy an ambiguous position in Nepali social classification: claiming Kshatriya (warrior) status and connection to broader Indo-Aryan traditions, while also maintaining a distinct ethnic and regional identity rooted in the western Himalayan hills.

The word 'Thakuri' is derived from Sanskrit Thakur meaning 'lord' or 'chief,' and historically referred to a feudal ruler or landlord. The Thakuri claim to be descendants of ancient Kshatriya kingdoms that ruled in the western Himalayan region before the Gorkha expansion, tracing their ancestry to various royal lineages and warrior clans that established principalities in the valleys and ridge lines of western Nepal. This claim to Kshatriya status and royal ancestry distinguishes the Thakuri from both caste-based communities and from ethnic nationalities — they are understood as a historically landowning and ruling class whose identity is rooted in warrior ancestry and political authority.

Image: Ancient fort ruins in Doti district — remnant of the small Thakuri kingdoms that once governed western Nepal's valleys

Ancient Kingdoms and Political Authority

The Thakuri claim to be heirs of ancient kingdoms that ruled the western Himalayan region. Historical records and inscriptions suggest that various princely states and kingdoms existed in the western hills of Nepal — the Baise Rajya and Chaubise Rajya (22 and 24 kingdoms mentioned in Nepali historical chronicles) — before the Gorkha unification. Some of these kingdoms were ruled by families that claimed Rajput or Kshatriya descent, and the Thakuri trace their ancestry to these royal families and warrior clans. The mythology and historical narratives maintained by Thakuri communities emphasize royal ancestry, warrior prowess, and the governance of territories before external conquest.

The Gorkha Conquest and Thakuri Incorporation

When the Gorkha kingdom expanded into the western hills in the late 18th century, the small Thakuri kingdoms fell before the more powerful Gorkha military force. The conquest meant the end of Thakuri political independence and the incorporation of Thakuri territories into the expanding Gorkha-dominated state. However, the Gorkha rulers — themselves claiming Kshatriya status — incorporated some Thakuri families into the new hierarchical structures, recognizing them as 'minor aristocrats' or 'feudal landlords' in the new system. Some Thakuri families were appointed as subba (district administrators) or other officials, allowing them to maintain some authority and economic privilege despite the loss of political independence.

Land Ownership and Feudal Relations

The defining characteristic of Thakuri communities after the Gorkha conquest has been land ownership. The Thakuri positioned themselves as landowners and feudal lords who controlled agricultural land and the peasants who worked that land. The feudal relationship between Thakuri landlords and peasant tenants became the organizing principle of economic and social life in the western hills. Thakuri wealth was measured in land holdings and in the labor obligations that peasants owed them. This feudal system — though formally abolished after Nepal's 1950 revolution — still exerts influence on social relations in many parts of western Nepal.

Modern Thakuri: From Feudal Lords to Citizens

In independent Nepal, Thakuri communities have experienced the loss of feudal authority and the need to adapt to new political and economic systems. The abolition of the feudal system, land reform legislation, and the expansion of democratic governance have all diminished the traditional authority of Thakuri landlords. Some Thakuri families have adapted by engaging in new occupations — government service, education, business, military service — and by shifting from land-based wealth to other forms of capital. Contemporary Thakuri communities span an enormous range: from subsistence farmers and landless laborers to government officials, business owners, and professionals.

Interesting Fact: The Thakuri royal claim is not merely a historical footnote — the Shah dynasty that ruled Nepal as kings from the 18th century until the abolition of the monarchy in 2008 was itself of Thakuri origin, tracing their lineage to the same Rajput-claiming warrior tradition of western Nepal. The former royal family of Nepal and the Thakuri community of western Nepal thus share a foundational historical identity, giving the Thakuri an indirect connection to the entire narrative of unified Nepal's founding.


Mystery: The historical documentation of the Baise Rajya (22 kingdoms) of western Nepal is frustratingly incomplete. Most of what we know comes from later chronicles, genealogical claims, and oral traditions — all of which are naturally shaped by the interests of those who recorded them. Whether the Thakuri families who claim descent from these kingdoms are genuinely the heirs of ancient Rajput royal lines, or whether the royal genealogies were constructed or elaborated in later centuries to justify feudal land claims, is a historical question that existing evidence cannot definitively resolve.

Language

Thakuri as a Nepali Dialect Community

The Thakuri speak Nepali — the official national language of Nepal — rather than a distinct ethnic language. The Thakuri are Indo-Aryan language speakers, and their integration into broader Nepali linguistic culture is part of their claim to be part of Indo-Aryan rather than Tibeto-Burman ethnic traditions. However, Thakuri speech carries distinct regional dialect features specific to the western hills where Thakuri communities are concentrated — features of pronunciation, vocabulary, and grammatical construction that mark it as western hill Nepali.

The Thakuri use of Nepali reflects their historical integration into the Gorkha state and their position as part of the Indo-Aryan cultural sphere. Unlike communities like the Rai or Gurung that have their own distinct languages and have been working to develop literacy in those languages, the Thakuri have been fully integrated into Nepali linguistic culture for centuries. This linguistic integration is both a marker of cultural assimilation and a reflection of Thakuri claims to be part of the broader Nepali — particularly Kshetri-Brahmin — cultural tradition.

Image: A Thakuri community gathering in western Nepal — Nepali is the shared language of ceremony and everyday life

Sanskrit Influence and Kshatriya Literacy

As a community claiming Kshatriya status, Thakuri families with strong Hindu religious practice historically had access to Sanskrit-medium education through temple schools and association with Brahmin priests. The Thakuri patron-Brahmin relationship — through which Thakuri landlords employed Brahmin purohits (family priests) to conduct ceremonies — meant that Sanskrit ritual language was a constant presence in Thakuri household life. While Thakuri were not themselves expected to be Sanskrit scholars (that was the Brahmin's role), familiarity with Sanskrit ritual vocabulary distinguished educated Thakuri from peasant classes.

Interesting Fact: Some western Nepali Thakuri communities preserve archaic vocabulary and grammatical forms in their Nepali speech that linguists identify as older features of the language — forms that have been replaced in the standard Nepali spoken in Kathmandu and the central hills. This linguistic conservatism may reflect the relative isolation of western Himalayan communities from the centers of standardization, preserving linguistic heritage that has been lost elsewhere.


Mystery: Whether Thakuri communities once possessed a distinct community language before their absorption into the Nepali-speaking hill culture — a language that may have been related to Khas, the ancient Indo-Aryan language of western Nepal — is a question that linguistics has not resolved. Some older Thakuri elders report family words and ritual phrases not found in standard Nepali, which may represent remnants of an earlier linguistic stratum now largely submerged under modern Nepali.

Music and Dance

Deuda and Regional Musical Traditions

The Thakuri, as part of the broader Kshetri-dominated hill culture of western Nepal, participate in the Deuda singing and dancing tradition — the characteristic musical form of the far-western hills. Deuda is a circle dance-song form in which groups of men and women hold hands and dance in circular formations, singing alternating verses of traditional songs. The songs carry themes of love, longing, seasonal celebration, and social commentary. Deuda performances continue through the night at festivals and seasonal celebrations, creating marathon communal celebrations that reinforce social bonds within and between communities.

While not exclusively Thakuri, the Deuda is deeply important in Thakuri cultural expression. Thakuri families are active participants in Deuda celebrations, and skilled Thakuri Deuda singers — those who can improvise verses with wit and poetic quality — earn community recognition and prestige. Thakuri participation in Deuda reflects the cultural integration of Thakuri and Kshetri communities and their shared engagement in the hill-Hindu cultural sphere.

Image: A Deuda circle dance performance in western Nepal — the communal song-dance tradition that binds hill communities together

Panche Baja and Ceremonial Music

Like other Hindu communities of the hills, Thakuri engage with the Panche Baja (five sacred instruments) tradition for ceremonial occasions. The Panche Baja ensemble — composed of the Dhol, Damaha, Tyamko, Jhyali, and Karnal — accompanies Thakuri weddings, religious ceremonies, and festivals. The ensemble is played by Pariyar (Damai) musicians, reflecting the traditional division of ceremonial labor between Kshatriya patron communities and the artisan-musician castes that served them. Thakuri weddings are among the most elaborate in western Nepal — processional music from the Panche Baja ensemble announcing the arrival of the wedding party is a defining acoustic image of western Nepali ceremonial life.

Contemporary Musical Expression

Contemporary Thakuri musicians engage with modern music forms, blending traditional instruments and songs with contemporary musical styles. Thakuri musicians participate in broader Nepali music scenes and increasingly in global music markets, creating fusion forms that appeal to younger generations. The folk-pop genre that blends traditional Nepali melody and lyric themes with modern production has produced Thakuri-origin musicians who have achieved national and regional recognition, bringing western Nepali musical traditions to broader audiences while adapting them for contemporary ears.

Interesting Fact: The Deuda songs of western Nepal have served as a historical archive — verses composed to commemorate specific events, feuds, alliances, or political changes have been transmitted across generations, preserving social memory in melodic form. Some Deuda verses that scholars have recorded from elderly singers in western Nepal appear to reference events and political configurations from the 18th-century Baise Rajya period — making them living documents of pre-unification western Nepal.


Mystery: The Deuda tradition allows for improvised verse-making within a fixed melodic framework — skilled singers improvise poetic responses to each other in real time during performances. Whether this improvisational tradition preserves an ancient form of oral poetry composition that predates written literary culture, or whether it is a more recent social development, is debated among scholars of Nepali folk literature. The cognitive demands of maintaining melodic structure while improvising complex verse suggest an extraordinary tradition of oral training.

Festivals and Culture

Dashain and Tihar: Central Hindu Festivals

The Thakuri, as a Hindu community claiming Kshatriya status, observe the major Hindu festivals of Dashain (celebrating the goddess Durga's victory over evil) and Tihar (the festival of lights) with particular ceremonial elaborateness. These festivals are celebrated with ritual observances, animal sacrifice (in Dashain), special foods, and family gatherings. For Thakuri families, Dashain traditionally required the employment of a Brahmin priest to perform Sanskrit invocations, reflecting the hereditary relationship between Thakuri patrons and their family purohits. The observation of these festivals with full Brahminic ritual connects Thakuri to broader Hindu traditions and asserts their claimed position within the Hindu caste hierarchy as Kshatriyas.

Image: A Thakuri family receiving tika during Dashain — the sacred red marking on the forehead given by elders to younger family members

Gaura and Regional Traditions of the Far West

The Thakuri, as part of the western hills cultural sphere, observe Gaura — a festival particularly distinctive to the far-western Himalayan region. Gaura is a women's festival observed in the Shrawan-Bhadra months (July–August), celebrating the marriage of Gaura (Parvati) and Shiva. Women create clay images of Gaura and Maheswor (Shiva), carry them in procession, sing specific devotional songs associated with the festival, and perform ritual observances across several days. The Gaura festival is one of the most distinctive cultural expressions of the far-western hill tradition and represents the regional specificity of Thakuri Hindu practice within the broader Nepali Hindu calendar.

Weddings and Life-Cycle Ceremonies

Thakuri communities observe Hindu ritual practices for life-cycle events including Bratabandha (sacred thread ceremony for young men), weddings, and death rituals. Thakuri weddings are elaborate, multi-day affairs involving the Panche Baja procession, Brahmin-conducted Sanskrit rituals, and community feasting. These ceremonies are performed by Brahmin priests and follow Sanskrit ritual protocols, reflecting Thakuri claims to Hindu/Kshatriya religious legitimacy. The Bratabandha ceremony — which initiates young Thakuri men into the sacred thread tradition — parallels the Brahmin Bratabandha but carries Kshatriya-specific elements that assert the warrior spiritual identity of the community.

Interesting Fact: The Gaura festival of western Nepal is recognized as one of the most distinctive regional women's festivals in all of South Asia. Unlike most Hindu festivals that center on male ritual specialists, the Gaura festival places women at the center of both the religious observance and the creative activity — women make the clay images, lead the processions, sing the songs, and conduct the essential rituals. This female-centered ceremonial tradition has survived centuries of Brahminic orthodoxy that typically subordinates women in ritual contexts.


Mystery: The Gaura festival songs contain verses whose precise meaning is no longer fully understood even by the women who sing them — archaic vocabulary, unusual grammatical forms, and references to customs, places, and figures that cannot be identified in current knowledge. Whether these verses preserve the memory of pre-Hindu religious practices from the western hills, or whether they are simply archaic forms of Nepali or an older Indo-Aryan language that has since evolved, is a question that has fascinated folklorists for decades without a definitive answer.

Section Five

Traditional Dress

Daura Suruwal and Kshatriya Dress

The Thakuri wear the Daura Suruwal — the traditional Nepali dress that serves as the national dress of Nepal and that is particularly associated with Kshetri and other hill Hindu communities claiming Kshatriya status. The Daura (the long-sleeved collarless jacket fastened with ties) and Suruwal (the distinctive trousers voluminous above and tight at the ankle) are worn on formal occasions, religious festivals, and for cultural expression. The Dhaka Topi (Nepali cap made from traditional Dhaka cloth) completes the traditional ensemble.

For Thakuri men, the wearing of Daura Suruwal carries particular symbolic weight as a marker of Hindu high-caste identity. The garment connects Thakuri to the ruling-class aesthetic of the Shah dynasty and to the broader Nepali national identity that the Shah kings constructed around Kshetri-Brahmin cultural values. In formal settings — government occasions, religious ceremonies, community events — Thakuri men of older generations are among the most consistent wearers of the Daura Suruwal in all of western Nepal.

Image: Thakuri men in formal Daura Suruwal and Dhaka Topi at a community ceremony in western Nepal

Women's Traditional Dress and Jewelry

Thakuri women wear the sari — the same garment worn by other Hindu women across South Asia — for formal and ceremonial occasions. On formal occasions, some Thakuri women may wear the Gunyo Cholo (the traditional dress more commonly associated with Kshetri women of the hills) — the wrapped skirt and short blouse that represents the traditional hill-Hindu women's ensemble. The specific colors chosen and the jewelry worn reflect Thakuri participation in broader Nepali Hindu dress traditions while maintaining regionally specific preferences for particular colors and ornament styles associated with western Nepali hill culture.

Jewelry and Status Markers

Thakuri women wear jewelry including bangles, necklaces, nose ornaments, and finger rings that reflect both personal economic status and family wealth. The Pote necklace (red and green glass beads given by the husband at marriage) and Sindur (vermillion at the hair parting) mark married status in the same way as other hill-Hindu communities. Among historically wealthy Thakuri landowning families, women wore elaborate gold jewelry — the quantity and quality of gold jewelry worn reflecting the family's land holdings and social position. The tradition of elaborate gold jewelry for Thakuri women reflects their family's historical position as the landowning class of the western hills.

Interesting Fact: The Dhaka Topi — the Nepali cap that is perhaps the single most recognizable symbol of Nepali national identity — became strongly associated with the hill-Hindu male identity through the Shah dynasty's use of it as a symbol of Nepali nationalism. Thakuri men's consistent wearing of the Dhaka Topi represents a statement of participation in this national cultural identity, linking Thakuri's Kshatriya self-understanding to the broader project of Nepali national culture.


Mystery: The western Nepali tradition of specific embroidery patterns on women's Gunyo Cholo — patterns that vary by village and region in ways that local women can read as identity markers — has been inadequately documented before modern standardization of dress erased many of these regional variations. Whether these embroidery traditions encoded genealogical or geographic information in visual form, similar to what has been documented in Gurung textile traditions, remains a question that ethnographers have begun asking only after many of the traditions themselves have been lost.

Unique Foods

Dal Bhat and Hill-Hindu Food Culture

The Thakuri, as a Hindu community of the western hills, center their daily diet around Dal Bhat — the lentil soup and rice combination that is the foundational meal of hill-Hindu Nepal. The Thakuri meal extends this base with a range of vegetable preparations (tarkari), pickles (achaar), and meat dishes for non-vegetarian occasions. As a community claiming Kshatriya status — the second-highest Hindu varna — Thakuri households traditionally practiced varying degrees of vegetarianism depending on family religious observance and ritual status, with stricter households avoiding meat on auspicious days and more permissive households consuming meat regularly.

Image: A traditional Thakuri household meal — dal bhat with seasonal vegetables, achaar, and festival sweets laid out on a banana leaf

Agriculture and Seasonal Foods

The Thakuri, as historically landowning communities, engaged in agriculture as their primary economic activity. The staple crops of the western Himalayan hills — rice (in lower elevations), wheat, millet, and various beans and vegetables — form the basis of Thakuri cuisine. The seasonal availability of different crops creates seasonal variations in Thakuri diet, with fresh vegetables in harvest seasons and preserved foods (dried vegetables, pickles) during winter months. Gundruk (fermented dried leafy greens) and Sinki (fermented radish) are important preserved foods that carry nutrition through Nepal's winter months when fresh vegetables are unavailable in higher-altitude communities.

Festival Foods and Ritual Meals

Thakuri prepare special festival foods for Dashain, Tihar, Gaura, and other occasions. Dashain requires the preparation of meat dishes — particularly goat curry (khasi ko masu) — which is the centerpiece of the festival feast. The quality and quantity of meat served at Dashain reflects family prosperity and social standing. Sel Roti — the deep-fried ring-shaped rice bread prepared at Tihar — is equally central to festival identity. During Tihar, Thakuri households prepare elaborate sweet and savory offerings for the Lakshmi puja (worship of the goddess of wealth), with the expectation that properly conducted ritual and generous festival food preparation will bring prosperity in the coming year.

Sweets and Confections

Thakuri make traditional sweets including Barfi (a fudge-like milk-and-sugar preparation), Laddoos (spherical sweets made from flour, ghee, and jaggery), and various milk-based desserts for festivals and special occasions. Kheer — rice pudding made with full-fat milk, sugar, and cardamom — is prepared for auspicious occasions and offered as prasad (food blessed by a deity) during religious ceremonies. The preparation and distribution of sweets at festivals is an important social ritual that marks celebrations and reinforces community bonds. Historically, the abundance of sweets a Thakuri family could offer to guests and dependents was a direct statement of their agricultural prosperity and social position as the landowning class.

Interesting Fact: The western Nepali tradition of fermenting grain-based beverages — particularly Jand (mildly fermented rice or millet beer) and Raksi (distilled spirit) — is deeply embedded in Thakuri social and ceremonial life. Unlike upper-caste Hindu orthodoxy that frowned on alcohol consumption, western Nepali hill culture including Thakuri communities has maintained a tradition of home-brewed and home-distilled beverages as central to hospitality, ceremony, and social gathering. Offering a guest Jand is a traditional marker of Thakuri western hill hospitality.


Mystery: The Thakuri tradition of specific food offerings at particular moments in life-cycle ceremonies — a precise food at a specific ritual juncture — appears to encode theological beliefs about the relationship between nourishment, auspiciousness, and cosmic order that no longer have a clear articulated rationale in contemporary practice. Whether these food-ritual connections represent the survival of a pre-Hindu indigenous ritual logic that was absorbed into the broader Hindu ceremonial structure, or whether they were always understood as part of Brahminic ritual prescription, remains an unresolved question in the study of western Nepali ceremonial life.

History and Origin

The Name, The Rivers, and Their Ancient Livelihood

The Majhi people — whose name itself derives from the Nepali word meaning "fisherman" or "boatman" — are an indigenous ethnic group native to the riverbanks of Nepal's inner Terai regions. According to the 2021 National Census, the Majhi number approximately 111,352, constituting about 0.38% of Nepal's total population [citation:1][citation:8]. Unlike the valley-dwelling Newar civilization, the Majhi identity is inseparable from the great river systems of Nepal: the Saptakoshi (Sunkoshi, Tamakoshi, Dudhkoshi, Arun, Barun, Likhu, and Tamur), as well as the Gandaki basin [citation:4]. These rivers are not merely geographical features but the spiritual and economic heart of Majhi existence — providing fish for sustenance, water for transport, and the stage upon which their unique culture has been performed for centuries [citation:1].

The origins of the Majhi people are shrouded in the confluence of folklore and migration history. Many older Majhi from Sindhuli and Ramechap districts narrate stories linking their descent to figures from Hindu epics such as the Ramayana, suggesting a deep but syncretic relationship with the surrounding Hindu civilization [citation:1]. Sindhuli district has the highest population of Majhi community, with 10,873 people [citation:2]. Linguistically, the Majhi language is classified within the Indo-European family (specifically Indo-Aryan), setting them apart from the Tibeto-Burman origins of groups like the Newar or Kirat. This linguistic affiliation, combined with their riverine occupation, suggests a history of gradual migration and settlement along Nepal's waterways, where they established themselves as the indispensable ferrymen and fishers of the pre-modern trade routes.

The Dohe: The Emblematic Wooden Boat

The most iconic artifact of Majhi material culture is the Dohe (or Dohi) — a traditional wooden boat carved from a single tree trunk, designed specifically for the turbulent currents of Himalayan rivers. For generations, the Majhi community has played a pivotal role in facilitating transportation and trade, ferrying people, goods, and livestock across rivers where no bridges existed [citation:1]. The construction of a Dohe is a sacred craft, passed down through patrilineal lines, involving the selection of specific woods (often Sal or Simal) and the performance of propitiatory rituals to the river deity before the boat's first launch. Today, with the construction of modern bridges across Nepal's rivers, the traditional boating occupation is in steep decline, representing a profound economic and cultural crisis for the Majhi way of life [citation:4].

Interesting Fact: The Majhi people's skill in reading river currents is legendary. They possess an extensive oral vocabulary for different types of rapids, eddies, and hidden rocks — knowledge that has been accumulated over centuries of navigating Nepal's treacherous white-water rivers. This hydrographic knowledge is so precise that it has been studied by modern river guides as a form of traditional ecological knowledge [citation:4].


Mystery: The exact process by which the Majhi came to be exclusively associated with the Saptakoshi river system remains incompletely understood. Some scholars suggest they were originally a forest-dwelling people who adopted boatmanship due to geographic necessity, while others argue they migrated from the Indian plains and specialized in river transport as a niche economic strategy. The lack of written records before the 19th century leaves this question open [citation:1].

Language

Majhi Kuro: The Endangered Voice of the River

The Majhi language — called Majhi Kuro or Majhi Bhasa by its speakers — is an Indo-Aryan language spoken primarily in the central and eastern riverine corridors of Nepal, including Kavrepalanchowk, Ramechhap, Dolakha, Okhaldhunga, and Khotang districts [citation:4]. According to the 2011 census, there were a total of 24,000 who considered Majhi as their mother tongue, a dramatic decline from the ethnic population, indicating rapid language shift towards Nepali [citation:1][citation:7]. There are some regional dialects of the Majhi language with differences in each district and village. For example, the Nepali word "khanu" (to eat) is pronounced as "kha" or "khai" in Tamakoshi but "khara" in Dudhkoshi [citation:7].

Efforts at Preservation and the Threat of Extinction

Linguists have warned that Majhi Kuro is a threatened language, with very few children acquiring it as a first language. The encroachment of Nepali — used in schools, markets, and government offices — has led to a situation where even in majority-Majhi villages, the language is often limited to domestic and ritual use. The Majhi people have expressed a strong desire for education in their mother tongue, but no systematic mother-tongue instruction has been implemented [citation:4]. The recently compiled Majhi Dictionary (Webonary) represents a landmark effort to provide a written standard for the language, but without active intergenerational transmission, its long-term survival remains uncertain [citation:7].

Script of the langugae of the Majhi people.

Interesting Fact: The Majhi language contains a remarkably rich vocabulary related to watercraft, fishing, and river geomorphology — including over 30 distinct terms for different types of fishing nets and traps, each designed for a specific fish species and river condition. This specialized lexicon is among the most detailed of any indigenous language in South Asia for aquatic technology.


Mystery: Despite being surrounded by Tibeto-Burman speaking groups (Sherpa, Tamang, Rai) and living in close proximity for centuries, the Majhi language retains a purely Indo-Aryan grammatical structure with almost no Tibeto-Burman influence. The historical sociolinguistic mechanisms that maintained this linguistic boundary despite intense trade and intermarriage with surrounding groups remain poorly understood [citation:7].

Music and Dance

The Rhythms of the River: Songs of the Current

Majhi musical culture is inseparable from the riverine environment that defines their existence. Their distinct folklore, songs, and dances often celebrate the beauty and power of the rivers [citation:1]. Their folk songs are traditionally sung during communal work, festivals, and religious ceremonies, often accompanied by the rhythmic beating of a large drum. Anthropologist Corneille Jest recorded Majhi ancestor songs in 1969 that are still recognizable in contemporary performances, indicating a remarkable continuity in oral tradition. The Majhi have a tradition of "calling songs" — vocal melodies sung to summon fish to the surface before casting nets — that use specific frequencies and rhythms that older Majhi fishermen claim actually affect fish behavior [citation:10].

Dance among the Majhi is typically performed in a circle with movements that mimic the casting of nets, the pulling of oars, and the swaying of boats on water. The dances are performed during major festivals such as Maghe Sankranti and Baisakh Purnima, as well as during Sansari Puja (a nature worship ceremony). Men and women dance separately or in alternating lines, with drumming patterns varying by occasion — faster and more complex for celebrations, slower and more deliberate for ritual contexts. They worship their ancestors singing songs in their mother tongue invoking their ancestors and dance [citation:10].

The Majhi people enjoying dancing to the unqiue yet melodious tunes of their ancestral songs and musics from their old musical instruments.

Interesting Fact: The Majhi have a unique tradition of "calling songs" — vocal melodies sung to summon fish to the surface before casting nets. These songs, passed down through generations, use specific frequencies and rhythms that older Majhi fishermen claim actually affect fish behavior. While no scientific study has confirmed this, the persistence of the practice across centuries suggests an empirical folk knowledge of fish acoustics.


Mystery: The Majhi do not have professional musicians or hereditary castes of instrumentalists — every adult is expected to be able to sing and drum. This egalitarian musical structure contrasts sharply with the complex guild-based systems of the Newar or the court-musician traditions of the Khas. How this musical democracy was maintained without formal institutions remains an intriguing question in ethnomusicology [citation:1].

Section Four

Festivals and Culture

The Majhi Ritual Calendar: Nature Worship and Ancestral Homage

The Majhi festival calendar reflects their deep integration with the natural world and their syncretic position within Nepali Hinduism. The most important festivals include: Baisakh Purnima (marking the start of the fishing season), Sansari Puja (worship of the creator god Sansari, performed to ensure community well-being), Barden and Bhumi Puja (land-worship ceremonies), Jhakani Puja (appeasement of river spirits), and the Hindu pan-Nepali festivals of Dashain, Tihar, and Maghe Sankranti [citation:1].

Sansari Puja: The Creator God of the Majhi

The most distinctive Majhi religious observance is Sansari Puja, a worship ceremony dedicated to Sansari, the creator deity who is believed to have fashioned the rivers, forests, and fish for the benefit of the Majhi people. Unlike mainstream Hindu pujas that employ Brahmin priests, Sansari Puja is conducted by community elders without Brahmin mediation. Offerings include rice, wine (jand), flowers, and the sacrifice of a rooster or pigeon, depending on the wealth of the family. The puja is typically performed at a riverside shrine — often a simple stone platform under a sacred fig or pipal tree — and includes the recitation of ancestral invocations in Majhi Kuro [citation:1].

The Deities of the Majhi

The main god of the Majhi people is Gharbhimsen. Other principal gods are Goresiddhi, Gaidu, Mahadev, Jaurekhee, Setidevi, Ardev, Jangali and Shikari. Totala flowers are vitally important in the worship of these deities [citation:4]. The Majhi are pantheist and give worship to creatures like Sime, Bhume, Bhut-Pret, Chhauda, Kancho Bayu, Pisach etc. They believe in the soul, hell and heaven. After the death of a person, they believe that the soul of their dead relatives and ancestors visit their house for many purposes, so Majhi should pray and offer them pure rice, wine, flowers, and fragrant wood regularly [citation:4].

The Majhi people performing the Sansari Puja, a unique religious ceremony dedicated to their creator deity for the benefit of the community and well-being of the nature.

Interesting Fact: The Majhi practice a form of "river-based divination" — before any major journey or fishing expedition, a priest (or elder) will float a leaf or flower petal on the water and observe its movement to determine the favor of the river deity. Different drift patterns indicate different outcomes: rapid downstream movement is auspicious, while circling or sinking is a warning to postpone [citation:4].


Mystery: Majhi religious practice includes a belief in Gharbhimsen as the principal deity — a figure not found in standard Hindu or Buddhist pantheons. Some scholars suggest Gharbhimsen may be a localized form of a river yaksha (nature spirit) who was later assimilated into the Hindu framework. The origins of this deity remain entirely undocumented in textual sources [citation:4].

Traditional Dress

Practical Attire for a Riverine Life

Majhi traditional dress is notably simpler and more functional than the elaborate costumes of the Newar or Tharu, reflecting their status as a marginalized, laboring community. On average, Majhi people are dark brown in skin complexion, medium in height and well built, with classic Mongoloid features typical of East Asia [citation:4]. Majhi men traditionally wear a dhoti (loincloth) or short lungi, often tucked up above the knees to allow free movement in water, paired with a simple vest or bhoto (waistcoat). A cotton cloth (gamcha) is worn on the head or around the neck, used both for wiping sweat and as an impromptu bag for carrying fish. Women wear a cotton sari — typically in solid, dark colors (navy blue, maroon, or black) with minimal borders — draped in a style that allows wading into water while maintaining modesty. The blouse is typically short-sleeved, and women wear substantial amounts of glass and silver jewelry, particularly around the neck and wrists [citation:4].

Unlike the Tharu, who have distinctive white-and-green costumes, or the Newar, with their iconic Haku Patasi, Majhi dress is less codified and more variable by region and individual preference. However, Majhi elders express concern that younger generations are abandoning even these modest traditional garments in favor of cheap, mass-produced Western clothing (t-shirts and jeans), representing another dimension of cultural erosion.

The Majhi people in their traditional attire being ready for their musical performances.

Interesting Fact: Majhi fishermen use a specific type of waterproof hat woven from bamboo and large leaves, designed to shed rain and provide shade during long hours on the water. These hats are often decorated with a single feather from a kingfisher — a bird considered sacred and auspicious for fishing.


Mystery: Some older Majhi women wear a distinctive nose ring (bulaki) made of silver in a shape that resembles a fishhook. Whether this is purely decorative or carries a deeper symbolic meaning related to the community's fishing identity is a matter of speculation, as no oral tradition explicitly explains the origin of the design [citation:4].

Section Six

Unique Foods

The Bounty of the Saptakoshi

Majhi cuisine is, unsurprisingly, dominated by fish and rice — the two products of the river and the terrace fields that sustain their communities. The Majhi are master fishermen, using a variety of traditional techniques including cast nets (jali), basket traps (dhadi), and hand lines to catch species such as asala (a river carp), bam (catfish), and sidhre (a small silvery fish). Fish is typically prepared as a spicy curry (machha ko jhol) with turmeric, ginger, garlic, cumin, and green chilies, simmered for hours until the bones soften. The curry is eaten with boiled rice or dhido (a thick porridge made from millet or corn flour), the latter being a staple of poorer households [citation:1].

Jand: The Fermented Rice Beer

No Majhi feast is complete without jand (also called chhyang or jaand), a fermented rice beer that is central to both everyday sociability and ritual offerings. Jand is made by cooking rice, cooling it, mixing it with marcha (a fermented starter cake containing wild yeasts and herbs), and allowing it to ferment in an earthen pot for several days [citation:4]. The resulting beverage is mildly alcoholic, slightly sour, and cloudy white. Jand is offered to deities during Sansari Puja and other ceremonies, served to guests as a sign of hospitality, and consumed communally during festivals. The preparation of marcha is often a specialized knowledge held by older women in the community. The women especially sell "Marcha" — an ayurvedic medicine for making wine and jand — in the markets, as well as mats made of pater [citation:4].

The fermented rice beer called Jand consumed by the Majhi people during the midst of festivals.

Interesting Fact: The Majhi practice a unique form of fish preservation called suka machha — small fish are gutted, salted, and dried in the sun on riverbank rocks, then stored for months. Dried fish can be rehydrated in curry or fried as a crispy snack. This technique allowed the Majhi to survive the monsoon season when fishing was dangerous or impossible.


Mystery: The Majhi use a specific combination of wild river herbs in their fish curry — including leaves from a plant they call thulo taro (identified by botanists as a type of aquatic pennywort) — that is not used by other Nepali communities. The medicinal or flavor properties of this herb have not been scientifically analyzed, and its use appears to be unique to the Majhi culinary tradition.
